Not Even Foresight Can t Prevent This Horrific Baldur s Gate 3 Decision

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1300 words 6 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

Tragedies in Baldur’s Gate 3 can’t always be avoided, even with prior knowledge. Although she seems suspicious, Yenna isn’t always hiding something, and players should wait to learn more. Orin’s target for kidnapping can be predicted and manipulated to save important companions.

Literature Review

On a second run of Baldur’s Gate 3, players may assume they’re experts, which may lead to a regrettable decision. Here’s how to avoid that.

There are some disasters players just can’t avoid in Baldur’s Gate 3, even when they know what’s coming. With a variety of complicated decisions, especially in Honour mode, Baldur’s Gate 3 bears replaying. Some players might try a completely different route each time they start a new playthrough, in the interest of seeing what’s different. Others will often stick with one or two particular paths, but even then, lucky or unlucky dice rolls may open up more subtle variations.

But even on a second playthrough, there are some tragedies that can’t be sidestepped. Players can plan ahead as much as they’d like, but some plot triggers are incredibly understated, and it’s easy to miss or misinterpret them. Take those misinterpretations too far, and the party could end up with a real mess on their hands. Here’s how to avoid acting rashly and causing tragedy based on assumptions from a previous playthrough.

              Baldur's Gate 3's Creepiest Boss Is A Mini Version Of An Even More Terrifying Creature   Baldur's Gate 3 doesn't tend to hold back from grisly foes, but one of D&D's most fearsome enemies didn't manage to make the cut in its full form.

Orin Doesn’t Always Kidnap Yenna In BG3 Don’t assume Yenna is hiding something in Baldur’s Gate 3 - sometimes, she’s just the innocent child she seems. The party first meets Yenna in Act Three of Baldur’s Gate 3. She’s likely to be one of the first NPCs they’ll encounter as they enter Rivington, a suburb of Baldur’s Gate. Yenna recognizes the party for a group of adventurers, and asks them to help her find her missing mother. They have multiple options here: donate some money or food, offer to find a city guard, or bluntly tell her that her mother’s probably dead.

If they have speak with animals active, the party can also speak with Yenna’s cat Grub, and his dialogue might raise eyebrows. Grub is anxious and secretive, although he’s protective of Yenna. Regardless of how their interaction proceeds, Yenna and Grub will appear at camp shortly after meeting the party. They’ll mostly just stand there. They can be spoken with again, but their dialogue doesn’t reveal much more about them.

For some players, Yenna and Grub seem like cut content - they’re more prominent than the average NPC, but all their dialogue doesn’t build to anything. For others, however, Yenna becomes a major plot point when she’s kidnapped and impersonated by Orin the Red, a changeling and one of the major antagonists of Act Three. For those who have experienced this part of the story, it may be tempting to attack Yenna outright on first meeting her, or when she first arrives at camp. However, that’s unwise.

              One Roleplay Choice Completely Destroys Orin's Story In Baldur's Gate 3   Depending on how you play Baldur’s Gate 3, you might make certain choices that fit the character you created. And that might ruin Orin’s story.

Yenna doesn’t always get kidnapped by Orin - sometimes it’s an actual companion instead. Saving that companion involves quite a bit of effort: the party will have to discover Orin’s involvement with the Temple of Bhaal, track down their murder tribunal, complete that scene by becoming an Unholy Assassin or killing Sarevok, and either trade a Netherstone for Yenna’s life or pass several Difficulty Class 25 skill checks. Sometimes, these companions don’t make it. Most good-aligned parties won’t want to fork over the Netherstone, and those DC 25 checks aren’t always attainable.

Of course, it’s possible to circumvent this by simply making a deal with Orin, but that’s best reserved for an evil playthrough of Baldur’s Gate 3. So, in order to avoid doing the unthinkable or going through all that work again, the player simply strikes down Yenna sooner rather than later. Doing so doesn’t lock out any story content, but it might draw some companions’ disapproval. It won’t allow them to skip Orin’s quest line, either, as she’ll simply possess someone else. As a result, it’s best just to wait until Orin’s possessed companion attacks Tav.

Orin’s Target Isn’t Random In BG3 How To Tell Who Orin Kidnapped

While there’s a degree of randomness in how Orin’s target is chosen on a first playthrough, it’s not totally up to chance, and can actually be predicted - if not preempted - in a repeat run. Orin always selects from five potential kidnapping options: Yenna, Lae’zel, Gale, Halsin, or Minthara. That’s Yenna’s entire reason for being in the game. If the player hasn’t recruited all the options above, or if all the options they have recruited are in the party when Orin’s story trigger is activated, it’ll be Yenna.

However, there are multiple ways to trigger Orin’s kidnapping. Given the most logical progression through the Act Three map and quests, most are likely to learn that there’s a traitor at camp the first time they meet Enver Gortash prior to his coronation. However, if the party decides to skip Gortash’s ceremony, they can instead learn of the kidnapping by visiting the Emperor’s hideout in the Lower City, where Orin herself will appear to announce the kidnapping.

It’s possible to pick who gets kidnapped by leaving the intended target back at camp when activating either of these triggers. If a player doesn’t want to side with Orin under any circumstances, and is worried about not being able to pass the skill checks required to free them, this can ensure that the lost companion is the one the player cares for the least. It’s not terribly nice to leave any of these companions (or Yenna) at Orin’s mercy, but it’s better than losing a beloved friend.

Many decisions in Baldur’s Gate 3 are shockingly complicated. It can take multiple playthroughs to understand how certain story triggers are, what they take into account, and what they affect. However, an experienced player can make the most use of these variable moments, calculating certain choices to be made at certain moments to affect certain outcomes.

This is the case with Yenna and Orin: with the right planning, a bad situation can be manipulated to avoid the worst possible scenario. However, taking this too far and trying to preempt Orin’s kidnapping can result in disaster. As a result, players should always wait until Orin reveals herself in Baldur’s Gate 3.
