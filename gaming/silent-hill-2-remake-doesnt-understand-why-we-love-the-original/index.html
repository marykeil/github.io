Silent Hill 2 Remake Doesnt Understand Why We Love The Original

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1400 words 7 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

The Silent Hill 2 remake from Bloober Team doesn’t live up to the fear and psychological uncertainty of the original, focusing too much on combat. The decision to prioritize action and appeal to a mass market audience is a misguided approach that risks alienating fans of the series. The remake fails to capture the unique atmosphere and gameplay elements that made the original Silent Hill 2 a beloved classic, and fans are left without a satisfactory way to play the original game.

Literature Review

The Silent Hill 2 remake looks like it might squander the opportunity to bring a classic back to life, and it’s especially frustrating for fans.

The upcoming Silent Hill 2 remake brings a beloved horror classic back to life, but at the current stage, it doesn’t look like the project is going to live up to the original. The Silent Hill series is charging full steam ahead out of a long hiatus, bouncing back from nearly a decade absent of any entries with a substantial batch of new games and experiences. These range from free content like Silent Hill: The Short Message to substantial new entries, and the Silent Hill 2 remake is one of the biggest projects of the bunch.

The original Konami subsidiary that made Silent Hill 2, Team Silent, was disbanded by the company after the first four games in the series. Silent Hill 2’s new incarnation is being developed by Bloober Team, the studio behind horror hits like Layers of Fear and Observer. Silent Hill 2 isn’t the first major franchise project that Bloober Team has taken on, with a 2019 Blair Witch game testing the company’s mettle at survival horror elements and ultimately receiving average reviews that didn’t quite live up to the positive reception generated by the studio’s other horror games.

Silent Hill 2 Remake Is Too Much Of An Action Game

Although Bloober Team is best known for horror that’s low on action and high on atmosphere, the studio is switching up its approach for Silent Hill 2, and it isn’t the right way to go about remaking the classic. A new State of Play trailer for the Silent Hill 2 remake, available on the official PlayStation YouTube account, focuses on the combat in a way that spotlights this issue. The main strength of Silent Hill 2 is the fear and psychological uncertainty that color the experience, and stringing together a reel of third-person shooter clips feels like a complete misfire.

The trailer seems to wear the inspiration of recent Resident Evil remakes on its sleeve, presumably with the intention of following a path that’s already been carved to critical acclaim. It’s not the right fit, however, as Silent Hill 2 always put a bigger emphasis on exploration than Resident Evil’s general tendency to highlight survival action. Although an abundance of fog is appropriately in play, the Silent Hill 2 remake footage doesn’t recapture the strength of the original game’s atmosphere, and the original use of space and shadows feels largely lost in favor of a more generic approach.

The Silent Hill 2 Remake Has Sounded Bad For A While

The general inefficacy of the Silent Hill 2 remake combat trailer doesn’t come as a huge surprise, as the project has been sounding like a misconceived endeavor for some time now. The biggest warning sign came from an Engadget interview where Bloober Team proclaimed a shift away from the studio’s focus on psychological horror toward mass-market appeal. Although getting a grip on solid combat could be useful for Silent Hill 2, the action should by no means be the development priority, so it was a bit of a baffling statement to boldly declare.

What’s even more confusing is the decision to make the State of Play trailer all about the combat in light of the initial reaction to the Engadget article, which generated controversy for the Silent Hill 2 remake and ruffled the feathers of many fans. It comes across as stubbornly forging ahead in a desperate grasp for the broad appeal that Bloober Team seems to think is the core of Silent Hill 2. Whatever its potential may be, the strategy carries the risk of alienating the audience most primed for the series to return.

Modern Gameplay Isn’t Innately Better For Silent Hill 2

The Silent Hill 2 remake feels like the consequence of reshaping classic games to fit modern gameplay norms, and it exemplifies why the approach isn’t always the best one. Adding accessibility features and polishing up basic problems is always helpful, but the term “quality-of-life” often gets wielded as a blunt instrument to hammer out any quirks or distinct choices that made games unique. The inclusion of button-mash quick time events in the gameplay footage is perhaps the most striking example of a misplaced feature, as it simply doesn’t fit the style of Silent Hill 2.

This isn’t the first time that Silent Hill has lost sight of what made it special. Konami’s initial dissolution of Team Silent was done to place more Western developers on the series, a maneuver that didn’t pay off. The turn away from a psychological horror focus is a big part of what stopped later games in the series from garnering the same reception as Team Silent’s original entries, and it’s disappointing that Bloober Team doesn’t seem to be learning from the mistakes of the past and recapturing what established Silent Hill 2 as one of the greats.

The Original Silent Hill 2 Isn’t Available Anymore

The most painful aspect of the Silent Hill 2 remake is that there’s no great way for fans to play an official copy of the original Silent Hill 2 today. Recent years have seen the PlayStation 2 release skyrocket in value, and the Silent Hill HD Collection for PlayStation 3 and Xbox 360 has enough problems to make it a significantly inferior version. This is only one of the many areas where Konami’s stewardship of the franchise has proven disappointing, but it makes the Silent Hill 2 remake’s mistaken priorities sting all the more.

A fan-made PC mode called Silent Hill 2: Enhanced Edition is one of the best ways to play the game today, but playing it legally would still require owning a rare PC copy of the game.

Although the Silent Hill 2 remake is ultimately just one symptom of a larger sphere of problems in the gaming industry, it’s an example that brings them into sharp relief. For Silent Hill fans, it’s also uniquely painful, as seeing something that’s maintained a strong legacy for over two decades get reduced to a more generic form is especially unpleasant. If it kept a focus on what made the original game great, the Silent Hill 2 remake could be something special, but all signs point to the sad truth that it’s failing at this guiding principle.
