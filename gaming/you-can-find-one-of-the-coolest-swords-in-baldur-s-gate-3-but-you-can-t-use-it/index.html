You Can Find One Of The Coolest Swords In Baldur s Gate 3, But You Can t Use It

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1300 words 6 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

Amassing an interesting library of weapons is exciting in Baldur’s Gate 3, but the highly coveted Steel Watch swords cannot be used. The Watcher Greatsword offers significant damage output but is too heavy to wield, making it more of a pretty piece of loot. The Watcher weapons, including the Watcher Crossbow, are restricted to use by automatons and cannot be acquired or used by the player’s party.

Literature Review

Act 3 of Baldur’s Gate 3 has a sword that could change the game for any party, but it’s not actually possible to put the powerful weapon to use.

Amassing an interesting library of weapons is one of the most exciting parts of progression in Baldur’s Gate 3, but one sword that could be the crown jewel of any collection remains unfortunately off-limits. For martial classes, weapons with unique attributes help add flavor to combat, especially for those who lack any access to spells that can often make higher-level play especially fun. The amount of freedom that Baldur’s Gate 3 offers generally provides avenues to acquire and use anything that seems like it could be in reach, but sometimes it’s the rare exclusions that sting the most.

It doesn’t take long to start requisitioning some memorable weapons in Baldur’s Gate 3, as Act 1 provides some decent lower-level options through a variety of avenues like enemy encounters and NPC traders. Acccessing the Adamantine Forge kicks things into a higher gear by offering the ability to forge a weapon out of mithral, the exceptional fantasy metal that’s more durable and effective than steel. Unsurprisingly, however, the very best weapons are generally found in Act 3 after reaching the titular city of Baldur’s Gate, where challenging fights and expensive goods are plentiful.

The Steel Watch Swords In Baldur’s Gate 3 Can’t Be Used

The armies of the absolute provide an outside threat to the city of Baldur’s Gate, but it doesn’t take long to realize that there are also threats from within. Lord Gortash, the chosen of Bane, is in control of the city, and his Steel Watcher automatons prowl the streets. These hulking mechanical behemoths wield appropriately oversized greatswords, and figuring out how to claim one of these blades for the party might be the first thought of many rogues or ne’er-do-wells that pass through the gates.

Unfortunately, however, there’s no actual way to wield the swords of the Steel Watchers in Baldur’s Gate 3. Although the game is full of humor, it does maintain a general tendency towards realism, so it’s not especially surprising that a petite character can’t swing a giant sword around like Cloud in Final Fantasy 7. What feels less fair is the fact that even an oversized party member can’t manage it, turning it into a pretty piece of loot that fails to serve its primary function for anyone who takes it off of a Steel Watcher.

The big advantage of a Watcher Greatsword in Baldur’s Gate 3 is its significant damage output, which ranges from 9-42 instead of the standard 2-12 of a basic greatsword. Most of this is slashing damage, with exactly 2 points being dealt as acid. There’s a heavy price to pay in terms of potential encumbrance, as the sword weighs 100 kilograms, so it does make sense that a sword that can take up most of a character’s carry weight on its own would be difficult to swing around in a fight.

It can be worth it to upgrade strength in Baldur’s Gate 3 for any sort of custom character, as the encumbrance limit and reliance on jumps makes it a highly relevant stat.

How Wielding A Watcher Greatsword In BG3 Should Work

The most obvious choice to wield a Watcher Greatsword among the party members in Baldur’s Gate 3 would be Karlach, as a tiefling barbarian with a muscular frame and an infernal engine for a heart would certainly be a prime candidate for feats of strength. A more conservative approach might restrict the act to creatures of Large size, which would exclude the Small or Normal humanoid forms of party members. There are ways to size a party member up to Large, however, and they bear as little fruit as attempting to hand the sword off to a gnome does.

Perhaps the most obvious way to increase a character’s size in Baldur’s Gate 3 is to use the enlarge spell, a second-level Transmutation spell that can be selected by sorcerers and wizards alike. The Elixir of the Colossus is an elixir that can achieve the same effect, and a quarterstaff called Bigby’s Chew Toy does likewise thanks to the Whossa Large Fellow? ability. Any one of these should make it possible to wield the Watcher Greatsword, even if only temporarily, but the advantages of becoming a giant in Baldur’s Gate 3 don’t extend that far.

There Are Other Unusable Weapons In Baldur’s Gate 3

Although Watcher Greatswords might be the most tempting weapon that’s off-limits in Baldur’s Gate 3, they aren’t the only thing that the characters are denied acces to. One example is the Watcher Crossbow, also found at the Steel Watch Foundry, which is likewise restricted to use by the automatons. This delivers 6-33 damage overall, which packs a slightly lesser punch than the Watcher Greatsword but still far outstrips standard crossbows in the game. The Steel Watch Foundry can be used to make a Hellfire Engine Crossbow for Karlach as something of a consolation prize, but it doesn’t deal comparable damage.

Foes with especially powerful weapons crop up at various points throughout Baldur’s Gate 3, and the game restricts the acquisition of these tools as a general rule. Exploits to acquire and use such weapons have been discovered, but Larian Studios has put work into patching them out of the game, so downing an Elixir of Hill Giant Strength won’t suddenly make a terrifying sword work on the player’s behalf anymore. It’s always possible that new workarounds for the Watcher weapons and other options could be found, but it’s exceptionally clear that they’re not intended for use.

As exciting as it would be to use a Watcher Greatsword in Baldur’s Gate 3, it does ultimately make sense that such a devastating weapon needs to be kept out of the party’s hands. Encounters have to be designed with a good degree of flexibility in mind, but swords that roll several d10s for damage would completely break the level of challenge. All the same, it’s disappointing to have a game-changing Baldur’s Gate 3 weapon within the grasp of the party while not being able to make use of it.
