You Might Be Missing Half Of Baldur s Gate 3 s Content And Not Even Know It

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1600 words 8 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

Not speaking with companions can cause players to miss out on quests and important parts of the story in Baldur’s Gate 3. Some players may not realize that they need to initiate conversations with companions outside of combat to unlock their personal quests and learn more about their stories, and not just when an exclamation mark icon appears above them. Ignoring or not starting a companion’s personal quest can lead to them leaving the party permanently, such as in the cases of Shadowheart and Halsin.

Literature Review

Some BG3 players are missing out on tons of content and quests by not speaking to their companions on a regular basis and getting to know them.

Just like many other RPGs that came before it, Baldur’s Gate 3 is built around an adventuring party with many companions for the player to choose from as they set out to save the world. However, surprisingly, there are some who are missing out on quests and chunks of the story due to a misunderstanding of a key game mechanic. This means that players are potentially missing out on almost half of BG3’s content and a large part of what has made the game so successful.

[Warning: The following article contains spoilers for Baldur’s Gate 3.]

Baldur’s Gate 3 is by no means the first game to have an impressive roster of companions who can join the camp and aid the player character with their missions. Games such as Dragon Age, Mass Effect, Skyrim and all past installments in the Baldur’s Gate series all have characters to meet and engage with as the story unfolds. However, some BG3 players seem to be a little confused about how the companions work outside of combat, which is leading to them missing out on large parts of the epic story.

Not Speaking With Companions Will Lead To Missed Quests

It seems the game mechanics in Baldur’s Gate 3 are proving to have a steep learning curve for some, as there are players who are missing out on content by not speaking to their companions at camp. While many games will have quest givers approach the main character or have some visual indication when they have something important to say, that isn’t always the case with BG3’s companions. Although they will sometimes have a white exclamation point above their heads, a lot of the time it is through conversation that the companions’ personal quests are unlocked.

Players Might Not Know They Should Talk With Companions Often

After the tutorial Nautiloid area, the player can recruit most companions throughout Act 1 and pretty soon the party camp area will fill up. Here, companions can be chatted with, letting the player learn more about their personal stories, and as their approval of the player character grows, they will start to share more. However, it appears that not everyone understands that companions won’t always make the first move when it comes to these chats.

Although companions will occasionally trigger cutscenes to further the story, many of these are dependent on approval or certain aspects of their backstories being revealed previously. Additionally, companions will sometimes have a white exclamation point to show that they have something to say, which has led to many misunderstanding its purpose and believing that it is only when these appear that the companions should be spoken to. However, this isn’t the case, meaning that some players are missing new plot points due to never talking to companions outside these two instances.

              Baldur's Gate 3 Companions Are Multifaceted But Lack One Key Trait   The companions in Baldur’s Gate 3 are all great, but there’s one crucial thing none of them have. It may be missed, but there are good reasons why.

The white exclamation point will appear throughout the game to indicate to the player that that particular companion has something to say regarding a specific situation or event. This is the same mechanic developer Larian Studios has used before in the Divinity: Original Sin series, but newcomers to the studio’s games might not realize that this isn’t the primary indicator that the companion wants to talk. This visual marker will appear in situations such as Gale musing on the party’s situation very early in Act 1 or after completing a certain quest for a companion.

The exclamation points will appear throughout the game, both in camp and while exploring the world, and indicate that a companion has something interesting to say about a specific event or topic.

However, this isn’t where companions’ personal quests will start and exist to give context to the character and the world they inhabit. By only speaking to companions when the exclamation point appears, large chunks of their dialogue and backstories will be missed. This problem seems to come from players being so used to characters approaching them with quests that they don’t realize that, in BG3, they are the ones who will have to initiate conversation in order to learn more. The more they are spoken to, the more they will open up and share, leading to the companion’s personal quests.

Companions Will Leave If Their Quests Are Ignored

All companions will leave if their approval of Tav drops low enough. However, there are some who will abandon the party if their personal quests are ignored or never started. Two of the most obvious are Shadowheart and Halsin, who will both leave the party at the end of Act 2 if their personal quests are ignored. However, to gain those quests, both characters have to be spoken with and befriended for them to tell the player about their backgrounds and what they need.

In Shadowheart’s case, she begins BG3 wary of sharing too much about her past and beliefs, but as she is engaged with, she will slowly open up. After that, she will share about her faith in Shar and speak at length about her desire to become a dark justiciar. So it would make complete sense to those who have spoken with her to take Shadowheart through the Gauntlet of Shar in Act 2, especially to the end area to find the Nightsong.

However, if she is never spoken to outside triggered cutscenes, such as her disagreement with Lae’zel, or when she has something to say about a particular event, then Shadowheart remains very guarded regarding her faith. If she is not present when the Nightsong is met, then afterward Shadowheart will be very angry she was not taken on that quest and will leave the party. As this was the first part of the personal quest, it is completely understandable; however, if Shadowheart has never been spoken to, this will catch some players off guard.

Speaking with companions will raise their approval, which will help crucial persuasion checks much easier.

Halsin’s personal quest is to lift the Shadow Curse, after which he will join the party as a full companion. However, in order to know what Halsin wants and why he is traveling with the party to the Shadow-Cursed Lands he has to be spoken to. No exclamation points will appear to Halsin prior to him becoming a full companion, as the only way to learn about Thaniel and how to lift the curse is to go and chat to the druid.

Just as with Shadowheart, Halsin will leave the party permanently if his personal quest is not completed. But to learn about Thaniel, and who he is to Halsin, a conversation must be initiated by the player. Then, to further the quest, and rescue Thaniel from the Shadowfell, Halsin must be sought out again on the player’s own initiative.

While it might make sense after playing other games to wait until prompted to speak with characters, this is not the case in BG3. Getting into the habit of chatting to companions will open up so many more quests and interactions in BG3 which can change the game in unexpected ways. With so many companions and different endings for each of them, it would take plenty of playthroughs to experience them all, which is a huge part of the appeal of RPGs like Baldur’s Gate 3.
