10 Best Dreamcast Games Of All Time

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1400 words 7 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

The Sega Dreamcast had an excellent library of games that still hold up today. The Dreamcast featured versatile and unique games that shaped the future of major franchises. Despite its relative market failure, the Dreamcast serves as a respectable swan song for Sega in the console wars.

Literature Review

The Sega Dreamcast was home to a lot of uniquely great games, and all of its finest titles are as compelling now as they were upon release.

For a system that’s often been painted as the console that killed Sega’s hardware market, the Sega Dreamcast certainly managed to rustle up an excellent library of games. There was never really anything wrong with the experience of actually playing a Dreamcast — and there was a lot that was genuinely great about it — but decisions surrounding its price and release and the unstoppable hype for the PlayStation 2 doomed it to comparative failure. Although it wasn’t a graceful way for Sega to bow out of the console wars, the Dreamcast makes for a respectable swan song. Perhaps the best part of the Dreamcast library is its extraordinary versatility, with many of the console’s best games featuring unique styles and ideas. Some of these titles would shape the futures of major franchises, while others ended up inspiring more spiritual successors than direct sequels. Regardless, all of the greatest games on the Sega Dreamcast brought something exceptional to the table, and all of them are still just as much fun to play today.

Hi-Fi Rush Is Like A Greatest Hits Album Of Dreamcast Classics
Thanks to its bold colors, unique design, and interesting gameplay mechanics, Hi-Fi Rush harkens back to some of Dreamcast’s most popular games.

10 Grandia 2 (2000) Game Arts, RPG

PlayStation 2 titles tend to dominate conversations about early 2000s RPGs, but the Dreamcast housed several exceptional entries in the genre. Grandia 2 is one major standout of the catalog, with fun combat and cute designs that give the world and characters an extra dose of charm. It ended up receiving a PlayStation 2 release as well, but severe compromises in the port make the Dreamcast version the superior option to play.

9 Samba De Amigo (2000) Sonic Team, Rhythm

It might seem unusual now to see a game from Sonic Team that doesn’t star the blue hedgehog, but the studio used to be a lot more experimental. Samba de Amigo is one unmitigated success that came out of this genre diversity, featuring simple rhythm gameplay that relies on maraca peripherals to get the adrenaline pumping. It’s simple, stylish, and, above all else, fun, and it’s easy to paint it as the necessary forerunner to other popular games like Guitar Hero and Rock Band.

“A Proper Dopamine Rush” - Samba De Amigo: Party Central Review
Samba de Amigo: Party Central doesn’t fully capitalize on its potential, but it still manages to be a ton of fun by getting the basics right.

8 Resident Evil – Code: Veronica (2000) Capcom, Survival Horror

Resident Evil – Code: Veronica might not have the honor of being a numbered game in the iconic survival horror series, but the level of quality that it delivers doesn’t feel like a spin-off. It represented a technical step up from the first two games at the time of its release, and its atmosphere and especially soundtrack are beyond reproach. The notable level of challenge also sets Code: Veronica apart from most other Resident Evil titles, which could be a draw or a turn-off, depending on the player.

7 Phantasy Star Online (2000) Sonic Team, Online RPG

Sonic Team managed yet another innovative Dreamcast title with Phantasy Star Online, which has the distinct and influential honor of being the first console online RPG. This helped serve as a major calling card for Dreamcast’s online capabilities, and its limitations as a single-player title don’t take away from how special of an achievement that was. The class builds and combat features a level of depth that can take time to truly appreciate, and although the story can feel like it takes a backseat, it’s atmospheric and rewarding for those who pay attention.

6 Sonic Adventure (1998) Sonic Team, Platformer

Sonic’s 3D transition isn’t as effortlessly graceful as the best 2D titles in the series, but Sonic Adventure more than makes up for it in imagination. Unique levels, charming interactions, and fun reinventions all keep the ball rolling, setting a high standard for 3D gameplay that the Sonic franchise has struggled to live up to since. Sonic Adventure 2 is another fun outing on the Dreamcast, but the richer atmosphere and more open possibilities of the original are hard to beat.

5 Soulcalibur (1999) Project Soul, Fighting

The transition to 3D was an exciting time for a lot of fighting game fans, as adding depth to arenas provided a thrill that the loss of 2D precision didn’t tend to overwhelm. Perhaps no game made as strong of a case as Soulcalibur, however, which doubled down on the innovations of its predecessor Soul Edge in a game that shined both on arcade cabinets and the Dreamcast. From the movement tech of the 8-way run system to the sophisticated implementation of weapons-based fighting styles, Soulcalibur carved out a niche that no other fighting game occupied.

4 Skies Of Arcadia (2000) Overworks, RPG

Skies of Arcadia might take the cake for the best RPG on the Dreamcast, and it has less to do with any one standout element and more because it features so few shortcomings. A fun skyfaring concept, memorable story and characters, and irrepressibly optimistic sense of adventure make Skies of Arcadia hard not to love. There’s a reason copies are in high demand today, although the aftermarket prices are certainly frustrating for anyone trying to pick up an original copy.

3 Crazy Taxi (2000) Sega, Racing

Crazy Taxi’s name sells the game. It’s a frenetic, inventive take on taxi driving, lifting the high-octane standard set by earlier arcade racers from Sega and throwing an extra dose of chaos into the mix. Crazy Taxi isn’t complex, and that’s sort of the point, delivering the perfect way to release pent-up stress or get lost in a pure gameplay experience that knows it’s fun and banks on it.

2 Shenmue (1999) Sega AM2, Action-Adventure

Grand Theft Auto 3 rightfully gets a lot of credit for revolutionizing the concept of open-world gaming, but Shenmue is another major part of that evolution. The immersive detail of its city setting and the ability to take gameplay at the player’s own pace made it stand out, and even the more awkward parts of the gameplay don’t stop it from being compelling today. The modern popularity of the Yakuza/Like a Dragon series shares some DNA with Shenmue, and it’s a shame that the truly excellent Dreamcast franchise was never able to evolve across the years in the same way.

Shenmue III Review: Where Did The Time Go?
After eighteen years, Shenmue III has finally arrived, and, for better or worse, it is exactly what fans of the series have been asking for.

1 Jet Set Radio (2000) Smilebit, Action

Jet Set Radio is all about the style, with vivid cel shading, slick tricks, and an absolutely killer soundtrack from Hideki Naganuma coming together to make for an especially memorable experience. The gameplay combines inline skating and graffiti tagging to emphasize a rhythmic sense of mastery, and it’s no surprise that it’s inspired games like Bomb Rush Cyberfunk to try to recapture the same magic. Jet Set Radio is unlike anything that came before it, and its legacy as a masterful Dreamcast game still dares to be defied today.
