10 Outrageous Far Side Comics Featuring Anthropomorphized Animals

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1600 words 8 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

The Far Side stands out from other comics by featuring anthropomorphized animals in unexpected and ridiculous situations. The comic strips use familiar animals in mundane settings to add humor and showcase Gary Larson’s outrageous style. The strips cleverly twist everyday scenarios, like a slug running through a house with salt or a porcupine receiving a balloon as a gift, to make them truly ridiculous.

Literature Review

From dogs on a date to porcupines opening presents on Christmas, these Far Side comics anthropomorphize animals in the most hilarious ways possible.

From bears to cows to insects, The Far Side frequently featured animals in situations that were wholly unexpected. Showing animals in human-like, anthropomorphized scenarios was something The Far Side did best. Likewise, The Far Side strips that had anthropomorphized animals in kooky circumstances set the comic apart from others like Peanuts or Beetle Bailey, which had more straightforward, down-to-Earth humor and storylines. Placing animals that everyone knows and loves in unremarkable settings, like on a date or opening presents on Christmas morning, adds a level of ridiculousness to the strip. They also provide cartoonist Gary Larson with an opportunity to demonstrate his trademark outrageous humor. Here are the best examples of The Far Side making use of anthropomorphized animals.

10 Best Far Side Comics Where Dogs Act Like Humans Canines embrace the absurd and topsy-turvy world of the Far Side in these hilarious, clever comic strips that feature dogs acting like people.

10 “A Phonecaw” Published in 1987

Gary Larson usually embraced absurdist and surrealist humor. However, he was no stranger to the use of puns either. This strip is the perfect blend of both kinds of Larson humor. The strip portrays two anthropomorphic birds in their living room when one of them takes a phonecall. Except in this case, because they’re crows, they are getting a “phonecaw.” Considering the punchline consists of just two words, Larson does a fantastic job of setting the scene. The mundanity of the crows’ interaction makes the reader feel like these two birds are in their normal dwellings rather than the kooky scenario it truly is.

9 “Don’t Run Through the House” Published in 1986

Salt is the enemy of slugs and snails. It dehydrates them, effectively killing them. Consequently, slugs have to be extremely careful around the substance. However, the young slug in the strip forgets this important rule and runs through the house with the salt shaker, much to the frustration of his mother. Using the trope of running through the house with something dangerous like scissors, Gary Larson adapts it to fit the unique domestic situation he has created with slugs. With the simple twist, Larson takes a scenario that every mother and child has experienced a thousand times, and makes it truly ridiculous.

8 “Don’t Encourage Him” Published in 1981

Male peacocks are known for the brightly colored plumage that they use to attract mates. Larson uses this animal fact for his early ’80s strip that puts a wacky spin on an everyday situation seen in the human dating world. The strip sees a flock of birds at a bar with a peacock drawing the attention of a plain white bird, to the annoyance of her drinking buddy. Thinking that the peacock is showing off for attention, the bird chastising Sylvia is not a fan of the bright hues of the feathers of their fellow bar mate. Much like a human unimpressed with a man’s attempt to win over her friend, Sylvia’s companion does not want her to reward this peacock’s transparent pickup attempt.

7 “I Was Hibernating” Published in 1987

It is well understood that bears need to hibernate in order to get through the winter. However, it seems that bears in the world of The Far Side have forgotten about this simple fact of animal life. Unfortunately for the bear in the strip, his peers are too quick to give him a funeral after mistakenly thinking he’s dead when in reality he’s only sleeping. While it’s certainly not pleasant by any means to wake up and find one’s self in a casket, the bear is lucky that he came to before being buried. Given that hibernation is a regular occurrence, hopefully these bears will check a pulse next time before making the same mistake.

6 “This Shouldn’t Last Long” Published in 1984

Opening presents on Christmas morning is a valued tradition of many families around the globe. As seen in this strip, porcupines evidently take part in their own yuletide festivities as well, and they are not all that different from their human counterparts. And just like humans, it appears that animals don’t always put the most thought into the gifts that they give each other. A porcupine family is opening their gifts when their child excitedly unwraps a box containing a balloon. This is probably the worst gift to ever give a sharp-quilled animal, and the parent’s prediction that the present won’t last long is likely an accurate one.

5 “Smells Like A House” Published in 1987

No one wants their home to have a bad smell when they have company over to visit. If a human entering someone else’s house were to say that it smelled like a barn, that would be highly embarrassing for the host. In The Far Side, where the surreal is real, the inverse is true for the cow guests of their cow host. In this strip, a pair of cows arrive at a friend’s barn, with one commenting negatively to the other that it “smells like a house.” Hopefully, this rude guest manages to keep their insult from being overheard, otherwise the poor barn owner will be absolutely mortified.

4 “Back To Your Canning” Published in 1993

In the 1970s and ’80s, the public was made more aware of how many dolphins were killed as a result of commercial fishing activities. The exposure of this practice, as well as the implementation of “dolphin-safe” labelling on tuna cans, led to a common belief that tuna cans contained (or at least used to contain) dolphin meat. This has been disputed, but it is the basis for this dark Far Side comic’s dark punchline. A pair of dolphin cops question a woman whose husband has disappeared, all while she is suspiciously canning several cans of tuna. While the dolphin cops may be oblivious, The Far Side readers have a sneaking suspicion that it’s not tuna in those cans. If they had a Dateline for dolphins, this Far Side character would surely make an appearance.

3 “Her Little Notebook” Published in 1983

While the setting may be specific to the gorillas in the strip, the words between them are just like humans. When one obviously angry gorilla questions where her mate was last night, there is a woman in the background jotting everything down. The woman writing in her little notebook is clearly a primatologist in the model of Jane Goodall or Dian Fossey, who lived among chimpanzees and gorillas, respectively, and cataloged their behaviors and interactions in painstaking detail. The gorilla being lectured tries to shush the angry one because they don’t want the exchange being recorded. However, it is a moot point as the gorilla is beyond mad and clearly wants answers.

2 “Worm Away” Published in 1993

Sparky’s date starts off well but, as this strip illustrates, a pothole ruins it all. When a can with the brand name “Worm Away” written on it rolls out of the glovebox, Sparky has a look of panic on his face. Meanwhile, his poodle date just stares at the bottle, which is not a great sign. Acting like humans on a date, the dogs find themselves in an awkward situation when one learns about the other’s embarrassing medical issue. But the use of the name “Worm Away” brings the scene back to the reality that these are still dogs at the end of the day. As a result, the scenario is made all the more off kilter and funny.

1 “It Was Only A Movie” Published in 1980

For the uninitiated to the classic movie The Godfather, there’s a scene in the film where a severed horse’s head is stuck in its owner’s bed as a means of intimidation. The moment is gory and, apparently, a horror to horses everywhere. In this strip, a horse and its companion leave a screening of The Godfather, which has left the horse visibly shaken. It’s hard to really blame the horse given the impact the scene has had on humans alone. The horse scene from The Godfather has been referenced in many movies and TV shows, being parodied in such works as The Simpsons and Despicable Me. But as The Far Side makes clear in this strip, the scene has very different connotations in a world where horses act like humans. The Far Side Summary: Written and drawn by Gary Larson, The Far Side is a comic strip series that ran from December 1979 to January 1995. A worldwide hit, The Far Side explores life’s surreal side and uses a mix of humans and anthropomorphic animals. As of 2020, Gary Larson decided to pick his pencil back up again and has started The Far Side up, circulating the comics on his official website. Writer: Gary Larson Colorist: Gary Larson
