One Baldur s Gate 3 Mechanic Is The Wrong Thing To Do But Feels So Right

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1300 words 6 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

Stealing everything in Baldur’s Gate 3 is highly rewarding, providing access to wealth and rare items, with no downside if done carefully. Players should look for prime targets like shopkeepers and high-level characters to steal from. They can use skills, spells, and items to create distractions and increase the chances of successful stealing and pickpocketing in the game.

Literature Review

Morality comes at a price in Baldur’s Gate 3. Sometimes, doing the wrong thing is what’s best for the whole party. Just don’t get caught red-handed.

One of the worst things players can do in Baldur’s Gate 3 is intensely rewarding. There are many opportunities to do bad in the Forgotten Realms. Whether it’s a full-blown evil playthrough of Baldur’s Gate 3, a single spree of villainy, or just a bit of mischief, those who go looking for trouble are bound to find it.

Of course, with every act of evil comes a balance of moral downsides and practical upsides. After all, only the most diabolical Baldur’s Gate 3 character would do evil without even standing to profit from it. It’s not a universal rule, but usually, the worse a dirty deed feels ethically, the better it pays. And there’s one terrible thing the party can do constantly. It may not be honorable or even excusable at times, but it’s certain to keep them living in luxury throughout their entire journey.

Steal Everything In Baldur’s Gate 3

Players should steal everything that isn’t nailed down in Baldur’s Gate 3: steal from the druids in the Emerald Grove, the duergar in the Underdark, the guards in the Lower City. Steal gold, weapons, armor, keys, books, scrolls, and potions. Steal from chests, closets, and NPCs’ pockets. Steal everything the party’s resident rogue can get their sticky fingers on, and even some things they can’t. If they see it and no one’s looking, consider it free for the taking.

Stealing everything in sight doesn’t just keep the party’s pockets full of gold - in some cases, it can also provide access to rare items. Sure, Baldur’s Gate 3 has a lot of useful items available through more legal means, like quest completion, wild scavenging, and legitimate purchase. But many of these are locked behind certain decisions, difficult to come by, or exorbitantly priced. Stealing is the only reliable way to get everything the party wants, whenever they want it. As long as no one’s the wiser, there’s literally no downside to stealing in Baldur’s Gate 3.

It’d be incredibly time-consuming to check every single chest, crate, and barrel the party comes across for good loot, not even to mention all the NPCs they’ll have access to. However, by taking a few cues, it’s pretty easy to spot a prime target. Shopkeepers are a safe bet. They almost always have something useful in their inventories, and even if it looks too difficult to steal, there’s another way for the party to gain a five-finger discount. Simply take some of the merchant’s gold instead - or the party’s own gold back after buying whatever it is they want.

There are also many things the party can pilfer lining the floors, walls, and shelves of the settlements, towns, and dungeons they explore. It’s important to be on constant lookout for anything that might be worth taking. If it’s locked away, it’s almost certainly worth stealing. A good Sleight of Hand skill helps not only with pickpocketing, but also with picking locks. And if all else fails, the knock spell can reliably open just about any door.

Pressing Left Alt on a keyboard or the Right Stick on a controller will reveal all interactive (and stealable) items in the player character’s vicinity, as well as the sight lines of any nearby NPCs. The items with names and outlines in red may upset NPCs if they catch the party touching them.

High-level characters are also bound to carry interesting loot, especially unique weapons and armor. If the party comes across a character who looks like one of the most difficult bosses in Baldur’s Gate 3, start searching their pockets. If they plan on fighting them, taking their healing items might even make that easier. Naturally, it’s especially difficult to steal things that a character has equipped. They’re likely to notice if the clothes on their back suddenly slip off. But with a high enough Sleight of Hand skill and a few more underhanded strategies, it’s nigh impossible to get caught.

How To Get Away With Stealing In Baldur’s Gate 3

Players can use a couple of tricks to make stealing and pickpocketing easier in Baldur’s Gate 3. Naturally, invisibility skills can help with all manner of stealth-related undertakings, whether it’s from a racial ability, a found item, or the invisibility spell. Items that boost Sleight of Hand, like the Gloves of Power the party finds on one of the goblins attacking the Emerald Grove, can improve the likelihood of pickpocketing success.

Believe it or not, a bard can also be a rogue’s greatest ally. If there’s a roving NPC who keeps interrupting the party’s attempts to steal something, the bard can serve as a distraction. Simply ungroup them from everyone else, have them stand away from any illicit activities, and start playing their favorite song. With a successful Performance check, they’ll have all the locals enraptured, and the party is free to do whatever they want while everyone else watches the show.

It’s also possible to distract some NPCs by having another player character strike up a conversation with them. This doesn’t work on everyone, however. Some NPCs only have one line of dialogue, and as soon as it’s done, the player is booted out of conversation with them, and they may immediately turn around to see another party member trying to pickpocket them. NPCs with multiple dialogue options, however, are ideal for this. The second character can simply stay on the dialogue select screen as long as they like while their companions empty the NPC’s pockets.

And if all else fails, save scumming in Baldur’s Gate 3 makes even the most difficult heists possible. Of course, the likelihood of success affects how practical save scumming actually is. If there’s a 50-50 chance of pickpocketing success, it’ll probably only take one or two tries to steal the item. If there’s a single-digit chance, however, it could take an evening of quicksaves and quickloads to score the loot. Still, it’s best to save whenever a party member is about to steal something. There’s a chance they could get caught, and forced into a fight they can’t handle.

If stealing is wrong, why would anyone choose to be right? It has near-endless benefits for the party’s financial situation, and as long as it’s done carefully, there’s almost no risk. Just don’t let the party get caught dipping into someone else’s pockets in Baldur’s Gate 3.
