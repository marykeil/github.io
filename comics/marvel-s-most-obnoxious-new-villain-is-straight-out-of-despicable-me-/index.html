Summary

Spider-Boy #3 introduces Killionaire, an obnoxious villain with similarities to a character from Despicable Me, adding a lighter tone for younger readers. Killionaire’s similarities to Vector from Despicable Me highlight Spider-Boy’s family-friendly tone and offer a softer introduction to the Marvel Universe for young readers. Spider-Boy #3 is aimed at younger audiences, featuring simple plots like dealing with a bully and caring for a lost pet. Killionaire is a perfect foe for Spider-Boy’s younger audience.

The introduction of Spider-Man’s “long-forgotten” sidekick, Spider-Boy, has shaken up Marvel’s status quo with a slate of all-new villains from this hero’s mysterious past. Killionaire, the latest member of Spider-Boy’s rogues gallery, is perhaps the most emblematic yet of this ongoing series, drawing inspiration from Despicable Me (2010) to create a menace in keeping with Spider-Boy’s lighter tone for younger readers.

In the Spider-Boy #3 story “Nonstop Action Figure” by Dan Slott, Paco Medina, and Erick Arciniega, we finally meet Killionaire, “Earth’s most entitled influencer” and the one responsible for Taskmaster’s heist of the Super Adaptoid’s arm from the previous issue. Making a miniature Super Adaptoid from the arm, Killionaire takes his new “Toy Soldier” on a petty rampage across the city while he livestreams the destruction.

Killionaire’s obnoxious nature is put front-and-center when Spider-Boy fails to stop the mayhem. Not content with merely having defeated the hero, Killionaire also orders his new minion to steal Spider-Boy’s shoes.

Killionaire Is Practically a Double of Despicable Me Antagonist Vector

As an entitled, obnoxious brat, Killionaire bears many similarities to Victor “Vector” Perkins, the primary antagonist of Despicable Me. While ostensibly an up-and-coming young villain, Vector is secretly inept and being bankrolled by his father; likewise, Killionaire is similarly naive and spoiled, expecting to get everything he wants by virtue of his obscene wealth. Not only are their personalities similar, the two share many key visual elements in their bowl haircuts, glasses, and tracksuits.

This comparison helps emphasize Spider-Boy’s overall younger, more family-friendly tone. While Spider-Man’s villains have ramped up in intensity over the years (from the Green Goblin’s maniacal scheming to Carnage’s theatrical rampage through New York), Spider-Boy’s much more upbeat foes range from “Humanimals” to the outrageous Emilo Helio, the Balloon Man. With the incredibly popular Spider-Man currently catering to older audiences, Spider-Boy gives younger readers a softer jumping-on point to the Marvel Universe.

Killionaire Is a Younger Marvel Villain for Younger Marvel Readers

Spider-Boy #3 is fairly transparent about being aimed at younger audiences: the book largely deals with simple plots like confronting a rich bully showing off their expensive new toy, and later on, how to handle a scared, lost pet. Given this, it makes sense that the series would take inspiration from a highly popular animated franchise. Killionaire’s evolution from Despicable Me’s Vector makes him a perfect foe for Spider-Man’s lost partner — just pray that he doesn’t start adding Minions to the Marvel Universe.

Spider-Boy #3 is available now from Marvel Comics.
