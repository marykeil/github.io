10 Funniest Howard the Duck Moments in Marvel History

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1500 words 7 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

Howard the Duck hilariously encounters a Commissioner Gordon pastiche and exposes his hypocrisy and bluster. The story of a vampire cow named Bessie sets the tone for Howard the Duck’s cynical take on heroism. Howard’s dark sense of humor shines as he sabotages a children’s TV program and parodies Spider-Man’s origin.

Literature Review

There is no other Marvel character quite like Howard the Duck, and here are the 10 funniest moments in his history.

Howard the Duck first waddled into the Marvel Universe in 1973, and brought with him a unique sensibility that has yet to be duplicated in American comics. The creation of Steve Gerber and Val Mayerik, Howard the Duck’s earliest adventures were a mixture of heavy social commentary and surreal, laugh out loud moments. Here are 10 of the funniest moments from the Gerber era of Howard the Duck.

10 Howard the Duck “Meets’ Commissioner Gordon Appearing in: Giant Size Man-Thing #5 by Steve Gerber and Frank Brunner

Howard learns a brutal lesson about Earth in this story: no good deed goes unpunished. After helping stop Garko the Man-Frog, Howard finds himself in jail, where he comes face to face with an overzealous, straight-laced Commissioner Gordon pastiche, named “Commissioner Gordonski.” When Gordonski finds out that Howard is actually a duck, he immediately folds and sets Howard free. Howard the Duck was never short on lampooning comic book tropes or characters, and here Gerber takes the wind out of Commissioner Gordon’s sails, comically amplifying his worst traits and showing it is all bluster.

9 Howard the Duck: Vampire Hunter Appearing in: Giant Size Man-Thing #5 by Steve Gerber and Frank Brunner

Shortly after being released from prison, Howard finds himself on the trail of a vampire killing farmers. He is shocked to learn that the vampire in question is actually a cow named Bessie. One of Howard’s earliest adventures, the story, titled “Hellcow,” set the tone for everything that was to come. First, the very notion of a vampire cow is ridiculous, but Gerber and Brunner manage to sell it well. Second, the story looks at the ideal of heroism and what makes a hero and Gerber’s conclusions about are highly cynical, a tone that would run through the book.

“A Never-Ending Loop of Reboots”: Howard the Duck Calls Out the MCU Machine In his 50th anniversary special, Howard the Duck has some choice words for a group that wants to acquire all IP and beam their stories to the galaxy.

8 Howard the Duck Fights Turnip-Man Appearing in: Howard the Duck #2 by Steve Gerber and Frank Brunner

Howard the Duck’s “rogues gallery” is full of some of the weirdest—but also funniest, characters in the Marvel Universe, and Turnip-Man is one of them. Arthur Winslow was a lowly night watchman with dreams of being a published author when he found a sentient space turnip. Seducing Arthur with dreams of power, the turnip bonded with him, creating the powerful Turnip-Man. At the time, there was no other villain like Turnip-Man. A generation of readers had grown up reading conventional villains like Doctor Doom, and Turnip-Man broke the mold, and continued Howard the Duck’s mission of sending up superhero comics.

7 Howard the Duck Sabotages a Children’s Television Program First Appearing in: Howard the Duck #5 by Steve Gerber and Gene Colan

A key part of Howard the Duck’s appeal was its dark humor, and it gets no darker than sabotaging a children’s television program. Howard fumbles his way onto the set of Gonzo the Clown, and when the clown hits him with a cream pie, the duck unleashes chaos on the set, attacking the clown and drawing laughs and cheers from the kids. Children’s shows featuring clowns were still a thing in the mid-1970s, and Gerber and Colan had a good laugh at their expense in this story. The result is one of the funniest stories in Howard the Duck’s career.

6 Howard the Duck Parodies Spider-Man’s Origin First Appearing in: Howard the Duck #5 by Steve Gerber and Gene Colan

As noted earlier, Howard the Duck never shied away from poking fun at the company’s characters, and in this story Gerber and Colan parody Spider-Man’s iconic origin. Desperate for money, Howard enters a wrestling contest and wins, but the promoter refuses to pay him on a technicality. Marvel fans will recognize this as a riff on Spider-Man’s origin as presented in Amazing Fantasy #15. This time, however, there is no robber to stop and thus no grand moral lessons stemming from it. Instead it becomes a rumination on the unfairness of life in the modern world.

5 Howard the Duck Runs for President First Appearing in: Howard the Duck #8 by Steve Gerber and Gene Colan

Howard the Duck’s solo title debuted in 1976, right as America was gearing up for not only its bicentennial anniversary but also a presidential election. It should have been a time of good feeling for the country, but it was still reeling from Watergate a few years earlier, and Gerber and Colan leaned into this disillusionment, spinning a hilarious story that actually made national headlines. Howard fumbles his way onto a third party ticket and rises quickly in the polls, but is brought down by scandal. Gerber pulls no punches as he brilliantly calls out the American political system.

“You’re Really More Cameo Material”: Marvel Calls Out a Harsh Truth About Howard the Duck Cult hero Howard the Duck celebrates his 50th anniversary and Marvel has used the occasion to call out a harsh truth about him.

4 Howard the Duck Foils a Canadian Super-Patriot’s Plans First Appearing in: Howard the Duck #9 by Steve Gerber and Gene Colan

Howard barely had time to recover from his presidential bid before learning of one of the most bizarre plots in Marvel history: to drop an army of beavers on Niagara Falls. The beavers would re-dam the Falls, causing them to flow backwards. This nutty scheme was the work of Le Beaver, a Canadian superpatriot with a grudge against America. Fans also learn Howard’s presidential run was a trick on Le Beaver’s part. Nationalism of any kind is bad, and Gerber calls it out by taking it to its hilarious extreme.

3 Howard the Duck’s Religious Bus Ride First Appearing in: Howard the Duck #11 by Steve Gerber and Gene Colan

Steve Gerber used Howard the Duck as a vehicle to explore various facets of the human condition, and in issue 11 he brilliantly critiqued organized religion. While on a bus ride to another city, Howard encounters a wide variety of religious adherents, each one claiming the truth. What follows is one of the book’s trippiest sequences, as the adherents become more and more violent the longer the ride goes on. The sight of a Hare Krishna fighting a priest is hilarious, and Gerber uses it here to send up organized religion. The story also introduced supporting cast member Winda Wister.

2 Howard the Duck Parodies Daimon Hellstrom First Appearing in: Howard the Duck #14 by Steve Gerber and Gene Colan

Howard the Duck skewered classic comic icons such as Commissioner Gordon, but the book also had no problem sending up contemporary characters either–in this case Daimon Hellstrom, who was relatively new at the time. Through a series of mishaps, Howard gets Hellstrom’s powers and goes on a rampage, calling out some of the character’s shortcomings. Hellstrom had long sought to purge himself of his demonic dark side, and he was successful. However, a duck is not a suitable vessel for such power, and Hellstrom was forced to take it back. Hellstrom came so close, but Howard denied him peace.

1 Howard the Duck Parodies Star Wars First Appearing in: Howard the Duck #22-23 by Steve Gerber and Val Mayerik

By the time Howard the Duck hit its stride, Star Wars had become a cultural sensation–making it ripe for parody. Gerber reunited with Val Mayerik to give fans a ridiculously over-the-top send-up, with established Marvel characters such as Man-Thing filling in for Han Solo, Chewbacca and the rest. Not content to be a simple parody, the story is also classic Howard the Duck, and the villain is one of the weirdest he ever fought. During its time, Howard the Duck was on-point satire, and never was that more clear than here.
