10 Best Science Fiction Anime Movies

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1800 words 9 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

The best science fiction anime movies have had a lasting impact, influencing new creations and resonating with audiences. Ghost in the Shell, Redline, Cowboy Bebop: The Movie, and Paprika are standout examples of science fiction anime. AKIRA is not only one of the best sci-fi anime movies but also one of the most influential movies of all time, shaping the cyberpunk subgenre and inspiring numerous artists.

Literature Review

The best sci-fi anime movies of all time change the genre and the world of film for the better with their incredible plots and concepts.

The best science fiction anime movies exploded onto the scene when they premiered and remain popular today. Portraying many possible futures and exploring the relationship of humans to each other, technology, and all that’s new and unfamiliar, they tell fans plenty about the state of the present as well as society’s hopes and fears for tomorrow. The best science fiction anime, at the very least, make waves through the world of film and anime when they hit the scene. At their very best, they change those worlds forever, becoming landmarks that influence and inspire new creations. Looking back on them years or even decades later, they continue to resonate with audiences.

10 Ghost In The Shell Remains Exemplary Cyberpunk Based on the manga by Masamune Shirow

In the technologically super-advanced Japan of 2029, Major Motoko Kusanagi and her team take on cyber-crime. Their latest threat is one of the scariest robots in anime: a mysterious rogue AI known as the Puppetmaster for its habit of possessing human brains. In her pursuit of it, Major Kusanagi will be forever changed. Ghost in the Shell flopped at the box office but quickly found its place in the hearts of critics and home video audiences. It went on to not only spawn additional anime, but influence many other famous science fiction films, such as The Matrix and Avatar. Fans remain impressed by Ghost in the Shell’s noir tone, exploration of the self and humans’ relationship to technology, and mix of hand-drawn animation and computer effects. Watch On Tubi

9 Redline Is The Wildest Sport Of The Future Original story by Katsuhito Ishii, Youji Enokido, and Yoshiki Sakurai

Technically, the Redline is completely illegal, with the chosen host planet threatening execution of anyone involved. But the most popular race in the galaxy must go on, and Sweet JP is determined to win the whole thing. To do it, he’ll have to fight through a rust bucket car, the other racers, and the alien mafia trying to sabotage them. Like Ghost in the Shell, Redline was a box office failure that found success on DVD. Though some may find its plot thin, that’s not what anyone is here for. One of the best Studio Madhouse anime is loved for the fluidity, energy, and perfectly balanced chaos of its animation, which reaches a fever pitch in the climactic race. Watch On Tubi

8 Cowboy Bebop: The Movie Showcases The Space Western Based on the original 1998 anime Cowboy Bebop, written by Keiko Nobumoto

The movie was released in Japan with the subtitle “Knockin’ On Heaven’s Door” rather than “The Movie.”

To audiences, the technological advances the solar system has made since the destruction of Earth are stunning, if a little rusty. To the crew of the Bebop, they’re nothing special: scrounging up money for cup ramen is way more important. When a criminal releases a deadly virus on Mars, they rush over hoping to collect the bounty, but wind up in a much bigger brawl than expected. This movie embodies the strongest aspects of Cowboy Bebop. Its well-choreographed action sequences, a soundtrack full of rock, jazz, and blues, and dry humor are all in top form. Taking place between episodes 22 and 23 of the series, it’s right at the point where the Bebop crew are closest and work best together, just before everything comes crashing down. Watch On Tubi

7 Belle Centers Around A Virtual World Of New Identities Original story written by Mamoru Hosoda

In real life, Suzu Naito is a shy singer too traumatized by her mother’s death to pursue her passion. But in the virtual world U, she takes the form of the beautiful Belle and sing again. She is also the only one to try and befriend Dragon, a powerful user in the form of a vicious beast, whose true identity hides a terrifying secret. Belle takes heavy influence from Disney’s 1991 Beauty and the Beast and the original 1740 fairy tale, La Belle et la Bête. However, Mamoru Hosoda takes as much advantage of the updated setting as possible, telling a story of how online personas connect people and reflect their identity, with a virtual world filled with gorgeous imagery. Upon its premiere at the 2021 Cannes Film Festival, Belle received a 14-minute standing ovation. Watch On Max

6 Paprika Delves Into The Darkest Dreams Based on the novel by Yasutaka Tsutsui

Dreams are a strange and mysterious science. Dr. Atsuko Chiba and her team believe they are on the way to figuring some of it out with their new DC Mini device. But when a terrorist steals the device and uses it to wreak havoc, they must face the worst terrors of the psyche to get it back. From Perfect Blue to Paranoia Agent, Satoshi Kon was a master of psychological horror. In this film, he used the limitless environment of dreams to create lurid and exciting imagery to his heart’s content. Coupled with a unique synth soundtrack, Paprika’s visuals excellently portray the concept of the line between reality and the inner workings of the mind being blurrier than one would expect. Watch On Amazon Prime Video

5 Promare Is A Sci-Fi Action Masterpiece Original story written by Kazuki Nakashima

One would expect a futuristic firefighter and one of the best anime characters with fire powers to be natural enemies. And indeed, that’s how Galo of the Burning Rescue and Lio the Burnish begin their relationship. But as they discover the corruption and conspiracies their world is built on, they’ll become closer than ever in burning it all down. Far and away one of Studio Trigger’s best anime, Promare is a rush of dazzling color and dynamic action. As its animation combines the best of 2D and 3D, the story combines the best of fantasy and technology, with the Burnish’s powers, Promepolis’ high-tech tools, and even a few ginormous mecha in there just for fun. Watch On Max

4 The End Of Evangelion Is A Search For The Will To Keep Living Based on the original 1995 anime Neon Genesis Evangelion, written by Hideaki Anno

As Third Impact rages and the world is on the verge of ending, humanity’s fate depends on the choice of one traumatized child. Broken by his experiences as pilot of Eva Unit 01, Shinji Ikari is tempted to just let humanity devolve, ending both pain and individuality. But the world will be saved if he can find the strength to continue living. The final two episodes of the original Neon Genesis Evangelion are famously abstract. They round out the philosophical themes of the show (concluding the Cruel Angel’s Thesis, as it were), but don’t do much for the characters audiences had become so attached to. The End of Evangelion movie expands outside of Shinji’s head, showing Third Impact in all its terrible glory and bringing the entire cast’s stories to definitive ends. Watch On Netflix

3 Memories Is An Impactful Sci-Fi Anthology Based on the short stories of Katsuhiro Otomo, written by Satoshi Kon and Katsuhiro Otomo

This anthology movie tends to pass under the radar, but deserves just as much attention as more famous sci-fi movies. In each of its three short films, Kon and Otomo revisit the themes of death, horror, and the advance of technology that they explored in both their previous works. However, each varies in tone, soundtrack, and animation style, creating unique experiences. In “Magnetic Rose,” a crew of astronauts explore an abandoned ship, quickly realizing that its past isn’t quite at rest. “Stink Bomb” condemns the poisoning of land and people by corporations with a darkly comedic story. And “Cannon Fodder” portrays how an authoritarian government and perpetual war dehumanizes people through the tragedy of a boy and his father. Watch On Tubi

2 The Girl Who Leapt Through Time Is An Old Story For A New Era Based on the novel by Yasutaka Tsutsui

When Makoto Konno stumbles upon a device that lets her leap back in time, she wastes no time using it to have as much fun as possible with zero consequences. Or so she thinks: it turns out that her careless use of this new ability has done major unforeseen damage. To fix what she’s broken, Makoto will have to learn what it is to take responsibility and love selflessly. One of Mamoru Hosoda’s best movies is one of many adaptations of Yasutaka Tsutsui’s 1965 novel. It takes considerable liberties with the story (for example, Makoto is a new heroine; her aunt Kazuko was the protagonist of the original book), but maintains the sci-fi core of the story. While it’s not as immersed in science as Paprika, a later work by Tsutsui, The Girl Who Leapt Through Time touches on the same themes of the good and evil humans can do with advanced technology. Watch On Crunchyroll

1 AKIRA Is The Quintessential Sci-Fi Anime Based on the manga by Katsuhiro Otomo

At the time of its release, AKIRA was the most expensive Japanese animated feature ever made.

Neo-Tokyo is a rough neighborhood, especially for Tetsuo Shima, the runt of a local biker gang. Tetsuo yearns to be stronger like his leader and best friend Kaneda, but gets his wish in the worst way possible. When he’s burdened with devastating psychic abilities, he quickly winds up with more power than he can control. AKIRA isn’t just one of the best sci-fi anime movies, it’s one of the most influential movies of all time. It was not only responsible for anime being seen as a true art form and achieving a new wave of popularity, especially in the United States, but it became a foundation of the cyberpunk subgenre and inspired the work of many artists from Masashi Kishimoto to Hideo Kojima to even Michael Jackson. Its groundbreaking visuals and animation techniques perfectly portray a world breaking down in the wake of a disaster. Overall, there’s no doubt that Akira is the best science fiction anime movie of all time. Watch On Hulu
