True Detective Theory The REAL Killer Was Shown In Night Country Episode 1 (& It Repeats Season 1)

SUMMARY

True Detective: Night Country incorporates supernatural elements with grounded horror, hinting at the involvement of the Inuit goddess Sedna in its mysteries. The drawing made by Pete’s son, Darwin, of a woman bleeding from her fingers could be a clue to Sedna’s role as an avenging deity seeking revenge. Night Country bears a resemblance to season 1, as the drawing of Sedna could be partially revealing the identity of the killer, similar to the drawing of the “green-eared spaghetti monster” in season 1.

Literature Review

True Detective’s central mystery is yet to be fully revealed, yet one theory suggests that the show has already shown the real killer behind things.

Warning: Contains SPOILERS for True Detective: Night Country Throughout its four seasons, True Detective has used complex storytelling and redirection to keep audiences guessing, but one theory suggests that Night Country has already revealed the identity of its mystery killer – and it’s a method that mirrors season 1. Incorporating supernatural elements with grounded horror, True Detective season 4 has already spawned countless fan theories. However, the show’s clear links to the anthology’s original season could be the biggest clue yet as to the actual solution – only this time with a fantastical twist.

Much as True Detective season 1 incorporated multiple mysteries, linking the individual murder of Dora Lange to an entire ring of cultists, Night Country has two separate story strands that intersect. One concerns the unsolved murder of a woman named Annie K, who suffered multiple stab wounds from her assailant. The other is centered on the sinister disappearance of a group of scientists, who were discovered frozen and naked on the ice. While both cases seem connected, the identity of the perpetrators remains unclear. One episode 1 clue, however, could provide the answer.

Who Pete’s Son Drew in True Detective: Night Country - Sedna Explained

Amid the mysterious goings-on at Tsalal Research Station and the aftermath of the Annie K murder, one True Detective: Night Country episode 1 moment hints at the involvement of the supernatural in Ennis’ multiple mysteries. In an early scene, Chief Danvers visits Pete Prior and his wife, Kayla, at their home. After a frosty exchange with Kayla, Danvers spots a spooky drawing made by the couple’s son, Darwin, of a woman with what looks like blood pouring from her fingertips. Although Kayla dismisses the picture as “just a local legend,” subsequent analysis suggests that the subject is actually Sedna – the Inuit goddess of the sea.

There are multiple versions of the Sedna myth. According to the most common account, as relayed by H. Newell Wardle in The Sedna Cycle: A Study In Myth Evolution, Sedna was a beautiful maiden who refused to marry any of the local hunters. One day, she was wooed by a passing fulmar, who fooled her into marrying him. After being stolen away to the cliffs, Sedna’s father came to try and rescue her. When the fulmar discovered her missing, he sent a great storm against the father’s boat. In a desperate attempt to save himself, he threw his daughter overboard, cutting off her fingers as she tried to cling on.

This image of a woman bleeding from her fingers seems to mirror the Sedna myth. Although different cultures offer differing accounts of her temperament, a common trope is that she is the mother of all sea creatures who seeks revenge on those who upset the balance between land and sea. Given the context of True Detective: Night Country, it could well be that Sedna’s anger is the key to everything.

Could Sedna Be Behind The Events Of True Detective: Night Country?

As the events at the heart of True Detective: Night Country become ever more mysterious, the possible involvement of a supernatural entity like Sedna becomes increasingly plausible. The repeated motif of “she’s awake”, heard throughout the series, would, for example, fit in with the conception of Sedna as an avenging deity. The power of Inuit culture and mythology, on full display throughout many season 4 scenes, also suggests that spirits like Sedna could influence the plot. There is also the mystery of the True Detective spiral, which while not previously associated with Sedna, does have spiritual significance.

A further factor is Sedna’s traditional role as a defender of the natural world. A major background plot point in True Detective: Night Country is the environmental concerns around the local mine poisoning local water supplies. If there really is a supernatural explanation for season 4’s murders, it could be that Sedna is seeking revenge on the townsfolk for neglecting their duty of care to the natural world. This explanation would certainly be a departure, given the grounded solutions in seasons 1-3. Nevertheless, moments like Lund’s terrifying warning to Navarro in Night Country episode 3 suggest that the show is indeed operating in supernatural territory.

True Detective’s Drawing Being A Big Clue Would Repeat Season 1

Of all the previous True Detective seasons, Night Country bears the closest resemblance to season 1. Beyond sharing themes and ideas, such as the signature spiral, the show also seems to be repeating aspects of season 1’s story – especially with Darwin’s Sedna drawing. In revealing the image of Sedna, the show could be partially revealing who is behind the crimes, just as season 1 did with the drawing of the so-called “green-eared spaghetti monster”.

First seen in an image provided by a girl chased by True Detective season 1 killer Errol Childress, the green-eared spaghetti monster was originally theorized by some fans to be a literal child-hunting creature. The truth was that the terrifying vision was just a child’s recollection of Childress himself, covered in green paint after decorating a house. However, the fact that the drawing appeared so early was actually a big clue towards Childress’ identity. Even if Sedna is not real (much like the green-eared spaghetti monster), True Detective could be repeating the trick by showing how the Night Country killer might appear.

True Detective: Night Country Could Fix A Season 1 Complaint

Unlike the image of the green-eared spaghetti monster, Darwin’s drawing has a basis in established mythology. This makes it much less likely that he has seen a real figure matching the description and becoming a potential candidate for the murderer. Conversely, however, the fact that the Sedna picture is likely not of a real person seen creeping about Ennis makes it much more likely that Night Country is fixing a season 1 problem – namely, that the show didn’t deliver on its promise of supernatural involvement.

While True Detective season 1 was gripping, some viewers were left disappointed that all the show’s discussions of Yellow Kings and spaghetti monsters were a dead end. After all was said and done, the monster behind season 1’s terrible crimes was just a man. Night Country could change this. From hints that Sedna is a major antagonist, to moments like Navarro’s polar bear vision and her warning from a seemingly possessed Lund, the show seems to be much more explicit in its supernatural connections. Much of the mystery is yet to be solved. Regardless, True Detective is finally recentering the show’s supernatural roots.
