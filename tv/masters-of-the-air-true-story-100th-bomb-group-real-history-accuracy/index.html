Literature Review

Apple TV’s Masters of the Air will tell the story of the World War II Air Force group, the 100th Bombardment, often called the “Bloody Hundredth.”

Apple TV’s World War II-based mini-series, Masters of the Air, tells the stories of the brave men of the 100th Bombardment Group, an actual United States Army unit established in 1942. The war drama, with executive producers Steven Spielberg, Tom Hanks, and Gary Goetzman, is meant as a companion to Band of Brothers (2001) and The Pacific (2010) and will continue the riveting exploration of the true stories of World War II. In the case of the 100th Bombardment Group, this means tales of those who took to the skies to confront the enemy.

Premiering on Apple TV on January 26, 2024, Masters of the Air features a massive cast portraying the historical figures of World War II’s early Air Force. Led by Austin Butler, best known for his star role in the 2022 film Elvis, the mini-series explores the stories of the ragtag team of hundreds of men who dedicated themselves to the “Bloody Hundredth.” Masters of the Air is based on the non-fiction novel Masters of the Air: America’s Bomber Boys Who Fought the Air War Against Nazi Germany by Donald L. Miller, which outlines who these men were, why the 100th Bomb Group earned its bloody reputation, and how they turned the tides of the war against Adolf Hitler and his allies.

              10 Most Exciting Things To Expect From Masters Of The Air   The latest WWII drama from the creators of Band of Brothers, Masters of the Air, has plenty of reasons for viewers to get excited for its release.

What Masters Of The Air Is Based On Masters of the Air is about the 100th Bombardment Group, but in particular, it is adapting a 2006 book called Masters of the Air: America’s Bomber Boys Who Fought the Air War Against Nazi Germany by Donald L. Miller. This book details the challenges that the 100th Bombardment Group faced while fighting Nazi Germany, from flying dangerous missions to being captured in prison camps. The book even delves into the effects bombings had on German cities. Ultimately, Masters of the Air depicts Miller’s story, while also diving deep into the real men that were a part of this incredible force.

The 100th Bombardment Group Was Activated In November 1942 In Boise, Idaho

The real-world Masters of the Air, the 100th Bombardment Group, was conceived on paper at an Orlando Army Base on June 1, 1942 (according to the 100th Bomb Group Foundation official website), and the 230 enlisted men and 24 officers were officially transferred in on October 27, 1942, through “Special Order 300.” Stationed in Boise, Idaho, the group wouldn’t be formally activated until November 14, 1942. The group entered its second phase of training by December, at which point the 100th Bombardment was organized into 37 crews, with ten men assigned to each crew. At this point, Col. Darr Alkire became the Group’s first Commanding Officer, but after their first training mission, Alkire would be replaced.

The Group’s Commanding Officer Left After Their First Practice Mission & Was Replaced By Howard Turner

The 100th Bomb Group underwent rigorous training under Darr Alkire until April 20, 1943, when they went through their first training mission. The Group started in Kearney, Nebraska, and all 37 flight crews were intended to fly the nearly 1300 miles to Hamilton Field, California. The practice mission reportedly yielded poor results, with Col. Alkire taking full responsibility for the failed attempt. He was ultimately relieved of command of the 100th Bomb Group (only five months after his appointment). He was later assigned to the 449th Bomb Group.

              Masters Of The Air Is Already Beating Band Of Brothers' First Follow-Up In One Major Way   The Pacific never reached the same heights as Band of Brothers, and Masters of Air has already gotten closer to the original series in one big way.

After Alkire’s departure, Col. Howard M. Turner was transferred to Kearney to take over the command of the 100th Bomb Group. Turner saw the Group through more training, and as soon as late May, the men were ready for their flight across the Atlantic Ocean. On June 6, 1943, the 100th Bomb Group landed at the operational base at Podington, England, and a couple of days later, all teams arrived at their permanent base at Thorpe Abbotts, England. Col. Turner only maintained his command until July 1, 1943. These first two commanders of the 100th Bomb Group are not among the character and cast list for Masters of the Air. Instead, the Group’s third commander, Col. Neil B. “Chick” Harding, will be featured in the Apple TV series.

Col. Neil B. Harding will be played by The Crown’s James Murray in Masters of the Air.

How The Group Earned Their “Bloody Hundredth” Nickname

It didn’t take long for the 100th Bombardment Group to earn the nickname the “Bloody Hundredth.” Though Masters of the Air’s Air Force team suffered similar casualties as other Bombardment groups by the end of World War II, they notoriously lost most of their aircraft on several single missions. In the months following their arrival in England, the 100th Bomb Group focused its attacks on Germain airfields and factories, as well as the naval facilities in France. During these missions, they would typically send 13 to 15 aircraft, with only one or two planes making it home. In all, the 100th’s losses included an estimated 229 planes, with 757 men killed or MIA and 923 taken as prisoners of war.

The Real 100th Bomb Group’s Biggest Battles & WWII Exploits Explained

The 100th BG’s first combat mission (the only one conducted under Col. Howard Turner) occurred on June 25, 1943, and involved an attack on the submarine yards of Bremen, Germany. This mission saw significantly fewer casualties than those that would later gain the Group its “Bloody” reputation, with only three planes (30 men total) lost. A couple of months later, under Chick Harding’s leadership, the 100th set out on its most successful and notable mission on August 17, 1943. It was an attack on a German aircraft factory, contributing 25 to 30 percent of the country’s fighter planes.

This attack saw the loss of nine 100th Bombardment crews (totaling 90 souls) and earned them their first Distinguished Unit Citation. Though the mission was a success, the 100th’s losses would only grow, leading to the “Black Week” of October 1943. After targeting Bremen, Germany, on October 8, several planes were destroyed, heavily damaged, or MIA, leaving only 17 able to fly for Marienburg, Germany on October 9. Miraculously, no further planes were lost during this attack, but the mission at Münster on the 10th saw only one airplane make it home.

Though “Black Week” nearly saw the 100th Bombardment obliterated, March 1944 saw the group head into what is now known as “The Big Week.” Given some time to rebuild, the 100th resumed their attacks on German aircraft factories, earning another Distinguished Unit Citation. On D-Day, June 6, 1944, the 100th Bombardment famously bombed several vital bridges and gun emplacements, losing only a few planes in the effort. This good luck continued through their remaining operations until the 100th’s final mission on April 20, 1945, in Berlin, with no losses recorded.

What Happened To The 100th Bomb Group’s Surviving Soldiers & Pilots After The War

Though the 100th Bombardment saw approximately 757 men either killed in action or missing, with another 923 confirmed taken prisoner of war, several soldiers were able to return home to their loved ones after World War II. Since the war’s conclusion, the 100th has continued to hold reunions for its surviving soldiers and their families every two years. During the first reunion in 1947, 39 survivors were reported to be in attendance. Many of these individuals will be characters in Masters of the Air, including Robert Rosenthal (Nate Mann), who has done several interviews over the years about his experience with the group.

Also in attendance at this first 100th Bombardment reunion were 19 friends and family members of the hundreds of brave souls who fell with their planes during World War II. Masters of the Air is guaranteed to explore their stories, outlining the actions of soldiers like Gale Winston Cleven (Austin Butler), who gave their lives for the freedoms earned in World War II. Their time as the titular Masters of the Air means expectations are high for the upcoming series since these stories are certainly worth excellence.

How Accurate Is Masters Of The Air To The 100th Bomb Group’s True Story?

Ultimately, Masters of the Air seems quite accurate to Donald L. Miller’s book and the real history of the 100th Bomb Group. In the show’s early episodes, key figures of the 100th Bomb Group are introduced and details about them and their relationships are revealed, such as Gale Cleven and John Egan’s friendship and Harry Crosby’s airsickness. Furthermore, the series depicts the mens’ flight to Thorpe Abbotts and their early missions in the spring and summer of 1943. Therefore, Masters of the Air seemingly prioritizes accuracy when it comes to its characters and the events they are going through.
