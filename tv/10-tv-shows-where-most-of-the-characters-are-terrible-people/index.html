10 TV Shows Where Most Of The Characters Are Terrible People

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1500 words 7 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

TV shows featuring terrible people offer a glimpse into the darker side of humanity, humanizing them and making their evil decisions calculated and sinister. Terrible people are great for comedy, as sitcoms like Seinfeld and It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia make light of the darkness within people’s souls. Shows like Succession and Veep showcase the morally bankrupt actions of people in positions of power, revealing their disregard for others and their obsession with personal gain.

Literature Review

Terrible people are a joy to watch. Comedy and drama shows both portray morally bankrupt characters to highlight the darkness within all of us.

Terrible people are often great to watch, and some of the best TV shows populate their worlds with nothing but villains. These shows can offer a glimpse into the darker side of humanity, with characters acting upon their most primal desires. Shows like Succession and Veep show the horrific deeds of people in positions of power, but they also humanize them just enough to make their decisions seem even more dispassionate. Realistic characters are more relatable, and if they are more than just caricatures of evil, then their misdeeds become more calculated and sinister. Terrible people are also great for comedy, with sitcoms like Seinfeld and It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia showing people living with no consideration for other people’s feelings. In this way, these shows make light of the darkness hiding within people’s souls. These morally bankrupt characters are an expression of how people might like to live if they could be free from moral and legal consequences. The joke is about how low they are so often willing to go to indulge their reckless impulses. Whether it’s for comedy or drama, people love to watch characters behaving badly.

11 Succession Everyone at Waystar Royco contributes to making the world a worse place

Based on Logan Roy’s abusive parenting techniques, it’s no surprise that his children turned out to be just as manipulative and selfish. He actively encourages ruthless competition among his children, playing into his warped view of the world. Logan built his media empire off of exploiting outrage, so it’s not as if he was starting from a place of moral righteousness anyway. Everyone at Waystar Royco is complicit in the company’s crimes and shady cover-ups, and this extends to their despicable political meddling, ultimately ushering a white nationalist into power.

10 Seinfeld The four friends end up in prison for their selfish ways

The controversial series finale of Seinfeld lays bare the selfishness and irresponsibility of the four main characters in a protracted trial filled with character witnesses. Jerry is faced with the elderly woman he mugged for a loaf of marble rye bread, and George has to admit how he faked a disability to get access to a private bathroom. Season 8, episode 3, “The Bizarro Jerry” underlines how codependent the friends are. Elaine tries to make friends with nice, normal people, but she ultimately realizes that she needs Jerry, George and Kramer, because nobody else will put up with her behavior.

9 Mad Men Vile bigotry becomes an everyday occurence at Sterling Cooper Draper Pryce

Almost everyone at Sterling Cooper Draper Pryce is involved in an extramarital affair. It is so normalized at the company that nobody bats an eye when Roger decides to leave his wife for his 20-year-old secretary. This is just one of the everyday injustices that go unnoticed, but there is plenty of casual racism and sexism in the office too. Mad Men doesn’t flinch in its portrayal of 1960s America, with even the nicest characters casually divulging abhorrent views on the civil rights movement, women’s places within society, and homosexuality.

8 It’s Always Sunny In Philadelphia The gang have no moral limits on their self-serving schemes

Each new season of It’s Always Sunny sees the gang reaching new lows. Their get-rich-quick schemes are so morally detestable that pretending to have AIDS to skip the line at a theme park and doing blackface for an unauthorized Lethal Weapon sequel probably don’t even crack the top ten worst things they have ever done. They’re all terrible influences on one another, and some of the best episodes of It’s Always Sunny come from the gang getting involved in absurd and offensive plans, like kidnapping a local critic or trapping their friends in a burning apartment.

7 The White Lotus The White Lotus revels in mocking the rich and famous

One of the key elements which unites the different seasons of The White Lotus is the premise of bad people having a bad time. The entitled guests at the resort don’t seem to realize their own privilege, complaining about minor inconveniences until a full-blown murder mystery starts to unravel, putting their petty problems into perspective. The upcoming season 3 of The White Lotus has added more big names to the cast, but it’s reasonable to expect the same formula lampooning the faults of the one-percent, and killing a few of them off.

6 The Sopranos The Sopranos is partly responsible for a whole generation of loathsome TV characters

Tony Soprano is one of TV’s greatest antiheroes, an unfaithful, violent, brutal mob boss. Tony’s associates in the DiMeo crime family are similarly prone to violence, and The Sopranos has many shocking deaths throughout its six seasons. In a show filled with cold-blooded killers and twisted manipulators, Tony Soprano may not even be the worst person. He at least has some humanizing qualities, like his pride in his children and his love of animals. Other characters, like Ralph Cifaretto and Christopher Moltisanti, are beyond redemption, making Tony seem sympathetic in comparison.

5 Arrested Development The high society family only care about maintaining their luxurious lifestyles

In the first episode of Arrested Development, George Bluth is arrested for a litany of financial crimes, but it soon turns out that he is just about the most morally upstanding member of his family. Lindsay is a self-centered performative activist, Gob is a narcissistic playboy, and Lucille is a cruel and manipulative mother. Michael sees himself as the moral center of Arrested Development’s Bluth family, and the only hope to provide his son with a positive role model. Unfortunately, the out-of-touch mega-rich Bluths find a way to poison his moral code as well.

4 Sons Of Anarchy There are no true heroes in the warring gangs of California

Sons of Anarchy presents a surprisingly sophisticated and democratic motorcycle gang, but they are still involved in importing and selling illegal firearms. Some of the gang’s members exist in a morally gray area. They are distrustful of the police, believing that they can do a better job of protecting their community, but they still extort local businesses and use violence and intimidation whenever deemed necessary. Jax starts out by attempting to move the gang away from its criminal operations, but he finds himself drawn deeper into their violent way of life, and he racks up an immense kill count.

3 Top Boy Violence is a way of life in Top Boy’s vision of London’s criminal underworld

Sully and Dushane’s quest to control the drug trade in their small corner of London is backed up by brutal bursts of violence. Every character in Top Boy lives by a shared moral code which prizes loyalty above all else, but murder is just part of the game. Modie is more erratic, and Jamie is more calculated, but everyone aspiring to reach the top shares the same violent streak. Sully’s execution of Dris is just one example of how old friendships can mean very little in the context of the ongoing struggle for power.

2 Veep Veep’s politicians care much more about their public image than their impact

Armando Iannucci’s scathing political satire paints a grim picture of the Washington D.C. bubble. Selina Meyer is the ruthlessly ambitious politician who draws a collection of vile spin doctors and opportunists into her orbit, each clinging to her coattails in their desperate pursuit of success. Any genuinely good people are either manipulated and dismissed like Richard Splett or thrown under the bus like Gary Walsh. In Iannucci’s vision of American politics, those in power only serve themselves, without any thought of what might be beneficial for the country or its people.
