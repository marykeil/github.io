8 Times TV Shows Switched Up Their Visual Styles That Resulted In Unforgettable Episodes

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1500 words 7 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

TV shows sometimes switch up their art styles for special episodes, giving viewers a refreshing change in visuals and animation. Popular shows like Bob’s Burgers and Community have experimented with different art styles, showcasing the power of collaboration and fan influence. Changing art styles can provide deeper insights into characters, like in Abed’s Uncontrollable Christmas episode of Community, and can enhance storytelling through unique visual experiences.

Literature Review

These 10 TV shows decided to take a different artistic direction on-screen, by switching up their individual visual styles for a singular episode.

While TV shows tend to find their format and stick with it, there have been some brilliant episodes that see a show switch up theirvisual styles, resulting in one brilliant episode. Each TV show will have its respective art style, whether this is done through the design of its characters and scenes in animation or cinematography and direction in live-action shows. It’s easy to get used to a TV show looking one particular way, so it’s always fun when one episode takes a different artistic direction. A change in art style in a TV show can happen for several reasons. This can be because of a crossover between TV shows, a way to spice up a filler episode, or, in the case of animated shows, an opportunity to display other artists’ work. While not every show decides to switch up its appearance for a singular episode, it’s always a refreshing change to see an episode of a TV show in a different light, color, or even dimension.

8 Bob’s Burgers Season 8, Episode 1: Brunchsquatch

Bob’s Burgers

Release Date January 9, 2011 Cast H. Jon Benjamin , Dan Mintz , Eugene Mirman , Larry Murphy , John Roberts , Kristen Schaal Seasons 13

One of Bob’s Burgers’ best episodes saw a brilliant change in design, with each scene shifting into another art style. The animation for this episode was inspired by fan-submitted artwork through an online contest, with 60 different artists working alongside the show to create the final product. While the Bob’s Burgers voice cast remained the same, each scene looked different, varying in coloring, character design, and visual structure. “Brunchsquatch” shows just how popular Bob’s Burgers has become over the years. It also proved just how powerful a show’s fanbase can be, and how animation, like any other art form, is a collaborative process. Not only was this episode a great way for Bob’s Burgers to kick off season 8, but it’s also an enjoyable watch, especially when the time is taken to fully consume all the unique art styles that appear with each new scene.

7 Community Season 4, Episode 9: Intro To Felt Surrogacy

Community

Release Date September 17, 2009 Cast Ken Jeong , Chevy Chase , Joel McHale , Jim Rash , Gillian Jacobs , Alison Brie , Yvette Nicole Brown , Donald Glover , Danny Pudi Seasons 6

Hit sitcom Community included a puppetry episode in season 4, with the study group recounting a tale involving a hot air balloon. This was also a musical episode with several original songs and included the voice of Community’s guest star, Sarah Bareilles. The inclusion of puppets made this episode even funnier, especially in scenes with a shirtless puppet version of Jeff (Joel Hale), but it also delved further into the mind of Dean Pelton (Jim Rash), who was determined to give the study group a session of puppet therapy.

6 The Fairly OddParents / The Adventures Of Jimmy Neutron, Boy Genius The Jimmy Timmy Power Hour Specials

“The Jimmy Timmy Power Hour” famously saw the TV crossover between two huge children’s shows: The Fairly OddParents and The Adventures of Jimmy Neutron, Boy Genius. The combination of these shows was highly anticipated, especially due to the success of both on Nickelodeon in the early 2000s. Although animated shows have had crossover episodes before, such as The Simpsons and Family Guy, “The Jimmy Timmy Power Hour” specials were unique because of how different the two shows looked. The trilogy specials used multiple animation styles. Traditional, hand-drawn elements were used in The Fairly OddParents segments and computer graphics for Jimmy Neutron. While each show had its respective style, it was interesting to see the characters appearing differently from the norm, with Jimmy (Debi Derryberry) in two dimensions and Timmy (Tara Strong), Cosmo (Daran Noris), and Wanda (Susanne Blakeslee) in three.

5 Community Season 2, Episode 11: Abed’s Uncontrollable Christmas

Community changed up its art style more than once, with the episode “Abed’s Uncontrollable Christmas” using stop-motion animation. The sitcom holiday episode allowed audiences to understand Abed (Danny Pudi) on a deeper level, which is represented through stop-motion. While the use of this art style is generally connected to children’s media, in this instance, it’s a connection to Abed and his relationship with his mother. Abed discovering she wouldn’t be visiting for Christmas resulted in his delusion, trying to remember a simpler time as a child, celebrating the holidays with his family before his mother left them. The way Abed views everything in stop-motion also shows just how much attention he pays to his friends, with each being perfectly replicated in this new style. This beautifully wholesome episode was not only refreshing with its change in visual style but is also a great watch in general.

4 It’s Always Sunny In Philadelphia Season 14, Episode 6: The Janitor Always Mops Twice

It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia

Release Date August 4, 2005 Cast Charlie Day , Rob McElhenney , Kaitlin Olson , Glenn Howerton , Danny DeVito Seasons 16

It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia’s hilarious episode “The Janitor Always Mops Twice” delves into Charlie Kelly’s (Day) mind and sees him, the Waitress (Mary Elizabeth Ellis), and the rest of the gang in a black and white, noir world. This episode saw certain aspects flooded with color, gangster characterization and costume design, and a genre-typical soundtrack, with everything being seen through Charlie’s memory via traditional film noir styling. Although it’s unlikely that the events of this episode actually happened in the show’s canon, it was an interesting way for audiences to get a better understanding of Paddy’s Pub’s illiterate janitor.

3 Fringe Season 3, Episode 6: Lysergic Acid Diethylamide

Fringe

Release Date September 9, 2008 Cast Joshua Jackson , Blair Brown , Anna Torv , John Noble , Leonard Nimoy , jasika nicole , Lance Reddick , Mark Valley , Seth Gabel Seasons 5

In Fringe season 3, Olivia (Anna Torv) is exposed to LSD, in order for Peter (Joshua Jackson) to enter her mind and locate her ego, resulting in him seeing her thoughts in an animated style. Despite Fringe already being one of the best sci-fi TV shows, this was the first time it changed style, but the in-story explanation makes the episode even more interesting. Although it only saw 16 minutes of animation, out of its average 43-minute runtime, this episode was a fascinating way to present the effects of drugs, as well as the complex way thoughts can be interpreted by different people.

2 My Name Is Earl Season 2, Episode 8: Robbed A Stoner Blind

My Name Is Earl

Release Date September 20, 2005 Cast Ethan Suplee , Jason Lee Seasons 4

After trying to make peace with former drug addict Woody (Christian Slater) and eating some mysterious medicine, Earl (Jason Lee) finds his vision warping, causing him to see everyone else as if they were made of clay. The effects lead the titular character to become passionate about the environment, determined to save everyone from the dangers of pollution. While it only seems fair that Earl would experience these claymation delusions, given how he’d previously treated Woody, it was still a hilarious way for My Name is Earl to provide a glimpse into Earl’s psyche.

1 Scrubs Season 4, Episode 17: My Life In Four Cameras

Scrubs

Release Date October 1, 2001 Cast John C. McGinley , Robert Maschio , Donald Faison , Christa Miller , Neil Flynn , Judy Reyes , Aloma Wright , Zach Braff , Sarah Chalke , Sam Lloyd , Ken Jenkins Seasons 9

The Scrubs episode “My Life in Four Cameras” makes a comment on the traditional sitcom format, showing the world through J.D.’s (Zach Braff) eyes. After meeting former Cheers writer Charles James (Ken Lerner) after he’s admitted, J.D. starts to wonder what his life would be like if it were a sitcom. Alongside a rather solemn storyline, Sacred Heart transforms, with brighter lighting, additional scene cuts representing a multi-camera setup, and a laugh track. Ironically, this episode won Scrubs an Emmy for Outstanding Multi-Camera Picture Editing despite the show’s general singular camera format.
