10 Biggest Reveals From Netflix s Alexander The Making Of A God Documentary

SUMMARY

Alexander the Great used bold cavalry tactics to defeat much larger armies, showcasing his superior mobility and military genius. Alexander’s mother, Olympias, played a significant role in shaping his ambition and convinced him that he was destined to conquer the world. Alexander was a man of many talents whose legacy is complex, but undeniably compelling.

Literature Review

Netflix’s Alexander: The Making of a God reveals 10 incredible facts about the life of Alexander the Great and his conquest of the Persian Empire.

Alexander: The Making of a God reveals 10 incredible facts about one of history’s most revered figures: Alexander the Great. This six-part Netflix docuseries follows Alexander’s life from succeeding his father to defeating Darius III and toppling the Persian Empire. Alexander: The Making of a God has a great cast, which it uses to create entertaining reenactments of the events described by the series’ contingent of expert historians. Although some details are left out of Alexander: The Making of a God, the docuseries gives a generally accurate account of Alexander’s life from age 20 to about 26. The series chooses not to cover Alexander’s youth or any of his conquests after the death of Darius III, but it still provides an incredibly vivid picture of his conflict with Persia. Along the way, Alexander: The Making of a God reveals 10 fascinating facts about Alexander’s life and conquests.

10 Alexander The Great Used Bold Cavalry Tactics To Pull His Enemies Apart Alexander’s elite Macedonian cavalry was critical to his military success

Before the infamous and mysterious death of Alexander the Great, he carved out one of the greatest empires the world has ever seen using innovative and bold cavalry tactics. Alexander: The Making of a God demonstrates the way that Alexander used his superior mobility to outmaneuver armies much larger than his own. A great example of this is the decisive Battle of Gaugamela, which Alexander famously won by running his cavalry parallel to the Persian defensive line, pulling Darius’ left flank with him before turning to charge through the hole this created in the Persian ranks. The Macedonian phalanx, an infantry formation developed by Alexander’s father, Phillip II, also played an integral role in Alexander’s military successes.

9 Alexander The Great’s Mother Set Him On A Conqueror’s Path Alexander’s mother was a Greek princess named Olympias

One thing Alexander: The Making of a God reveals about Alexander the Great’s relationships is that his mother, Olympias, had a massive influence on him. According to the Netflix docuseries, Alexander’s mother is the one who set him on his path, convincing him that he was the son of a god, destined to conquer the world. The docuseries also lays the murder of several potential challengers to Alexander’s claim to the Macedonian throne at Olympias’ feet. She is made out as being far more ambitious than Alexander early on, which is an interesting detail if true.

8 Alexander’s Receptiveness To Other Cultures Caused Dissent In His Ranks Alexander often adopted the clothing and/or traditions of the territories he conquered

Alexander understood that he could never rule such a large empire if he tried to impose Macedonian culture on everyone he conquered.

One of Alexander the Great’s best qualities as an emperor was his receptiveness to other cultures. Alexander was educated by Aristotle, one of the greatest thinkers in human history, and the understanding of other cultures he took from that education made him far more open-minded toward foreign customs and traditions. Alexander understood that he could never rule such a large empire if he tried to impose Macedonian culture on everyone he conquered. Unfortunately, this caused dissent among Alexander’s Macedonian troops, who accused him of abandoning his heritage. Dissent among his troops was one of Alexander’s greatest roadblocks, eventually forcing him to abandon his Indian Campaign after his exhausted, homesick soldiers mutinied, forcing Alexander to turn toward home.

7 Alexander The Great And Darius’ Rivalry Shaped The World For Centuries Alexander’s victory allowed Hellenistic culture to sweep across western Asia

Alexander: The Making of a God spends a great deal of time focusing on the rivalry between Alexander the Great and King Darius III. This approach shows how profoundly impactful their rivalry was on the course of history. Alexander may have died at just 32 years old, his empire disintegrating almost immediately, but the diffusion of Greek culture he fostered from the Balkans to India changed the world forever and led to the formation of modern, Hellenistic Western society. Had Darius won this conflict, the ideas and schools of thought that now define Western culture might not be relevant. It should be noted that the Roman Empire, which took its first breath about 300 years after Alexander’s death, might have achieved a similar diffusion of Greek culture, but it’s difficult to know what the Roman Empire may have looked like without Alexander’s influence.

6 Alexander The Great Had A Brutal Side To Him Alexander’s violent nature is often understated in historical accounts

Alexander’s willingness to burn a city full of innocent people — people he purported to protect, no less — proves how cruel he could be.

One thing that Alexander: The Making of a God reveals rather coyly is the brutal side of Alexander the Great. Alexander is one of the most romanticized figures in history, but this Netflix docuseries hints at the dark side that lurked beneath Alexander’s refined appearance. In Alexander: The Making of a God episode 2, his ruthless side rears its ugly head when he orders Ptolemy to let Halicarnassus burn after its people refuse to join his cause. Alexander’s willingness to burn a city full of innocent people — people he purported to protect, no less — proves how cruel he could be.

5 Alexander The Great Respected Darius Despite Their Rivalry Alexander gave Darius an elaborate funeral in Babylon

Alexander: The Making of a God is careful to emphasize the respect that Alexander held for Darius and Persia. He understood that his accomplishments were, as yet, nothing compared to Persia’s, and he recognized that if he planned to sit on Darius’ throne, he couldn’t afford to set a precedent of disrespect towards that throne. So, Alexander chose to host an elaborate funeral for Darius, carefully framing himself as the lawful successor to the kingship. Though part of the respect he showed Darius was clearly political, Alexander seems to have genuinely considered Darius a peer — perhaps his only peer.

4 Alexander The Great’s Tomb Is Still Missing Alexander was originally buried in Memphis, Egypt before his remains were moved to Alexandria

Alexandria is most commonly believed to be the location of his tomb, as it is the last place it can be reliably located in the annals of history.

Another fascinating detail revealed in Alexander: The Making of a God is that Alexander’s tomb has been missing for over a thousand years. The docuseries details the efforts of Dr. Pepi Papakosta to excavate Alexander’s tomb from its believed resting place in Alexandria, but sadly, a marble statue of Alexander is as close as she comes. Alexandria is most commonly believed to be the location of his tomb, as it is the last place it can be reliably located in the annals of history, but theories abound concerning the location of his body if, indeed, it wasn’t destroyed centuries ago.

3 Alexander The Great May Have Truly Thought Of Himself As A God Alexander referred to himself as the son of Zeus-Ammon

Obviously, it’s impossible to know exactly what went on inside Alexander the Great’s head. However, Alexander: The Making of a God makes a strong argument that Alexander truly thought of himself as a living god, or at least a demi-god. The docuseries suggests that Alexander was told by his mother that he was the son of Zeus. This belief was reinforced when an oracle in Siwa told him he was the son of Amun, an Egyptian god closely tied to Zeus. Alexander insisted from then on that he was the son of Zeus-Ammon, entitled to rule via his divine parentage. While Alexander may have considered himself divine or semi-divine, his insistence on being considered such by his people may have been a manipulation meant to legitimize himself in the eyes of people who had thought of their kings as divine for centuries.

2 Alexander The Great Was Deeply Spiritual Alexander often sought the blessings of various gods

Whether Alexander thought he was a god or not, it’s clear from watching Alexander: The Making of a God that he was a deeply spiritual person. This is evident on several occasions, most notably when he forces his army to halt so that he can make a sacrifice at the Shrine of Achilles before marching into Asia Minor. Later, Alexander would nearly kill himself making the hazardous trek through the desert to reach the oracle at Siwa. Alexander’s willingness to endanger himself and his army to seek the favor of the gods proves that he was a deeply spiritual man.

1 Alexander The Great Was A Man Of Many Talents Alexander’s brilliance went far beyond military tactics

If there’s one thing to take from Alexander: The Making of a God, it’s that Alexander the Great was a man of incredible talent. Professor Lloyd Llewellyn-Jones correctly asserts that not only was Alexander one of the greatest military commanders ever, he was also a poet, scientist, inventor, and philosopher. Though he could be brash — cruel even — Alexander was undoubtedly brilliant, and his skills went far beyond military tactics. Alexander’s legacy is complex, but there’s no doubting his intellect, curiosity, and ambition, which still grip the imagination of millions of people today.
