St. Swithin’s Day History & Folklore: Why People Think July 15 Predicts The Summer’s Weather

According to Britannica, the real St. Swithin (also spelled Swithun) was an Anglo-Saxon saint who lived in England in the 9th century. After serving as a council member to the kings Egbert and Aethelwulf, Swithin became the Bishop of Winchester in 852. Legend says that, at his request, he was buried in the Winchester Cathedral churchyard so that rain and passing footsteps might fall on his grave.

St. Swithin’s Day itself actually relates not to the date of the titular saint’s death, but to the day his remains were removed from the churchyard and put inside the cathedral. According to tradition, a great storm followed this act. As a result, a superstitious connection was established between St. Swithin’s Day and the weather. Legend has it that, if it’s raining on July 15th, it will rain for 40 days, whereas if it’s sunny, dry weather will continue.

Why One Day Takes Place On St. Swithin’s Day

There are several reasons why One Day is set on St. Swithin’s Day. Practically, given the decades-spanning scope of the story, revolving the narrative around a fixed point in the calendar helps to provide some structure. Furthermore, the date not only makes sense given the story’s starting point during university graduation, but also helps One Day avoid some of the clichés around more established calendar milestones like Valentine’s Day or Christmas. Beyond this, however, there is a more subtle thematic reason for the St. Swithin’s Day link.

With its association with meteorological phenomena, One Day’s St. Swithin’s Day setting provides a form of pathetic fallacy, using the weather to signal to the audience what may be ahead for the characters. At its most basic, rain suggests sadness, while sunshine implies positivity. This established narrative tool is only enhanced by setting the story around a day so closely associated with the weather. July 15th may seem like a random date in One Day, but it has been very deliberately chosen.
