Leia Always Shared Darth Vader s Greatest Weakness - & It s Why She Didn t Become A Jedi

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1600 words 8 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

Leia’s pursuit of the Force was driven by her desire to protect the people she loves, just like Anakin. Leia believed that the Jedi couldn’t save the people she loved, leading her to abandon her Jedi path. Leia’s selflessness and ability to let go of her attachments sets her apart from her father, Anakin.

Literature Review

Princess Leia shared Darth Vader’s weaknesses with attachments and a fear of loss in Star Wars, and that’s why she did not become a Jedi.

Princess Leia Organa, the biological daughter of Anakin Skywalker/Darth Vader, shared many of her father’s weaknesses - and it’s why she walked away from becoming a Jedi. Played by the late Carrie Fisher, Princess Leia Organa is one of the greatest heroes of the Star Wars saga. Star Wars has revealed Leia always used the Force in her political career - using words as weapons in the same way Anakin used a lightsaber.

Leia didn’t just inherit the Force, though. Although Leia’s adopted parents were responsible for her strong morals and ethics, she was more like Anakin than she would have liked. What’s more, it’s becoming clear Leia shared some of Anakin’s greatest weaknesses - and they were responsible for her decision to abandon the Jedi before the Star Wars sequel trilogy.

Leia Pursued The Force For The Same Reason Anakin Did

Leia’s attachments ran deep, and led to her pursuit of the Force in the first place. In Beth Revis’ novel The Princess & the Scoundrel, Leia - jealous of her brother Luke’s abilities - pondered why she wanted to grow in her own power. “What is the point of power if it cannot save the people I love,” she asks. Her perspective on power and its use follows the exact same logic that drove Anakin to become Darth Vader. For both Anakin and Leia, passion is what leads them to train as Jedi.

While they have very different origins (Anakin raised as a slave and Leia raised as a princess), they both want to protect those they love above all else. When Anakin is given the opportunity to leave Tatooine with Qui-Gon Jinn in Star Wars: Episode I - The Phantom Menace and become a Jedi, his thoughts go to his mother. He initially said he could not leave her, but then resolved to save her, promising his mother that he would return and free her. Even then, as a boy, Anakin was faced with limitations; he cannot save her yet, but the Force and the Jedi offer him that possibility, at least in his mind.

Leia’s mindset is similar: the Force is a power she can wield to save those she loves. Like Anakin, she was frustrated that she could not offer that protection; in the book, Leia explains, “If I had even a fraction of [Luke’s] power, I could do something with it, but I don’t.” Leia also has ample reasons for fearing loss. In the first Star Wars movie, Leia watched her home planet, Alderaan, explode, killing her parents. By the end of the original trilogy, she had a brother and has fallen in love with Han. Even before her son, Leia had gained a lot to lose.

Leia Left The Jedi Because She Believed It Couldn’t Save The People She Loved

Anakin wrestled with an intense fear of loss in Star Wars: Episode II - Attack of the Clones and Star Wars: Episode III - Revenge of the Sith. This was initiated by the brutal death of his mother, and his fear of Padmé’s death drove him to the dark side. The light side of the Force can do many things, but the Jedi knew of no way to save someone from death or bring someone back from the dead. This limitation, and Palpatine’s promise that only the dark side offers that power, is what drove Anakin’s fall.

When Anakin realizes that he cannot save Padmé with the light side of the Force, just as he could not save his mother, his solution is not, as Master Yoda had hoped, to let go of his fears and accept loss. Although not a violent turn to the dark side, Leia similarly did not release her fears or embrace the inevitability of loss and death; instead, she ended her Jedi path. Star Wars: The Rise of Skywalker suggests that this decision is mainly about protecting her son, but The Princess and the Scoundrel hints at an underlying connection to her father.

Leia sensed Ben woud die if she pursued her Jedi training, but she also had this history of wanting the power to prevent death. A desire for power and a fear of loss are both tied to the dark side, which calls into question what Leia’s Jedi path might have led to. She did not seek power in the way that Emperor Palpatine did, but her frustration in her inability to protect her loved ones meant she sought the kind of power traditionally associated with the dark side. Had she pursued this, perhaps Leia could have become the next Darth Vader, solidifying this connection to her father.

Leia Finally Let Go Of Her Attachments At Her Death

Ultimately, Leia’s path could not have been further from her father’s. Although she shared, as Obi-Wan rightfully pointed out in the Obi-Wan Kenobi series, Anakin’s passion, fearlessness, and forthrightness, and while she did have similar fears, there is one key difference: where Anakin chose selfishness, Leia chose selflessness. Unlike Anakin, Leia really does prioritize her loved ones throughout her life; she sacrifices herself just to help Kylo Ren see the light.

Although the tragic passing of Carrie Fisher certainly played a role in this plot, the conclusion to Leia’s story did offer closure on her connection to Anakin’s flaws. Leia accomplished something Anakin was unable to: she set aside her attachments and let go of her need for control. In death, Leia pulled Ben back to the light, but that did not mean that her son would live; in fact, she could not be sure her efforts to bring him back from the dark side would be successful. Leia surrendered herself to the Force and let go of her fears — what Anakin needed to do all along.

Leia Didn’t Save Her Son… But Ben Solo Saved The Person He Loved

Although Leia’s choice to valiantly lay down her life in The Rise of Skywalker did return Ben Solo to the light side of the Force, her predictions about her Jedi path were sadly correct. Ben died at the end of the Star Wars sequel trilogy. However, the cause of his death raises some interesting questions about this quest that both Leia and Anakin had pursued: Ben drains his life force, thereby resurrecting Rey. That is, he brings her back to life, both saving the person he loves and wielding the power to reverse death.

The conclusion of Star Wars: The Rise of Skywalker introduced a version of Force heal, revealing there is a way to reverse death itself - the power pursued by both Anakin and Leia. This is likely another aspect of the Force Dyad, but it’s also important to note that Ben was motivated by something very different to his mother and grandfather. He was not seeking additional power, but instead was simply trying to save Rey, presumably knowing this would kill him. In his last moments, Ben too demonstrated the selflessness that became central to Leia Organa - proving himself his mother’s son rather than simply Darth Vader’s grandson.
