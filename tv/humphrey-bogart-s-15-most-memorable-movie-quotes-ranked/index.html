Humphrey Bogart s 15 Most Memorable Movie Quotes, Ranked

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 2000 words 10 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

Bogart’s iconic line “Play it again, Sam” was never actually said in Casablanca, but he had many other memorable and quotable lines. Bogart’s portrayal of characters like Fred C. Dobbs in The Treasure of Sierra Madre and Philip Marlowe in The Big Sleep showcased his range as an actor. Bogart’s characters often had a blend of charm, vulnerability, and complexity, making his movie quotes stand out and resonate with audiences.

Literature Review

The legend of classic Hollywood Humphrey Bogart delivered some of the most memorable lines in cinema across movies Casablanca and The Big Sleep.

Humphrey Bogart was one of the biggest stars in classic Hollywood cinema and his roles have included some of the most memorable movie quotes in film history. From the romantic classic Casablanca to the film noir favorite In a Lonely Place, as well as the thrilling adventure The African Queen, Bogart, time and time again, delivered lines that have had a unique effect on popular culture and stand in audiences collectively memorable as some of the best quotes ever. With equal parts charm and vulnerability, Bogart could deliver a movie quote like no other. Bogart had countless phrases that immediately stood out to viewers as some of cinema’s most memorable and quotable lines, although potentially the most famous line associated with him, “play it again, Sam”, which was commonly attributed to Casablanca, was never actually uttered by any character in that movie at all. Despite this, Bogart had plenty of other memorable lines that have stood the test of time and today remain insanely quotable and are often referenced in current media and releases.

15 “I Think I’ll Go To Sleep And Dream About Piles Of Gold Getting Bigger and Bigger and Bigger.” The Treasure of Sierra Madre (1948)

The Treasure of Sierra Madre was one of the most interesting films in Humphrey Bogart’s acclaimed career. Tackling themes of greed, individualism, and selfishness, this John Huston film highlighted the darkness at the center of human existence. Bogart’s character of Fred C. Dobbs shined a light on the money hungry plot of The Treasure of Sierra Madre with this quote about dreaming “about piles of gold getting bigger and bigger and bigger” which showcased the aspirational nature of the movie’s characters, desperately trying to discover wealth during the gold rush of the 1920s.

14 “I Don’t Mind If You Don’t Like My Manners. I Don’t Like ‘Em Myself.” The Big Sleep (1946)

The private detective Philip Marlowe in The Big Sleep never exactly played by the rules and Humphrey Bogart’s expert portrayal of the character suited his individualistic and self-determined nature very well. This line about not liking his own manners was the perfect encapsulation of Marlowe’s contradictory nature, as Raymond Chandlers’ whiskey-drinking, chain-smoking, no-nonsense protagonist, Marlowe. It was showcased that Bogart’s portrayal of Marlowe could be as self-deprecating as he was determined, and The Big Sleep was full of incredible one-liners such as this.

13 “Oh I Didn’t Say I Was A Gentleman. I Said I Was Tired.” In a Lonely Place (1950)

The Nicolas Ray-directed film noir In a Lonely Place featured Humphrey Bogart as one of the most interesting characters, Dixon Steele. A down-on-his-luck Hollywood screenwriter, Bogart imbued Steele with vulnerability and dark undertones that made it one of his most sinister and complex roles. When being questioned by police about a murder, Steele was asked why he didn’t call the girl a cab as, “isn’t that what a gentleman usually does under the circumstances?” However, Steele replied that he never said he was a gentleman and implied there may be a darker truth hidden beneath the surface.

12 “My, My, My! Such A Lot Of Guns Around Town And So Few Brains.” The Big Sleep (1946)

Humphrey Bogart played the hardboiled private investigator Philip Marlowe in The Big Sleep and, in doing so, delivered some of the most memorable lines in film history. A classic story starring Bogart opposite his wife and collaborator Lauren Bacall, this line about so many guns with “so few brains” showcased just how far ahead of the curve Marlowe believed he was. Always attempting to appear one step ahead, Marlowe undermined the intelligence of those standing in his way to uncover the complex conspiracy of murder, blackmail, and love.

11 “There’s No Sacrifice Too Great For A Chance At Immortality.” In a Lonely Place (1950)

The struggling screenwriter, Dixon “Dix” Steele, played by Humphrey Bogart in In a Lonely Place, gave many hints that he had a darker nature beneath his vulnerable exterior. A thrilling exploration of jealousy and deceit, Bogart highlighted Dix’s willingness to do anything to achieve his aims with the line “there’s no sacrifice too great for a chance at immortality.” On the surface, this was about his screenwriting career, but it hinted he was willing to do whatever benefited him, to give in to his worst impulses, and to pose a threat to those in his life.

10 “A Man Takes A Drop Too Much Once In A While, It’s Only Human Nature.” The African Queen (1951)

With a reputation as a hard-drinking heavy smoker, Humphrey Bogart played into this distinction both in front and behind the camera. Bogart’s character of Charlie Allnut in The African Queen, much like himself, enjoyed a drink which was encapsulated by this quote about it only being “human nature” to take “a drop too much once in a while.” Bogart could expertly play this type of hard-drinking character and even won the Academy Award for Best Actor for his portrayal of Allnut.

9 “When You’re Slapped, You’ll Take It And Like It.” The Maltese Falcon (1941)

As Humphrey Bogart’s Sam Spade and Mary Astor’s Ruth Wonderly questioned Peter Lorre’s Joel Cairo in The Maltese Falcon, Spade told Cairo “when you’re slapped, you’ll take it and like it” in one of the most effective threats in movie history. The line represented Spade’s no-nonsense approach to investigating and showed that he was willing to step outside the rules to get results. Equally threatening and offensive, this tense interrogation scene was extra satisfying considering the low-life crook, Joel Cairo, was at the opposite end of this attack.

8 “We Didn’t Exactly Believe Your Story, Miss Wonderly. We Believed Your 200 Dollars.” The Maltese Falcon (1941)

Humphrey Bogart as Detective Sam Spade revealed his hand with this line delivered to Mary Astor as Ruth Wonderly. When the mystery surrounding Wonderly’s missing sister started unraveling, Spade showcased just how good a detective he was by stating he did not believe her story at all but instead worked for the one thing that really mattered: Money. Spade went on to say “you paid us more than if you had been telling us the truth” which highlighted that in the world of private investigators, the meaning of the word true was subject to change for the right price.

7 “A Man Alone, He Gets To Living Like A Hog.” The African Queen (1951)

While the characters played by Humphrey Bogart are so often portrayed as staunchly independent protagonists, they are often quickly revealed as vulnerable romantics in search of true love. This tenancy to want someone to love in life and the benefits of companionship can be seen from this memorable line by Bogart’s character of Charlie Allnut in the adventure film The African Queen. Allnut stated that “a man alone, he gets to living like a hog” which highlighted how the gin drinking Allnut had let himself go after so long without a woman in his life.

6 “Here’s Looking At You, Kid.” Casablanca (1942)

Casablanca

Release Date November 26, 1942 Director Michael Curtiz Cast Humphrey Bogart , Ingrid Bergman , Paul Henreid , Claude Rains , Conrad Veidt , Sydney Greenstreet Runtime 102 minutes

This iconic phrase was improvised by Humphrey Bogart during the filming of the Paris flashback scenes in Casablanca. Rick Blaine said “here’s look at you, kid” for the first time as a farewell to his love Ilsa Lung, and a reminder of all the memories they had made together. A term of endearment, Bogart’s unmatched delivery is presented with is unique charm and sincerity which helped the line achieve its iconic status today more than 80 years since Casablanca was first released.

5 “Louis, I Think This Is The Beginning Of A Beautiful Friendship.” Casablanca (1942)

In the final line of Casablanca, Bogart’s Rick Blaine uttered one of cinema’s most iconic phrases about the “beginning of a beautiful friendship.” However, this wasn’t in the original script at all (via BBC.) Casablanca producer Hal B. Wallis wanted to end on a positive closing phrase that would retain the film’s cynical nature and offered Bogart two options, the other being “I might have known you’d mix your patriotism with a little larceny.” The line hinted at more adventures between Blaine and Captain Louis Renault, and a Casablanca sequel was even planned, titled Brazzaville, which sadly never got made.

4 “I Was Born When She Kissed Me. I Died When She Left Me. I Lived A Few Weeks While She Loved Me.” In a Lonely Place (1950)

Humphrey Bogart’s character of Dixon Steele showcased his sensitive nature and deeply emotional wounds with his beautiful line from In a Lonely Place. Discussing his love for Laurel Gray, played by Gloria Grahame, this line was an eloquent representation of deep sorrowful love on its own, but when considered with the dark murderous plot of In a Lonely Place it became quite sinister. Dixon was a troubled and controlling man, and this obsessive love he had for Gloria meant he could hurt her in more ways than just emotionally. A memorable line that worked on several levels.

3 “We’ll Always Have Paris.” Casablanca (1942)

A slick and memorable line about the power of reminiscing over old love, Humphrey Bogart’s Rick Blaine told his Casablanca love interest, Ingrid Bergman’s Ilsa Lund, that they will “always have Paris.” A heartfelt representation of giving up on love for the greater good and of the enduring power of the memories Rick and Ilsa made together. Reflecting on the time that they spent together in Paris before the war, this iconic phrase gave viewers insight into happier times for the film’s protagonist before all the complexities seen in Casablanca were brought to the forefront.

2 “Of All The Gin Joints, In All The Towns, In All The World, She Walks Into Mine.” Casablanca (1942)

Uttered by Humphrey Bogart as Rick Blaine, this phrase highlighted the unique coincidence that caused Casablanca star Blaine to be confronted with his old love, Ilsa Lung, in a Moroccan bar of all places. The phrase showcased Bogart’s potential as a romantic lead who could still maintain his cynical nature. Bringing up true vulnerability, Blaine was cursing fate for forcing him to open his closed heart once more. The unexpected reunion led to shock and disbelief for Blaine, and he would be forced to deal with a flood of old memories and emotions.

1 “It’s The Stuff Dreams Are Made Of.” The Maltese Falcon (1941)

Referencing a William Shakespeare line from the play The Tempest “We are such stuff as dreams are made on, and our little life is rounded with a sleep,” Humphrey Bogart delivered one of cinema’s most memorable lines towards the end of The Maltese Falcon to represent the hollow nature of the sought-after ornamental bird. With so many chasing the wealth the object represented, in the end, it was the expectations people put on it that gave it meaning. The quote became so iconic that Warner Bros Studios began using it as a catchphrase to represent the magic of moviemaking. Sources: BBC
