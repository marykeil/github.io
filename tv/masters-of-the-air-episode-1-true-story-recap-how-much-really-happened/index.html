Literature Review

Masters of the Air episode 1 introduces Gale “Buck” Cleven, John “Bucky” Egan, and many more members of the historic 100th Bomb Group of World War II.

Summary Episode 1 of Masters of the Air introduces the 100th Bomb Group and their challenges during World War II. Some aspects of the episode are confirmed, such as that “Alice from Dallas” was the nickname of a real B-17 Flying Fortress owned by the 100th Bomb Group. However, other facts are harder to confirm, such as John “Bucky” Egan’s mishap with a narwhal tusk in Greenland.

In the very first episode of Apple TV+’s Masters of the Air, audiences are introduced to the 100th Bomb Group, or the “Bloody Hundredth” during the spring and summer of 1943, but how much of the episode truly happened in real life? Created by Tom Hanks and Steven Spielberg, Masters of the Air is a war drama following the true story of the men in the U.S. Army Air Forces’ 100th Bomb Group, which saw serious challenges during World War II. The cast of Masters of the Air includes Austin Butler, Callum Turner, Anthony Boyle, Barry Keoghan, and more. Masters of the Air episode 1 lays the foundation for the 100th Bomb Group, its key players, and its job within the war. At the very start, Gale “Buck” Cleven and John “Bucky” Egan are introduced as best friends who are both headed to the same Air Force unit in Europe. Upon arrival, Bucky discovers that performing missions is much more dangerous than it was made out to be. Meanwhile, Harry Crosby is an airsick navigator who gets his group into a bad situation. Ultimately, the 100th’s first mission is not only scrubbed, but ends in the significant loss.

8 Did The 100th Bomb Group Call Their Plane “Alice From Dallas”? Buck’s co-pilot suggests the nickname in episode 1

As Buck and his crew fly to Thorpe Abbotts, where the 100th Bomb Group will make their home, his co-pilot announces that they should call their plane, “Alice from Dallas.” The other men like this name, and soon, pass around a photo of one of the airman’s sweethearts. Thus, “Alice from Dallas, Alice our Palace” becomes a mantra among the men. As it turns out, “Alice from Dallas” truly was the name of a B-17 Flying Fortress that belonged to the 100th. It is unclear whether Buck was the aircraft’s pilot, but it certainly existed.

7 Did Gale “Buck” Cleven’s Almost Crash His Plane In Greenland? Buck makes a successful landing, despite high winds

In the very same scene, Buck has a very close call with his crew. The “Alice from Dallas” is set to land in Greenland to refuel before they continue on to England, however, severe winds make the landing far from easy. In Masters of the Air, Buck coolly manages to land the “Alice from Dallas”, despite some frightening turbulence, essentially calling the winds nothing more than a breeze. While there is no record of this event happening in real life, this scene does its job by showing Buck’s two greatest strengths: flying and staying calm under pressure.

6 Did Bucky Really Break A Narwhal Tusk Pretending To Be A Unicorn? Buck brings the bartender a unicorn toy to make up for it

After landing, Buck and the 100th hang out at a bar, and Buck has a delivery for the bartender from Bucky. Inexplicably, it is a plastic unicorn. Unsure of what happened, Buck asks for more information, and the bartender reveals that when Bucky passed through weeks prior, he broke a narwhal tusk hanging on the wall while drunkenly pretending to be a unicorn. Unsurprisingly, there are no firm records of this occurring in real life, however, that does not count it out completely. The tale could have been a more personal story taken from primary sources.

5 Did Harry Crosby Really Have Airsickness? Harry Crosby’s airsickness left his group with bad coordinates

Aside from Buck and Bucky, Harry Crosby is another significant character in Masters of the Air, though his introduction is not so happy. While flying into Thorpe Abbotts, Crosby’s nausea causes him to misread his map, causing his group to nearly fly into France. But was Harry Crosby truly airsick? According to Donald Miller’s book “Masters of the Air: America’s Bomber Boys Who Fought the Air War Against Nazi Germany,” the answer is yes. As portrayed in Masters of the Air episode 1, Crosby had doubts about his navigational abilities and suffered from airsickness at low altitudes.

4 Was Meatball The Dog Real? Captain Bernard DeMarco brings a dog named Meatball to Thorpe Abbotts

As Buck and the rest of the 100th finally land at Thorpe Abbotts, Bucky is surprised to find a dog with the crew. The husky is named Meatball, and has been adopted by Captain Bernard DeMarco, played by Adam Long. Apparently, the dog enjoys flying and remains with the 100th through episode 1. It turns out, Meatball the dog was very much real. As reported by the 100th Bomb Group Foundation, Meatball was a half-grown husky that the crew picked up in Labrador. Meatball remained at Thorpe Abbotts throughout the war.

3 Did The 100th Really Lose Most Of The 349th During Their Mission? All but 2 of the 349th planes were lost

At the climax of Masters of the Air episode 1, Buck, Curtis Biddick, and other members of the 100th fly their first mission over Germany. Unfortunately, right from the start, it does not go to plan. Even before facing German flak, one plane turns around due to mechanical troubles. Then, an entire squadron is out of alignment with the rest of the formation, making them vulnerable. Finally, when the German aircrafts begin their attack, the 349th is targeted, and the squadron loses all but two of its planes. The mission is ultimately scrubbed, and the 100th return to England unsuccessful. In general, this tracks with what went down during the real 100th’s first mission. In June 1943, the 100th set out on a mission to drop bombs on Bremen, Germany and other surrounding targets. In real life, their 17 planes were part of a bigger formation of 275 American bombers. During this mission, 18 planes were shot down, and three of them were from the 100th. This meant that 30 men were killed in action. In this way, Masters of the Air replicates both the losses of the mission, and the effects on the men afterward.

2 Did Steve Bosser’s Hands Really Freeze To His Gun? Sgt. Steve Bosser suffers from the cold temperatures during the mission

While the 100th’s first mission was a disaster on many levels, one specifically awful thing that occurred was that Sgt. Steve Bosser, who was Buck’s ball turret gunner, had his hands freeze to his gun after the Germans broke a hole through the gunner’s pool. While there are no records online of Bosser’s injuries, there is definitely still a chance that this occurred to the soldier. Once again, this Masters of the Air detail was likely gleaned from primary sources rather than online databases.

1 Was Colonel Harold Huglin Really Sick? Col. Harold Huglin shows signs of illness from his very first appearance

Throughout Masters of the Air episode 1, Colonel Harold Huglin appears a few times, exerting his power over the 100th. For example, he scolds Bucky about his behavior and presents their very first mission. However, another major aspect of Huglin’s character is his mystery illness, which shows itself when Huglin repeatedly vomits blood. Ultimately, Col. Huglin’s sickness is not present in any records online. Once again, this is a detail so specific that it likely came from first-hand accounts. Therefore, it is likely that Huglin’s sickness did really happen, but never made it online.
