10 Book Series Warner Bros. Needs To Turn Into A TV Series Instead Of Harry Potter

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1900 words 9 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

Warner Bros. should consider adapting other YA and middle grade fantasy books for TV series instead of remaking Harry Potter . The Diviners, The Infernal Devices, An Ember in the Ashes, The Raven Cycle, Inkheart , and other series would be great options for adaptation. These books offer fresh and diverse stories with compelling characters that have the potential to become as big as Harry Potter .

Literature Review

Warner Bros. doesn’t need to remake Harry Potter as a TV series when there are other great young adult and middle grade books to adapt.

Warner Bros. is working on a Harry Potter TV remake for Max, but there are plenty of other YA and middle grade fantasy books deserving of adaptations — and they should probably get a series instead. While the notion of a Harry Potter show that faithfully adapts the books is exciting for longtime fans, there’s controversy surrounding the upcoming reboot. For one, the Harry Potter movies remain immensely popular, and it’ll be difficult to sell viewers on a new cast. The backlash against author J.K. Rowling may also work against the series. Finally, the fact that Warner Bros. is repeating the original seven-book story rather than exploring other potential Harry Potter spinoffs is a sore point for some, as studios seem to be treading the same paths over and over. If Warner Bros. wants to dig into fresh stories, there are many other great YA and middle grade books to turn into TV series. With the right execution, any of them could become as big as Harry Potter.

10 The Diviners By Libba Bray An Atmospheric Setting & Supernatural Story Make This Ripe For Adaptation

Libba Bray’s The Diviners series masterfully blends horror and fantasy elements, and its atmospheric setting and compelling cast of characters make it ripe for a TV adaptation. Set in New York in the late 1920s, The Diviners follows new girl Evie O’Neill as her family sends her to live with her uncle in New York following a scandal at home. Evie and her friends soon realize something supernatural has awakened in the city, and they spend multiple books investigating and facing the occult. With diverse and likable characters, a supernatural mystery, and a fun setting, it’s a wonder The Diviners doesn’t have an adaptation yet. While there was discussion of a Diviners movie back in 2012, nothing ever came of it. If the rights are up for grabs, Warner Bros. should seize the opportunity to bring this story to the small screen. It’d certainly feel more original than a second take on Harry Potter, and it’d replicate some crucial elements from the Wizarding World series. Like Harry Potter, The Diviners follows a group of teens as they discover there’s more than the mundane. There’s no wizarding school, but it’s got New York with demons and ghosts — and that’s more than enough of a selling point.

9 The Infernal Devices By Cassandra Clare The Shadowhunters Saga Can Continue On The Small Screen

Cassanda Clare’s The Mortal Instruments series has received a movie and TV adaptation, but none of her other Shadowhunter books have made their way to the screen. The Infernal Devices would be an ideal way to continue the story, as it’s set prior to the events of The Mortal Instruments, and an adaptation wouldn’t need to connect to the previous ones. The Infernal Devices takes place in 19th-century London, and its atmosphere is enough to sell anyone wishing to spend more time in places like Diagon Alley and Hogsmeade. Clare’s books have a darker tone, but the characters make it easy to get invested. If Warner Bros. were to adapt The Infernal Devices as a television series, it could give Shadowhunters a shot at a proper adaptation. While repeating The Mortal Instruments a third time would be as tiring as a Harry Potter remake, it might be worth it to see Clare’s other Shadowhunters series brought to the screen. And even if Warner Bros. only adapted The Infernal Devices, it’d no doubt land among fantasy fans and historical romance lovers.

8 An Ember In The Ashes By Sabaa Tahir Its Themes Of Oppression And Rebellion Feel Relevant

An Ember in the Ashes is a gripping YA series by Sabaa Tahir, and it’s surprising it hasn’t been adapted into a TV show or movie yet. Paramount purchased the rights to the series more than a decade ago (via Deadline), but it doesn’t seem to have gone anywhere — and it’s unclear where things stand. If the rights become available, Warner Bros. would be wise to invest in this series. With its themes of oppression and uprising, it’d undoubtedly appeal to fans of movies like The Hunger Games, and the added fantasy element would help it stand out. Set in the tyrannical Martial Empire, An Ember in the Ashes follows Laia, a slave turned rebel spy, and Elias, a reluctant soldier training at the academy Laia is sent to. The two form a bond and eventually work together to change the future of the Empire. And while this series is darker than a story like Harry Potter, its compelling characters, timeless themes, and action would likely make it a successful TV series.

7 The Raven Cycle By Maggie Steifvater Blue’s Story Has Franchise Potential

Another beloved YA series that still doesn’t have an adaptation is Maggie Steifvater’s The Raven Cycle, and Warner Bros. should adapt this over a Harry Potter remake. A strange and unique story, The Raven Cycle follows Blue, a girl from a family of clairvoyants whose fate is to kill her “true love.” This prophecy becomes scarier than ever when Blue falls in with a group of Raven Boys and joins them in their efforts to wake a sleeping king. There’s a lot going on in The Raven Boys, but its lovable characters and unique world would do well on the small screen. The Raven Boys also has a spinoff series called The Dreamer Trilogy, which means a TV franchise won’t need to end after the first story is through. With at least one spinoff baked in, The Raven Boys could prove a promising addition to Warner Bros.’ lineup. The company simply needs to give unadapted book series a chance to shine.

6 Inkheart By Cornelia Funke These Books Deserve A Second Chance

Inkheart already had a failed adaptation back in 2008, but the underrated Brendan Fraser movie deserves a reboot on the small screen. While the 2008 film didn’t do Cornelia Funke’s books justice, the fun concept can work if executed properly. And shows like Percy Jackson & the Olympians and His Dark Materials prove that second-chance book adaptations can work. Inkheart could be just as magical for Warner Bros. as a Harry Potter reboot, and it comes without all the baggage. Inkheart follows Meggie, a girl whose father adores books but doesn’t read them aloud. Meggie soon learns why: because her father can read characters and objects out of their stories, as well as transport people into the pages of a novel. This happened to Meggie’s mother, forcing her into a book called Inkheart and bringing dangerous villains into the real world. When those villains track down Meggie and Mo, they’re taken on a fantastical journey that would make a stunning fairytale-esque adaptation.

5 The Simon Snow Trilogy By Rainbow Rowell Simon & Baz’s Adventure Has Everything A Fantasy Story Needs

If Warner Bros. is looking for an entertaining magic school story, it should adapt Rainbow Rowell’s Simon Snow trilogy — a story that pulls inspiration from Harry Potter. With a touching LGBTQ+ romance at its center, Simon Snow takes place at a magic school and has all the makings of a fun fantasy story. As its two main characters, Simon and Baz, come to term with their feelings for one another, Simon also grapples with being the “Chosen One.” The later Simon Snow books also explore what’s next for Simon and Baz after leaving their version of Hogwarts, putting an interesting spin on the magic-school trope. Rather than rehashing Harry Potter, Warner Bros. could adapt Rowell’s novels to add something new to the genre. It would also bring more LGBTQ+ representation to the small screen, a welcome development given all the diverse series getting canceled recently.

4 Akata Witch By Nnedi Okorafor This Would Bring Something New To The Fantasy TV Landscape

Those interested in the witch and wizard element of Harry Potter might enjoy Nnedi Okorafor’s Akata Witch series, which would make an ideal adaptation for Warner Bros. to pursue. The series follows 12-year-old Sunny, whose albinism and U.S. birth make her an outsider in Nigeria. Her differences become a good thing when she encounters a magical group called the Leopard People and forms an Oha Coven, setting her on a path to prevent tragedies and save others. Akata Witch is unique in everything from its story to its magic system, and it would bring something new to the fantasy TV landscape.

3 The Conqueror’s Saga By Kiersten White A Genderbent Vlad The Impaler Story Is Worth Watching

Kiersten White’s The Conqueror’s Saga is a far cry from Harry Potter, but it’s a YA series that doesn’t get enough attention during discussions about adaptations. That’s a shame, as White’s genderbent take on Vlad the Impaler is gripping and timeless. It tackles important subjects like sexism and puts a unique spin on history, and its politics and action would translate well to the small screen. Warner Bros. should consider adapting it, as it would fare well in a TV landscape where Dracula stories and similar narratives thrive.

2 Pandava By Roshani Chokshi A Middle Grade Story Based In Hindu Mythology Is Perfect

If Warner Bros. is looking for series that can appeal to all ages, Roshani Chokshi’s Pandava books still need an adaptation. Beginning with Aru Shah and the End of Time, this series follows a 12-year-old girl who lies to fit in. When she claims that a magic lamp at the museum her mom works at is cursed, Aru is forced to prove it. This sends everything spiraling out of control, freezing time and sending Aru on a quest to track down the legendary Pandava brothers. Based on Hindu mythology, this series is just the kind of middle grade story streamers need more of.

1 Silver In The Bone By Alexandra Bracken There’s Room For More Arthurian TV Shows

Alexandra Bracken’s Silver in the Bone series is ongoing, and only one book has hit shelves so far. However, it’s already showing promise with the first installment, which follows Tamsin Lark on a quest to free her brother from a curse. This journey takes her to Avalon, where she discovers the world of Arthurian legend. However, Avalon has lost its magic, presenting an obstacle for her group. Their adventure is one full of darkness and the occult and would make a gripping TV series. Given the success of shows like Cursed, it’s clear there’s room for more fantasy shows based on King Arthur, and Warner Bros. can capitalize on that.
