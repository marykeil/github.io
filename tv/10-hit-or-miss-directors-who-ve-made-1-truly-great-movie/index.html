10 Hit-Or-Miss Directors Who ve Made 1 Truly Great Movie

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1700 words 8 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

Hit-or-miss directors can still have one truly great movie. Making just one great film is more than most achieve in their entire lives. Some hit-or-miss directors started off with incredible debut movies but failed to recapture that magic in their later career. Others gained success through so-bad-it’s-good movies that left a lasting impact on popular culture. Directors like Roland Emmerich, Jamie Babbit, and Simon West have had mixed careers, but their one truly great movie, such as Independence Day, But I’m a Cheerleader, and Con Air, stands out as a significant achievement in their filmography.

Literature Review

Despite some movie directors’ filmographies being a hit-or-miss mixed bag, they still managed to knock it out of the park with one great movie.

Some film directors have truly disappointing filmographies, however, even a stopped clock is right twice a day, and they still managed to deliver one truly great movie. From big budget blockbuster directors to small independent filmmakers, hit-or-miss directors must be commended because even if many of their films don’t reach the heights of their greatest achievements, the fact that they are willing to fail, showcased a commitment to their artistry and trying something new. Making a movie is no easy feat, and to have produced just one great film is more than most achieve in their entire lives. There are some hit-or-miss directors who started off their careers with incredible debut movies that seemed to signal them as a new major voice within Hollywood, only to never recapture the magic of that first outing and have a lackluster career moving forward. Other directors achieved greatness through a different route, by creating something that was so-bad-its-good that, despite legitimate criticisms, managed to break through and leave a last legacy on popular culture. Whatever the circumstances, it’s important to recognize those hit-or-miss directors who managed to make one truly great movie.

10 Roland Emmerich Independence Day (1996)

Independence Day

Release Date June 24, 2016 Director Roland Emmerich Cast Bill Pullman , Sela Ward , Vivica A. Fox , Jessie Usher , Charlotte Gainsbourg , Liam Hemsworth , Maika Monroe , Judd Hirsch , Brent Spiner , William Fichtner , Jeff Goldblum , Joey King Runtime 145 minutes

Director Roland Emmerich has made a name for himself making disaster movies such as 2012 and White House Down whose audience and critical reception has been mostly lackluster. However, the stars aligned in 1996 and Independence Day stood as Emmerich’s crowning achievement as a director for its strong performances, stunning visual effects, and thrilling spectacle that led to it becoming the highest-grossing movie of that year. Not only was Independence Day a major success for Emmerich, it became an important moment in the history of Hollywood and disaster movies and science fiction saw a major resurgence following its release.

9 Jamie Babbit But I’m a Cheerleader (1999)

But I’m A Cheerleader

Release Date July 7, 2000 Director Jamie Babbit Cast Natasha Lyonne , Clea DuVall , RuPaul Charles , Cathy Moriarty , Melanie Lynskey , Eddie Cibrian , Wesley Mann , Richard Moll , Douglas Spain Runtime 85 Minutes

Sadly, director Jamie Babbit never quite managed to top the achievement of her underrated LGBTQ+ coming-of-age directional debut, But I’m a Cheerleader. A satirical exploration of a woman sent into conversation therapy to “cure” her lesbianism, But I’m a Cheerleader starred Natasha Lyonne and delivered the laughs while retaining its emotional resonance. While Babbit has continued to direct feature films like The Quiet and The Stand In, nothing compared to the standard set by But I’m a Cheerleader and she found more success as a television director on series like Gilmore Girls, It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia, and Russian Doll.

8 Simon West Con Air (1997)

Con Air

Release Date June 5, 1997 Director Simon West Cast John Cusack , Dave Chappelle , Ving Rhames , John Malkovich , Steve Buscemi , Nicolas Cage Runtime 115 minutes

Simon West has directed several fun action movies, like Lara Croft: Tomb Raider and The Expendables 2, but his one truly great achievement was his directional debut Con Air. An action-packed thriller starring Nicolas Cage and John Travolta, Con Air ticked all the right boxes and has gone on to achieve a cult following for its committed performances, outrageous plot, and over-the-top spectacle. While West has had a consistent career in Hollywood since Con Air, no movie he has made since has had nearly the same impact as this debut.

7 Paul W. S. Anderson Event Horizon (1997)

Event Horizon

Release Date August 15, 1997 Director Paul W.S. Anderson Cast Jason Isaacs , Kathleen Quinlan , Sean Pertwee , Laurence Fishburne , Richard T. Jones , Joely Richardson , Sam Neill Runtime 96 minutes

The release of Event Horizon in 1997 was sadly overlooked and the film by Paul W.S. Anderson was slated by critics who did not respond to its blend of horror and science fiction. Despite this, Event Horizon has been reappraised in recent years and gained a cult following for its strong cast, use of imagery, and thrilling visuals and now stands as Anderson’s best effort as a director. Sadly, this overdue acclaim came too late, and in the years following the release of Event Horizon, Anderson has mostly directed entries in the Resident Evil film franchise to mixed levels of success.

6 Michael Cimino The Deer Hunter (1978)

The Deer Hunter

Release Date February 23, 1979 Director Michael Cimino Cast Robert De Niro , John Cazale , john savage , Meryl Streep , Christopher Walken , George Dzundza Runtime 184 Minutes

Michael Cimino showcased himself as a director of extreme potential with the release of his second film, The Deer Hunter, a Vietnam War movie starring Robert De Niro, John Cazale, and Meryl Streep. A huge success with both audiences and critics, The Deer Hunter went on to win Best Picture at the Academy Awards and, sadly, Comino never topped this extraordinary achievement. Comino’s follow-up Heaven’s Gate received universally negative reviews, although it has since been reappraised as a misunderstood classic. However, it was too late, and Cimino’s subsequent works were minor affairs with diminishing returns.

5 Mathieu Kassovitz La Haine (1995)

French director Mathieu Kassovitz found global acclaim after winning Best Director at the Cannes Film Festival for his crime thriller La Haine. A raw and captivating social commentary, La Haine was an uncompromising investigation into the social and economic divisions of Paris in the 1990s that explored class, police brutality, and race. While Kassovitz continued filmmaking after La Haine he never quite managed to recapture the depth and urgency of that film and some later releases, such as Babylon A.D. starring Vin Diesel, received an overwhelmingly negative reception.

4 Ed Wood Plan 9 From Outer Space (1957)

The low-budget science fiction director Ed Wood has been commonly referred to as the worst director of all time and even had his life and difficult film career immortalized in the 1994 Tim Burton biopic of his life, Ed Wood. An earnest, albeit unsophisticated director, Wood’s films were campy, eccentric, and messy, but, despite this, one has managed to carve out a special place for itself in popular culture. Wood’s 1957 movie Plan 9 from Outer Space has evolved a reputation as a so-bad-it’s-good film that gained a cult following due to its charming creative choices and amateurish technical effects.

3 M. Night Shyamalan The Sixth Sense (1999)

The Sixth Sense

Release Date August 6, 1999 Director M. Night Shyamalan Cast Bruce Willis , Toni Collette , Haley Joel Osment , Olivia Williams , Donnie Wahlberg Runtime 107 minutes

Whether fairly or not, director M. Night Shyamalan has often been the butt of jokes when it comes to his film career, with movies categorized by twist endings such as Unbreakable, The Village, and Signs, he has sometimes been perceived as a one-trick-pony. However, even the harsh Shyamalan critics must admit he got things right with his career-defining movie The Sixth Sense. The story of a young boy who sees dead people, The Sixth Sense was the perfect representation of Shyamalan’s unique style and established his public perception as a thriller director with a love for a good twist.

2 Richard Kelly Donnie Darko (2001)

Donnie Darko

Release Date October 26, 2001 Director Richard Kelly Cast Jake Gyllenhaal , Holmes Osborne , Maggie Gyllenhaal , Daveigh Chase , Mary McDonnell , James Duval Runtime 113 minutes

Richard Kelly’s debut Donnie Darko was an interesting psychological thriller that appeared to be deeply layered with hidden meaning and probing philosophical depth. The incredibly intriguing nature of Donnie Darko set Kelly on course to be one of the most promising young directors in Hollywood. However, since its release, Kelly failed to live up to these expectations and a director’s cut release of Donnie Darko revealed the film as less thematically rich than it initially seemed. Later works, like Southland Tales and The Box, failed to have much of an impact and Kelly has remained a one-hit-wonder.

1 Tommy Wiseau The Room (2003)

The Room

Release Date June 27, 2003 Director Tommy Wiseau Cast Juliette Danielle , Robyn Paris , Greg Sestero , Kyle Vogt , Carolyn Minnott , Tommy Wiseau , Dan Janjigian , Philip Haldiman , mike holmes Runtime 91minutes

The undisputed king of so-bad-its-good movie directors must be Tommy Wiseau and his movie The Room. A cult classic whose production was dramatized by James Franco in The Disaster Artist, there’s no denying The Room had a great impact and inspired countless watch parties, midnight showings, and a dedicated audience. Since its release, Wiseau has rebranded The Room from a failed drama to a dark comedy as every scene was remarkably stiff while remaining endlessly quotable. The Room was a special kind of failure and Wiseau’s follow-up Big Shark, a consciously made bad movie, lacked its authentic magic.
