Summary

“Masters of the Air” episode 3 depicts the real-life Regensburg mission, a risky and devastating bombing raid by the 100th Bombardment Group during WWII. The Regensburg mission aimed to destroy German factories and disrupt their production, but it resulted in heavy casualties for the American forces involved. The mission’s success in damaging German resources was limited, and the losses suffered by the 100th Bomb Group and other participating groups led to a pause in daylight bombing for several months.

During Masters of the Air episode 3, the 100th Bombardment Group goes on a dangerous long-haul mission that flies over Regensburg, Germany and ends in North Africa, and while the events of the mission may seem dramatic or incredible, the Regensburg mission did in fact happen in real life. Masters of the Air episode 3, and the rest of the war drama, tell the true story of the notoriously unlucky 100th Bombardment Group during World War II. While the first two episodes of the series provided background and context to the 100th, episode 3 finally puts them in action.

Masters of the Air episode 3 is particularly stressful as the 100th embarks on their riskiest mission yet. The beginning of the episode reveals that the men are planning to fly from their base, Thorpe Abbotts, in England, over Germany where they will drop bombs on enemy factories, then make their landing in North Africa as a bit of a vacation to confuse the Germans. However, issues arise immediately when only one group of planes is sent into the air, when there were supposed to be two. Buck, Bucky, Curtis, and Harry are all part of this life-changing mission.

What The Purpose Of The Regensburg Mission Was

The Regensburg mission depicted in Masters of the Air isn’t a work of fiction created for the series but is a real mission that the men of the 100th Bombardment Group flew on. The mission stands out both in history and in the show because of the devastating losses it resulted in. Before any damage occurred, the Regensburg mission was intended to be a massive hit against German forces. In the original plan, as noted by Air and Space Forces Magazine, 376 B-17 Flying Fortresses would drop bombs over Schweinfurt and Regensburg, targeting an aircraft factory and a ball-bearing factory.

The Regensburg plan was layered as well. Of the 376 bombers set to fly, there would be two groups. Both would take off from Thorpe Abbotts, but they would split off over Germany. One group of 230 planes would continue on to Schweinfurt and return to England, while the other 146 bombers would hit Regensburg then travel to Africa. In reality, only that second smaller group managed to take off on time due to intense cloud cover in England, and with fewer numbers, they became vulnerable to German attacks. Though Regensburg was successful, they suffered many losses in the process.

Ultimately, Schweinfurt/Regensburg, if executed as planned, was projected to be a major hit against Germany. By taking out their factories, German resources would be damaged, and as a result, all their production would slow. Targeting a ball-bearing factory was particularly strategic. Though ball-bearings seem like a strange target, the loss of them would be detrimental to the Germans. Ultimately, the Regensburg attack did hurt the Germans, but not nearly as much as the Americans hoped. In hindsight, a slightly different tactic might have made all the difference in August 1943.

How Many People Died In Masters Of The Air’s Regensburg Mission

As shown in Masters of the Air episode 3, the Regensburg mission caused many casualties and losses among the 100th. Men were both killed and forced out their damaged planes, leaving them unaccounted for and at risk of becoming POWs. Of the 146 planes that were sent on the Regensburg mission, 24 were lost. Nine of those 24 bombers belonged to the 100th Bomb Group. While some airmen went down with their planes, like Lt. Curtis Biddick, others were able to escape their B-17s before they crashed, such as Sgt. William Quinn, who landed in Belgium.

According to the American Air Museum, the Regensburg mission resulted in 38 deaths, nine wounded, 130 POWs, 16 escaped, 20 interned, and 40 returned to duty. While a handful of the dead were aboard planes that safely made it to Africa, the majority were killed due to damages to their planes. Furthermore, all the wounded made it to Africa. All in all, these losses were staggering for the 100th and the other groups that participated in the mission. Not only had men lost their lives, but entire chunks of soldiers were simply gone.

One notable loss that is clearly depicted in Masters of the Air episode 3 is that of Lieutenant Curtis Biddick. Played by Irish actor Barry Keoghan, Biddick was a real member of the 100th Bombardment Group who was ultimately killed in action during the Regensburg mission. While it would be somewhat difficult to say what happened to him in real life, the series successfully fills in the blanks, showing Biddick lose his life with the intention of saving another. Biddick’s fate is tragic, but Masters of the Air does the real soldier justice by accentuating his heroic ideals.

What Happened After Regensburg

The end goal of the Regensburg mission was to successfully take out German factories. Therefore, in some respects, the mission was a success. The 100th and accompanying planes did drop bombs on Regensburg and destroyed a German ball-bearing factory. However, the resulting losses for American forces were so catastrophic, both for the Regensburg mission and the Schweinfurt mission that occurred later, that such massive daylight bombing deep in German territory was put on pause for five months. The mission that was supposed to easily debilitate Germany hurt the Americans in equal measure.

Daylight bombing was primarily practiced by the U.S. during World War II and was thought to be a risky method of attack. While visibility was improved for the bombers, the same was true for the enemy.

That said, the Germans were definitely affected by the Schweinfurt/Regensburg mission. The bombing of Regensburg was highly destructive, while Schweinfurt was less so, but still effective in harming German forces. Though the Germans considered the raid a victory due to the number of Americans killed and their ability to recoup quickly, the mission still frightened them. It showed that the U.S. had strength and numbers in its Air Force. In fact, because Schweinfurt/Regensburg happened on the same day as the RAF’s Operation Hydra, German forces were deeply shaken going forward.

After Regensburg, members of the 100th successfully landed in North Africa. Although they found safety there, many of their planes were now useless and unable to be flown back to England. Unfortunately, the return trip was also unpleasant. According to Air and Space Forces Magazine, the Regensburg force lost three bombers on its way back to England while they attempted to make an attack in Bordeaux. Therefore, from beginning to end, the Regensburg mission, as depicted in Masters of the Air, was deeply harmful to the 100th and U.S. Army Air Forces.
