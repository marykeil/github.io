Will Smith s Sci-Fi Movie Letdown Was Ruined By Changing 1 Key Part Of The Book

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1100 words 5 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

Will Smith’s “I, Robot” movie greatly deviates from Isaac Asimov’s original book, with changes in characters and a different setting. The movie missed the opportunity to explore the expansive world of the book by confining events entirely to Chicago. Despite the differences, both the book and movie maintain the core element of Asimov’s Three Laws of Robotics.

Literature Review

This 2004 Will Smith movie was based on a book by Isaac Asimov, but the adaptation was ruined due to altering a key aspect of the story.

Of all the movies to star Will Smith, there’s one that could have been much better if it were more faithful to its source material. Will Smith’s sci-fi movies are among his most popular, and he’s starred in a variety of films in the genre over the years. He has also been in more than one book adaptation, with each project differing in levels of accuracy when compared to the original literary work. However, there’s one of Smith’s projects in particular that strayed very far from the book on which it was based.

Will Smith’s best roles have been spread across his decades-long career, with the actor becoming one of the biggest names in Hollywood. His characters tend to fall on the heroic side, saving the world in films such as 1996’s Independence Day and 1997’s Men In Black. Smith’s acting range also extends to more heart-wrenching narratives like 2008’s Seven Pounds and even anti-hero characters such as the main role in 2008’s Hancock. Out of all Will Smith’s movies, one from the mid-00s represents the biggest missed opportunity due to a misguided book change.

Robots Are Illegal On Earth In Isaac Asimov’s I, Robot Book Asimov made it so that robots featured primarily in space

Will Smith’s 2004 I, Robot movie is based on Isaac Asimov’s 1950 book of the same name. There are many huge differences between the two versions of the story, one of which is the fact that Smith’s character isn’t even in the book. The other more notable change is that robots become illegal to use on Earth in Asimov’s book other than for very specific purposes. Instead, US Robots corner what they refer to as the “Extra-Terrestrial market.” This means they sell their wares to the off-world pockets of humanity on planets such as Mercury.

The robot ban in Asimov’s book comes about due to the fact that they’re being used to replace the human workforce and are threatening the ever-shrinking job market. In the movie, the robots are shown to be a valued part of human society. Instead of being used as workers, they take on a role more similar to a butler or maid and are sold and marketed as such. None of the 2004 I, Robot movie is set in space, whereas the book features an extended look at the robots’ work on faraway planets and space stations.

Setting Will Smith’s I, Robot Movie In Space Could Have Fixed The Movie The futuristic depiction of Chicago had its limits

The I, Robot movie stripped away many of the elements that made the book so iconic. While the film did make some welcome contributions, such as updating the outdated design of Asimov’s robots, it also missed out on exploring huge areas of the author’s world. The movie could still have started on Earth but then headed to the stars in pursuit of Sonny rather than confining events entirely to Chicago. Great sci-fi action movies often have a huge scope, so this was a big missed opportunity.

In fact, the scene where Will Smith’s Del Spooner is trying to find Sonny in a swarm of identical robots is heavily inspired by a sequence from the book. One of the main differences, other than Sonny being created for the movie, is the search in the book takes place away from Earth and via a series of interviews. While some of the changes to this scene are understandable given the change in medium, most of the alterations are damaging. There are various other parts of the movie that fall short of the source material due to its altered setting.

Alan Tudyk served as the voice actor and motion capture artist for Sonny in 2004’s I, Robot movie.

I, Robot Still Keeps The Main Point Of Asimov’s Book The Three Laws of Robotics appear in both versions of the story

Asimov’s Three Laws of Robotics feature in much of his work, and they’re carried over verbatim into the movie.

Despite the disparity between Isaac Asimov’s I, Robot book and Will Smith’s movie, the core element of both stories remains intact. Asimov’s Three Laws of Robotics feature in much of his work, and they’re carried over verbatim into the movie. The Laws relate to the responsibility of robots to keep humans and themselves safe in order of priority, and they’re programmed into the robots with the intention of being airtight and borderline impossible to circumvent.

              One Failed Isaac Asimov’s Adaptation Needs A Remake (But Not As A Movie)   There is one Isaac Asimov adaptation in particular that needs revisiting, but a movie may not be the best format for it to reach its potential.

Asimov’s book places its robotic characters in intriguing scenarios that force the Three Laws to interact in unexpected ways, fully exploring the benefits and faults of the immovable rules. The movie’s approach is much less subtle. Will Smith’s I, Robot installs the Laws in all the story’s robots but also gives Sonny the ability to completely ignore them whenever he likes. So, while Asimov’s original story becomes something of a science-fiction intellectual exercise, the 2004 movie with Will Smith takes the form of something more similar to a detective tale blended with action-adventure elements.
