It s Always Sunny In Philadelphia 10 Best Frank & Charlie Episodes

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1800 words 9 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

Danny DeVito’s addition as Frank Reynolds elevated It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia into the beloved show it is today. Frank and Charlie share a unique, disturbing, yet oddly sweet bond, engaging in absurd and zany activities together. Frank and Charlie’s relationship showcases their selfishness and narcissism, while also providing moments of love and connection beneath their degeneracy.

Literature Review

It’s Always Sunny’s Frank has been stealing the show since he was first introduced in season 1, and he has the best chemistry with Charlie.

Although there were seven episodes before he came along, It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia didn’t really become the show that fans know and love today until Danny DeVito joined the cast in the role of Frank Reynolds. DeVito shares terrific on-screen chemistry with all his co-stars, but particularly Charlie Day, whose character Charlie Kelly shares a bed with Frank. These two characters exhibit the absurd selfishness and narcissism that the main characters of It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia are famous for, but when it comes to each other, there’s a strange love and connection underneath their degeneracy. In their spare time, Frank and Charlie like to boil denim, explore the sewers naked, fish for crabs in the Schuylkill River, and get involved with any number of get-rich-quick schemes. In the show, they often rope each other into their zany plans, from getting married to sleeping in a dumpster. Sometimes the two just want to play nightcrawlers, and it’s in those disturbing but oddly sweet moments of connection that make Frank and Charlie’s relationship unique and their episodesso enjoyable to watch.

It’s Always Sunny: 10 Best Dennis & Dee Episodes Dennis and Dee might be TV’s worst twins. That has made for some hilarious It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia episodes centered on the pair.

10 “Frank’s Back In Business” Season 8, Episode 7

When Frank’s old company hits hard financial times, they call upon Frank – or, more specifically, his ruthless It’s Always Sunny alter ego “The Warthog” – to return to the office in season 8’s “Frank’s Back in Business”. Much like Michael Douglas took an ambitious young Charlie Sheen under his wing in Oliver Stone’s Wall Street, Frank takes Charlie under his wing as a protégé. Charlie’s naïveté and Frank’s insistence on making a quick buck crash into each other in hilarious fashion when Charlie tries to figure out what their company makes, only for Frank to answer “money” to Charlie’s confusion. It’s both a perfect characterization of the friends and a pointed critique of capitalism.

9 “Who Pooped The Bed?” Season 4, Episode 7

A mysterious poop appears in Frank and Charlie’s bed in season 4’s “Who Pooped the Bed?” and Mac and Dennis are determined to get to the bottom of it. It’s certainly a juvenile premise, but the hilarity of the episode comes from its style. It frames the case of the bed poop like an Agatha Christie murder mystery. Charlie and Frank’s sleeping together in the same bed has always just been an accepted, albeit mocked, aspect of their friendship, but it’s funny to have an episode that focuses on some of the issues that may come with this arrangement.

8 “The Gang Gets Invincible” Season 3, Episode 2

While Mac, Dennis, and Dee are trying out for the Philadelphia Eagles in season 3’s “The Gang Gets Invincible,” Frank and Charlie hang back with the tailgaters. Charlie intends to just sit back, relax, and drink beer all day, but his plans quickly change when he realizes Frank has laced his first beer with LSD. This episode has some classic moments of Dennis thinking he’s a star athlete and a man pretending to be Donovan McNabb despite looking nothing like the Eagles’ quarterback. It’s also a good reminder that while Frank and Charlie are good friends, that doesn’t make Charlie immune from Frank’s propensity to make unilateral decisions that could have harmful effects on others.

7 “The Gang Dines Out” Season 8, Episode 9

The Gang’s power dynamic is laid bare in season 8’s “The Gang Dines Out.” Frank and Charlie’s dinner reservation at Gugino’s happens to coincide with that of Mac and Dennis, and they spend the whole episode deciding who’s going to “pay tribute” to the other table. While Mac and Charlie catching each other’s eye across the restaurant is the episode’s greatest moment, Frank wearing an obvious toupee is nearly as funny. If nothing else, this episode shows that Charlie and Frank have as strong a connection in-universe as it appears to viewers. “The Gang Dines Out” gives quick reminders of why pairing the characters is fun, but it’s when their storylines come together at the end that reveals why It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia is at its best when Frank and the gang are together.

It’s Always Sunny: 10 Episodes That’ll Never Get Old It’s Always Sunny In Philadelphia has been on the air for over a decade, and yet there are still some episodes that will never get old.

6 “Mac & Dennis Break Up” Season 5, Episode 9

When Dee points out that Mac and Dennis have a co-dependent relationship, they decide to take a break from each other in season 5’s “Mac & Dennis Break Up.” Mac moves in with Frank and Charlie and instantly drives them up the wall with his bossiness. Frank refuses to stop cutting his toenails with a steak knife or sharing a bed with Charlie. This episode revealed that Frank and Charlie refer to themselves as the “Gruesome Twosome” and reveals why the pair are perfectly suited for each other in a way no other It’s Always Sunny characters’ relationships can mimic.

5 “The Gang Goes To A Water Park” Season 12, Episode 2

Season 12’s “The Gang Goes to a Water Park” is one of It’s Always Sunny’s all-time funniest episodes, as everybody has a hilarious storyline. Mac and Dee are stuck in a slide with a bunch of kids and Dennis takes on a protégé in the art of swindling. Meanwhile, Frank and Charlie are determined to ride all the rides before they leave, so they fake a terminal illness to skip the lines. It’s ridiculous, dark, and grimly funny. Confident in their con, they decide they can go on the new Thunder Gun ride that isn’t even open yet. Undeterred, they pour a bottle of water down the slide, thinking it’ll suffice, and then Frank promptly cuts up his back, sliding down the slide. Despite nominally being the most “pure” pairing of characters in It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia, Frank and Charlie are not above lying and dirty tactics to get what they want, inevitably suffering for being scoundrels.

4 “Frank’s Pretty Woman” Season 7, Episode 1

Frank wants to marry his favorite sex worker in the season 7 premiere, “Frank’s Pretty Woman”, so Charlie tries to prove to his sleeping partner that he’s capable of finding love based on his own merits. While Charlie has sweet intentions in trying to raise Frank’s self-worth, he goes about it horribly. Charlie’s understanding of relationships has always been strange, his fixation on the Waitress being a prime example, and this episode puts that naïveté to the test. Despite the vulgarity and crassness of the episode, it shows an impressive understanding of sex work. Frank likes who he likes, and he doesn’t apologize for it, even if Charlie sees it as “wrong”. The episode posits sex work as a legitimate profession, making it clear it’s Franks’ specific choice of woman that is the issue, not her chosen profession.

It’s Always Sunny In Philadelphia: 9 Sitcom Tropes Subverted By The Show
It’s Always Sunny In Philadelphia is one of the most unique shows on TV and it became that by avoiding tropes often seen in other sitcoms.

3 “Mac Fights Gay Marriage” Season 6, Episode 1

In the season 6 premiere, “Mac Fights Gay Marriage”, Dennis decides to settle down and gets in touch with his old high school girlfriend, Maureen Ponderosa. Dennis marries her impulsively and grows to regret it almost instantly. Meanwhile, instead of continuing to crack each other’s backs because Charlie can’t afford a chiropractor, Frank and Charlie get married for the government benefits. They soon fall into the same pitfalls as many married couples. Their realization that their close domestic partnership is in many ways just like a marriage is a funny and revealing insight into Frank and Charlie’s relationship. When they have to be handheld through what a gay marriage entails, learning that it’s just a normal marriage between two men, It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia makes a point about how people can overthink queer relationships.

2 “Dennis Gets Divorced” Season 6, Episode 2

Just as Dennis’ marriage comes to an unceremonious end one episode later, so does Frank and Charlie’s. After realizing they were happier before they got married, they decide to go back to the old arrangement. Uncle Jack handles their divorce proceedings, resulting in one of the episode’s funniest scenes as Frank and Charlie bicker over who will get “a series of drawings of elephants”. The two sitting across from each other slowly realizing they like each other despite how much they’ve fought all episode is a perfect encapsulation of their relationship.

1 The Gang Finds A Dumpster Baby Season 3, Episode 1

While Mac and Dee are trying to turn the titular dumpster baby into a star in the season 3 premiere, “The Gang Finds a Dumpster Baby”, Charlie and Frank become dumpster divers. They fill their apartment with “treasures” they’ve recovered from dumpsters, then end up sleeping in one when they run out of space in their apartment. This episode provided one of the first hints that Frank could be involved in Charlie’s mysterious parentage. It’s one of the early episodes of the series, but it shows why Frank and Charlie are one of the best duos in It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia. They both have the same interests and a powerful belief that whatever scheme they’ve cooked up will be successful, even when it’s failed so badly that they’re sleeping in a dumpster together. “The Gang Finds a Dumpster Baby” is a look at what’s to come for the grimy, industrious, and simpatico friends.
