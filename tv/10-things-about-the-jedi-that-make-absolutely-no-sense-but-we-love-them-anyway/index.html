10 Things About The Jedi That Make Absolutely No Sense (But We Love Them Anyway)

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 2000 words 10 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

The Jedi Order may be great heroes, but not everything makes sense about them. The Jedi Code was enforced inconsistently, which seems odd. The Jedi, who are supposed to be peacekeepers, immediately become soldiers in the Clone Wars.

Literature Review

Although the Jedi are undoubtedly some of Star Wars’ most powerful figures, there are several things about them that make very little sense.

While the Jedi are no doubt Star Wars’ most iconic figures, they are also frequently contradictory and confusing, and several things about them make no sense. The origins of the Jedi date back, as Luke Skywalker said in Star Wars: The Last Jedi, “A thousand generations,” but age does not always equal wisdom. By the time of the prequel trilogy’s Jedi Order, plenty of inconsistencies and even flaws had developed with the Jedi. Several upcoming Star Wars movies indicate that the franchise is reassessing the role of the Jedi in the galaxy and what their self-imposed rules should be. Updates on Rey’s new Jedi Order movie in particular suggest that Star Wars will be throwing into sharp relief what the Jedi Order’s mistakes were in the past. The Jedi will surely always remain beloved, but these 10 nonsensical aspects of the prequel trilogy’s Jedi Order are undeniable.

How To Watch Star Wars Movies In Order
Here is how to watch all Star Wars movies and TV shows chronologically and in order of release, and how each fits into the Star Wars timeline.

10 Even A Child Is Wiser Than A Jedi Archivist Star Wars: Episode II - Attack of the Clones

Obi-Wan Kenobi hunts for information about the lost planet Kamino in Star Wars: Episode II - Attack of the Clones, and approaches Jedi Archivist Jocasta Nu. When Obi-Wan explains that the Jedi archives have no record of the planet, however, Jocasta abruptly tells him that if it’s not in their records, it simply doesn’t exist. This interaction would be odd enough, given an archivist should surely know there is always more to learn. The shutdown makes Jocasta Nu rather suspicious, but she’s intended to only be curt. What makes this exchange even more confounding is that a Jedi Archivist, a Jedi Grand Master (the highest Jedi rank in Star Wars), and one of the most powerful Jedi cannot figure out why this planet may be missing. Instead, when Obi-Wan brings the problem to Master Yoda, Yoda turns the question back around to a group of younglings. It’s only then that one of the children says it could be missing because someone removed it from the archive memory, which Obi-Wan and Yoda both respond to as though it’s a stroke of genius rather than a fairly obvious explanation.

9 The Jedi (Initially) Treated The Clones Like Droids Star Wars: The Clone Wars

Once Obi-Wan does track down Kamino and uncover the clone army being made for the Republic, the Jedi accept the clones’ existence and the Jedi’s right to use them much too quickly. Even more than the alarming nonchalance with which they accept a mysterious army apparently made for them, though, is the fact that the Jedi then go on to “employ” the clones — if it can be called that — in the Clone Wars. Rather than the Jedi leading with compassion and respecting all life, they embrace the artificial creation of living beings who fight and die for them. It is true that the advent of the Clone Wars require immediate action. Still, the willingness of the Jedi to permit the clones to be created just to serve in the Republic Army and follow orders as soldiers seems entirely contradictory to who the Jedi are meant to be. Worse still, it takes time for the Jedi to really begin to understand clone individuality and the fact their deaths are as tragic as any other being’s.

8 Despite Being “Peacekeepers,” The Jedi Mobilized As Soldiers Immediately Star Wars: Episode II - Attack of the Clones

Coinciding with the Jedi’s acceptance of a clone army is their immediate engagement in the Clone Wars themselves. When the Jedi come to the rescue of Anakin, Padmé, and Obi-Wan Kenobi on Geonosis in Attack of the Clones, they do not hesitate to engage in battle, and, when Yoda rather bluntly says “Begun, the Clone War has,” he makes it clear that the Jedi will be participating. The speed with which the Jedi become soldiers is quite staggering. As Ahsoka Tano states directly in the Ahsoka show, she was raised to be a Jedi, not a soldier. Her training as a youngling in no way prepared her to fight in a war. Nevertheless, despite all Jedi younglings up to that point presumably receiving the same or at least very similar training, the Jedi (even Padawans) seem confusingly prepared to enter a war, with many becoming generals even as teenagers, such as Anakin Skywalker. The Jedi’s ability to transition for peace-keeping Jedi to full-fledged soldiers is one of the strangest Jedi plot points.

7 Mourning Is Against The Jedi Code Star Wars: Episode III - Revenge of the Sith

The Jedi rule against mourning is perplexing, perhaps even more so than their rule against attachments. In Star Wars: Episode III - Revenge of the Sith, when Anakin goes to Master Yoda with fears over losing someone he loves, Yoda tells Anakin, “Mourn them do not; miss them do not.” This suggests that the Jedi approach to death is not to mourn. This is supported at multiple points during The Clone Wars show as well, including when multiple Jedi die in a bombing of the Temple and when Master Luminara says she is willing to accept the death of her Padawan, Barriss Offee. However, this rule is not set in stone. In the canon Star Wars novel Dark Disciple, written by Christie Golden, maverick Jedi Master Quinlan Vos explicitly says that Jedi can grieve. This makes the rule unclear, but the notion that Jedi shouldn’t mourn, as Yoda indicates in Revenge of the Sith, is most perplexing. The Jedi are meant to be compassionate and one with the Force. While it’s surely bittersweet for someone to join the living Force, it makes no sense that Yoda’s policy would essentially boil down to forgetting someone once they’ve died. 6 The Jedi Play Fast & Loose With Their Own Rules Star Wars: Episode III - Revenge of the Sith

The issue with the inconsistent rule about mourning is symptomatic of a larger problem with the Jedi Code; that the Jedi themselves don’t seem to always follow their own rules or at least follow them in the same way. In the same novel, Dark Disciple, Mace Windu, an extremely respected Jedi master, breaks one of the Jedi’s key philosophies. The Jedi are not supposed to kill unarmed opponents, meaning that an outright murder plot is surely against the rules. Despite that, Windu advocates for the Jedi Council to sanction the assassination of Count Dooku. Alarmingly, the Jedi Council give Windu very little pushback and, ultimately, all agree that Count Dooku must die. Of course, part of this is not so much inconsistency with the Jedi; this mainly stems from the fact that during the Clone Wars, the Jedi lost themselves, and that contributed to their downfall. Unfortunately, that is made most apparent when Anakin loses all faith in the Jedi after witnessing Mace Windu about to kill an unarmed Palpatine in Star Wars: Episode III - Revenge of the Sith, leading him to side with Palpatine and turn to the dark side of the Force.

5 Force Sensitivity Is Hereditary, But The Jedi Don’t Have Children Star Wars: Episode III - Revenge of the Sith

Although Anakin clearly demonstrates there can be serious pitfalls to Jedi having attachments, he’s also proof that the Jedi are cutting themselves off at the knees by refusing to allow romantic relationships among their ranks. Anakin’s obsession with Padmé leads to the destruction of the Jedi, but his attachment is also the very reason the Jedi carry on, as his son, Luke Skywalker, is extremely strong with the Force. Given how hereditary Force-sensitivity is, so much so that it passes down even to Anakin’s grandson Ben Solo, it’s odd that the Jedi would make having children forbidden.

4 Obi-Wan Says Only A Sith Deals In Absolutes, Yet The Jedi Say No Attachments Star Wars: Episode III - Revenge of the Sith

One of the most confounding lines in all of Star Wars is Obi-Wan Kenobi’s insistence in Revenge of the Sith that “only a Sith deals in absolutes.” This line, arguably an absolute itself, seems outright incorrect. The very fact that the Jedi Order forbid entire aspects of human nature (namely, romance and attachment), suggests that they have many principles they are unwaveringly black-and-white about. Perhaps Obi-Wan was caught up in the moment, witnessing the fall of his former Padawan to the dark side of the Force, but if this is an actual Jedi tenet, it’s a remarkably confusing one.

3 Yoda & Obi-Wan Back-Peddle On The Most Important Jedi Rule Star Wars: Episode III - Revenge of the Sith

From the moment Anakin Skywalker arrives on Coruscant in Star Wars: Episode I - The Phantom Menace, the Jedi are concerned about his age; at nine years old, Anakin had time to grow attachments to his family, which made him a risk. Despite not only that perception but also their worries coming to pass, when the Jedi Order falls, Obi-Wan and Yoda explicitly decide to send Luke to his family. This is particularly odd because they intended Luke to be the future of the Jedi; it’s unclear why they immediately went back on a foundational rule.

2 Why Didn’t Obi-Wan & Yoda Change Luke & Leia’s Names? Star Wars: Episode III - Revenge of the Sith

Yoda and Obi-Wan go to a lot of trouble to make sure the twins are hidden and kept safe during the Imperial Era to ensure that Darth Vader never learns of their existence. Despite the great lengths they went to, including separating the two of them and Obi-Wan committing to a life of exile and solitude, they failed to take the most basic precautions. When Padmé gives birth, just before she dies, she names both Luke and Leia; Yoda and Obi-Wan keep these names. While Anakin wasn’t there, had the two ever discussed baby names, a ‘Luke Skywalker’ would have been a fairly immediate alarm bell, particularly given he was being raised on the Lars’ farm. It was an odd choice for Obi-Wan and Yoda, who could have kept Padmé’s wishes but concealed the names until a later time. Even from a Star Wars chronology standpoint, this made little sense; nothing in the original trilogy indicated that Padmé had named them, so this was a wrinkle the prequels didn’t have to add.

1 Jedi Cut Off Connections To Their Families… But Keep Their Family Names Star Wars: Episode I - The Phantom Menace

Despite the Jedi being so resolute in their rule against attachments, they inexplicably allowed the members of the Jedi Order to keep a key connection to their families. Each of the Jedi retained their last (or family) name, tethering them to where and who they came from. Given how concerned the Jedi were with the dangerous influence of family attachments, this is perhaps the most confusing Jedi choice in Star Wars; the Jedi could have done away with last names or adopted some sort of universal last name.
