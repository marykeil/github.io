How Luke Evans Pinocchio Character Compares To His Other Disney Villain

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1600 words 8 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

The Coachman in Pinocchio employs magic and turns children into donkeys, while Gaston in Beauty and the Beast rejects the idea of magic and relies on physical strength. The Coachman is portrayed as a more sinister and evil character than Gaston, with the Coachman abducting children and turning them into donkeys to work in his salt mine. Gaston presents a bigger threat to the heroes in Beauty and the Beast and leads a mob against the Beast, while Pinocchio could have easily avoided the Coachman with enough care.

Literature Review

Luke Evans appears in Pinocchio as the Coachman character and he also stars in Beauty and the Beast as Gaston. How do the two villains compare?

Luke Evans appears in Pinocchio as the villainous Coachman and this is not the first time he’s played a villain in a live-action Disney movie. Pinocchio is another in a long line of Disney live-action remakes, a remake of the 1940 animated movie, which was originally an 1883 Italian children’s novel. In the 2022 remake, directed by Robert Zemeckis, an animated Pinocchio, voiced by Benjamin Evan Ainsworth, is joined by live-action and animated characters from the original Pinocchio story. Luke Evans also joins the cast as the Coachman, the man who kidnaps Pinocchio, and takes him to Pleasure Island.

Luke Evans has had a unique career. In the early 2000s, he was primarily working in theater. It wasn’t until 2010 that he was cast in a notable film role, as Apollo in Clash of the Titans. A year later, he had two memorable, though unsuccessful, starring performances as Zeus in Immortals and Aramis in The Three Musketeers. Since then, Luke Evans has had a slew of underrated roles and performances in major franchises, including as Gaston in the 2017 live-action Beauty and the Beast remake. It’s worth comparing Evans’ Gaston to his other Disney villain, Pinocchio’s the Coachman.

Gaston And The Coachman Have Different Views On Magic The Coachman Is More Mystical Than Gaston

Gaston and the Coachman have very different views on magic. Almost every Disney movie has some sort of magic, but stories like Pinocchio and Beauty and the Beast feature a more fairytale kind of magic than something like Encanto or Frozen where the magic is almost superhero-like in quality, and the magical systems are more clearly explained. Evans’ villains have different relationships with magic in their respective worlds. Gaston seems turned off by the very idea of magic. When he sees the monstrous Beast in the mirror, he immediately resolves to kill it.

The Coachman, on the other hand, seems to be an employer of magic. He turns wayward children into donkeys on his island and forces them to do hard labor. Additionally, in a change from the animated Pinocchio to the live-action version, the Coachman employs magical creatures to do his bidding. In the animated version, his helpers are shrouded in shadow, but in the live-action film, they appear as smoky figures with glowing yellow eyes. Gaston would never associate with this type of sorcery,preferring to rely on physical strength, as his diet of five dozen eggs a morning suggests.

The Coachman Is More Evil Than Gaston Gaston Is Not As Inherently Sinister As The Coachman

The Coachman in Pinocchio is presented as a much more sinister force than Gaston, even taking into account the depths Gaston falls to at the end of Beauty and the Beast. Gaston is arrogant, mean-spirited, obnoxious, vain, and entitled. Despite everything wrong with the villain, he is still not as evil as the Coachman. The Coachman is almost devilish in how he looks, how he entices people to join them, and what happens to the children he does invite to his island, which is anything but a “pleasure”.

There is a reason Pinocchio is considered one of Disney’s scariest animated movies, and that’s in part because of the Coachman. The live-action adaptation respects this legacy with an equally cruel and dangerous version of the character. In both movies, the Coachman captures impressionable young children and turns them into donkeys to work endless hours in his salt mine. Everyone may unfortunately know someone like Gaston, but he’s nowhere as evil as the Coachman.

While acknowledging that Coachman is evil, Evans does point out that the Pinocchio villain isn’t entirely at fault. Evans talked about the character’s proclivity to abduct in an interview,

“But as far as The Coachman is concerned, they deserve to be turned into donkeys.”

It seems that in Evans’ mind, the Coachman doesn’t consider himself the villain.

Gaston Is A Bigger Threat Than The Coachman Gaston Is More Dangerous To The Heroes Than The Coachman

Despite the Coachman being objectively more evil than Gaston, there is no doubt that Gaston is one of the best villains in the Disney canon. He presents a bigger threat to Belle and the Beast than the Coachman does to Pinocchio. The Coachman takes advantage of children, using his guile to trick them into coming back to Pleasure Island. However, with enough care, Pinocchio could have easily avoided the Coachman and he manages to escape from him once on the island. Gaston presents something even more threatening in Beauty and the Beast.

Gaston manages to whip up the fury of a town and turns the citizens into a mob by fear-mongering, turning neighbors against neighbors, and using the threat of the unknown Beast and his castle to turn the townsfolk’s fear into hate. Gaston leads the mob against the Beast and his servants and almost succeeds in killing the Beast. While the Coachman may be a more frightening figure, Gaston represents a real kind of evil that is powerful and has many real-world parallels.

Evans Disappears Into His Pinocchio Role Luke Evans Is Unrecognizable As The Coachman

As Gaston, Luke Evans barely has to make any changes to his appearance beyond putting on the costume. With dark hair, a chiseled chin, and a classically handsome look, Evans is an easy match for the Gaston of the animated film. With the Coachman, however, Evans completely disappears into his role, as was his intention. In an interview with Black Film and TV, Evans describes what he told Zemeckis, the director, when they thought about what the character should be,

The first thing I said to Robert Zemeckis was that he [the Coachman] can’t be anything like Gaston. I mean we can’t have colors that remind people of Gaston, or he can’t look like Gaston. In a way, I would rather disappear completely into this character with costume hair and makeup and that’s sort of what we did to the point that I think I’m sort of unrecognizable as this Coachman character.

Evans has leading man looks, so a lot of work had to go into the design of the Coachman to make him look evil and twisted enough that audiences would credibly believe he was abducting children. Zemeckis and Evans were happy with the end result, with Evan telling ScreenRant,

“And before we knew it, we’d created this creature of the night who makes me look almost unrecognizable, which I loved. It was a joy to have such a brilliant, talented costume designer and makeup artists and hair [stylists] to create this thing. That’s what I got to use, and it was a wonderful tool that I totally made the most of.”

Evans rarely hides in makeup and costume for his performances, so that he does it this effectively in Pinocchio is a sign of his natural talent as an actor, no matter who he’s playing.

Gaston Is More Important To The Story The Coachman Is A Supporting Player While Gaston Is A Main Character

The last major difference between Luke Evans’ characters in Pinocchio and Beauty and the Beast is that Gaston is much more critical to the plot than the Coachman is. The Coachman is a significant character in that he drags Pinocchio away from Geppetto and sends the puppetmaker after his son. Once Pinocchio leaves Pleasure Island, however, the Coachman disappears from the story. Gaston, on the other hand, is central to Beauty and the Beast.

He appears at the beginning of the movie, trying to woo Belle, and he’s present throughout the rest of the film, growing increasingly bitter about Belle denying his advances. It’s Gaston who leads the charge against the Beast once he discovers Belle has feelings for the creature. At the end of the film, Gaston nearly kills the Beast before perishing himself. Without the Coachman, Pinocchio loses an interesting character. Without Gaston, Beauty and the Beast ceases to be an interesting story.
