New Crunchyroll Romance Anime Isn t Just Great, It Explains Something Few Understand

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 900 words 4 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

A Sign of Affection is an important anime that explores sign language and highlights the differences between American Sign Language (ASL) and Japanese Sign Language (JSL). The series accurately depicts JSL and showcases cultural differences, such as mouth movements and fingerspelling, which are more common in JSL than ASL. The anime raises awareness about deaf culture and the importance of accurate representation, demonstrating the significant differences that exist in sign languages.

Literature Review

A Sign of Affection, Crunchyroll’s new romance, has given fans the cutest shojo in years, as well as insight on how different sign language can be.

The latest shojo romance anime from Crunchyroll has proven that it’s not just great, but also an important show that explains something very few people understand when it comes to sign language. A Sign of Affection, based on the original manga by artist-writer duo suu Morishita, hit the ground running as the first must-see anime of 2024. The dynamic between its two main characters, Yuki and Itsuomi, has made them one of the best anime couples in recent years, but the subject of the series makes it go far beyond that with its subtle yet clever details.

The big hook of the series is that Yuki, the main character, is a deaf woman who has fallen for Itsuomi, a fellow student with a passion for learning languages. It’s an interesting premise that gives the story a fascinating way of exploring their different character dynamics through a blossoming romance. However, eagle-eyed viewers will notice that something seems a bit different about the way Yuki signs. The simplest greetings and phrases that some may have casually learned growing up are either slightly or dramatically different from how Yuki signs them.

This change in signs actually has a fairly simple yet consistently overlooked reason: Japanese Sign Language (JSL) is a completely different language from American Sign Language (ASL).

A Sign of Affection Emphasizes How Different ASL & JSL Are Produced by Ajia-do Animation Works, based on the manga by suu Morishita

There is no universal language for those that sign. Just like how varying languages can be radically different from one another depending on their family and community, ASL and JSL are completely different with varying ways to sign things that are different from how the American deaf community may sign. For example, one of the most common phrases in A Sign of Affection is “Thank You”, as Yuki shows her respect to Itsuomi for doing small things for her. In ASL, “Thank You” tends to be signed by placing the right hand flattened against the chin, then extending it outward. In JSL, however, the signer instead lays their left hand horizontally, places their right hand vertically on it and then raises it upward while slightly bowing their head, similar to raising a knife to cut a vegetable on a cutting board.

A Sign of Affection is adamant about getting these signs accurate to not just be true to what JSL is like but also be accessible to people that know this language. However, American Sign Language users won’t completely understand these signs, because they’re being used in a language completely different from the one they learned. This just shows that even though someone knows how to use ASL does not mean they will be capable of communicating with every person that knows how to sign.

A Sign of Affection Features Cultural Differences in Japanese Sign Language

Something else that really stands out about A Sign of Affection is how it integrates various cultural differences that are more commonly used in JSL than ASL. According to the website Start ASL, it’s more common for Japanese signers to mouth their words than American signers. This allows those that are signing to “distinguish between various signs.” (Start ASL, 2021.) A Sign of Affection features this constantly whenever Yuki is consistently signing without using a phone to communicate with her friends that don’t know JSL. However, something that’s also used more is fingerspelling. This allows users to spell out more complicated words with ease, something that, “is used more in JSL than it is in ASL,” (Start ASL, 2021.) and Itsuomi actually puts this in action as he learns to sign more complicated phrases from Yuki.

In the end, A Sign of Affection has been doing wonders in opening the world up to deaf culture, similar to how A Silent Voice did the same for anime fans when it released. suu Morishita has actually stated that the pair spoke with deaf people to ensure they got the signing culture and movements right to create a story with important representation. The lengths Crunchyroll’s newest romance has gone to accurately depict JSL from the manga has not only made the series great but also brings to light just how different signs can be.
