6 Real-Life Fighting Styles To Expect In Netflix s Avatar The Last Airbender Season 1

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1500 words 7 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

The live-action remake of Avatar: The Last Airbender will feature six real-life martial arts, contributing to its impressive portrayal of bending. Each bending type is based on a different martial art, such as Tai Chi for waterbending, Hung Ga for earthbending, and Northern Shaolin Kung Fu for firebending. The use of martial arts like Baguazhang for airbending and Tessenjutsu for the Kyoshi Warriors adds depth and excitement to the show’s combat scenes.

Literature Review

Netflix’s live-action remake of Avatar: The Last Airbender is expected to feature aspects of six real-life martial art forms in its first season.

Netflix’s live-action remake of Avatar: The Last Airbender is expected to feature six real-life martial arts in season 1. Excitement for the series is gaining steam as Avatar: The Last Airbender’s release date inches closer, and there are plenty of things driving up the anticipation. The show’s dedicated fandom has had mostly positive responses to the various updates on the show, and importantly, the response to the show’s portrayal of bending has been widely praised. The positive responses to Avatar: The Last Airbender’s live-action bending come in part from similarities between it and the bending sequences from the original show. The anime used a different martial art to form the basis of each type of bending, and it appears Netflix’s remake is following that model as closely as possible. This means Avatar: The Last Airbender’s cast will be demonstrating an impressive array of martial art forms, especially considering there are two more real-life fighting styles that don’t involve bending, bringing the grand total up to six.

6 Tai Chi — Waterbending Tai Chi focuses on fluidity and deliberate movement

Tai Chi is one of the most common types of martial arts to expect in Avatar: The Last Airbender season 1 because it forms the basis of waterbending. Water is the element of change, constantly moving and adapting to fill whatever space is available to it. This idea is reflected in the fluid, purposeful movements of Tai Chi. Though some of The Last Airbender’s most powerful benders are waterbenders, water is not an element of brute strength. Instead, waterbending is like Tai Chi, requiring careful control and discipline to fully master. This ancient Chinese practice is defined by slow, intentional movements that help build nuanced control of the body and relax the mind. Indeed, Tai Chi is used as a form of meditation as often as it’s used for exercise or self-defense. The way the relaxing fluidity of Tai Chi’s motions is so perfectly suited to the philosophy behind waterbending speaks to the depth and quality of Avatar: The Last Airbender’s worldbuilding. Avatar: The Last Airbender season 1 will consist of eight episodes, streaming exclusively on Netflix on February 22.

5 Hung Ga — Earthbending Hung Ga makes use of strong stances and hand movements

Hung Ga, the inspiration for earthbending, is another martial art to expect in Avatar: The Last Airbender season 1. According to Uncle Iroh in the original show, earth is the element of substance. He describes the people of the Earth Kingdom as diverse, strong, persistent, and enduring. It’s no coincidence that these are the very same traits required to become a master of Hung Ga. Hailing from southern China, Hung Ga is characterized by strong stances and hand techniques, many of which can be seen watching earthbenders in Avatar: The Last Airbender. In the traditional form of Hung Ga, beginners could spend years simply learning the basic stances, often maintaining them for hours at a time to strengthen the required muscles and perfect the technique. This clearly requires formidable strength, endurance, and persistence, showing that Hung Ga is the perfect martial art for the people of the Earth Kingdom.

4 Northern Shaolin Kung Fu — Firebending Northern Shaolin kung fu often uses powerful kicks and spinning blocks

Firebending in Avatar: The Last Airbender is based on Northern Shaolin kung fu, making this martial art one of the most prominent in the series. Originating in Northern China, this style of kung fu is known for leaping kicks; spinning blocks/parries; and quick, aggressive attacks. These techniques can often be seen in the original show, and seeing them in live action is one of the most exciting things about Avatar: The Last Airbender season 1. Firebenders are some of the most ruthless in the show’s world, and the aggressive Northern Shaolin kung fu style fits their often hot-headed nature well. Zuko, someone who bends purely through rage at the beginning, is a perfect example of how this fighting style makes sense as the basis for firebending. Its characteristic leaps and other acrobatics should be stunning to see in live action as well, which could be important considering how much firebending will feature in Avatar: The Last Airbender.

3 Baguazhang — Airbending Baguazhang is known for its focus on circular movement

Baguazhang is another chinese martial art that will appear in Netflix’s Avatar: The Last Airbender season 1. Baguazhang makes up the basis of airbending, and it is characterized by circular movement. Practitioners of Baguazhang often walk in a circle while executing forms, which is something Aang often does in the original Avatar: The Last Airbender. Baguazhang uses its flowing, circular forms to evade opponents, allowing a skilled practitioner to spar with multiple foes. Again, Aang is highly evasive and routinely fighting against greater numbers. Another similarity between Baguazhang and airbending is that practitioners of Baguazhang are typically proficient with a variety of weapons, including a staff. Staffs are the most commonly used weapons of the Air Nomads, and Aang uses his staff as his primary weapon throughout Avatar: The Last Airbender. Since all the airbenders besides Aang are murdered long before the events in Netflix’s Avatar: The Last Airbender season 1, it’s possible Aang will be the only one to perform Baguazhang forms in the show, but it’s also possible that a flashback shows a few other airbenders in action.

2 Tessenjutsu — Kyoshi Warrior Technique Tessenjutsu is practiced with an iron war fan

Tessenjutsu is one of the most fascinating martial arts expected to appear in Avatar: The Last Airbender season 1. Tessenjutsu is the art of the Japanese war fan, and it will feature in Netflix’s The Last Airbender remake via the Kyoshi Warriors. This martial art is centered around the use of iron Japanese war fans, which were typically held together by several wooden ribs. The fans could be used for deflecting the weapons of assailants, or for attacking head-on. The Kyoshi Warriors are some of the most exciting side characters expected to appear in Avatar: The Last Airbender season 1, and their tessenjutsu style should be one of the most entertaining forms of combat on the show. It takes incredible agility and reflexes to successfully use tessenjutsu against an opponent with a sword or similarly large weapon, so The Last Airbender will have to choreograph those sequences carefully. If it does, tessenjutsu could be one of the most eye-popping fighting styles in the show.

1 Dim Mak “Touch of Death” — Chi-Blocking Dim mak focuses on striking pressure points

Dim mak, or the Touch of Death, is one of the most mysterious martial arts to expect in Avatar: The Last Airbender. Its historical existence is something of a controversy, but there are certainly people today who use some principles of the ancient concept in modern martial art forms. The basic idea is to block opponents’ pressure points with extremely precise blows, thereby incapacitating them to some degree. Some even claim it could be used to strike one perfectly fatal blow with minimal force. The clear parallel to dim mak in Avatar: The Last Airbender is the chi-blocking style of fighting used by Azula’s friend, Ty Lee. Ty Lee fights her opponents by hitting them quickly and precisely on pressure points that block their chi, temporarily preventing them from bending and often causing temporary paralysis. Ty Lee’s acrobatic chi-blocking style should be impressive in live-action, and it will make her one of the most dangerous characters in Avatar: The Last Airbender season 1.
