10 Clichéd Movie Tropes Hollywood Needs To Retire Already

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1400 words 7 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

The clichéd movie trope of the ticking clock diminishes storytelling and relies on predictable suspense. It’s time for Hollywood to find more creative ways to build tension. The unrealistic plot device of someone unexpectedly standing behind a character strains credibility and insults viewer intelligence. Filmmakers should move away from this contrived humor. The action movie trope of characters firing rounds without reloading compromises narrative integrity and overlooks the necessity for resourceful solutions. Writers must abandon this unrealistic trope and acknowledge basic firearms mechanics.

Literature Review

Overused film tropes have become clichés; it’s time to retire them, making space for more creative and thoughtful storytelling in cinema.

While tropes are common in many films across genres, some have been so excessively used that they’ve evolved into clichés, suggesting it’s time to retire them from cinema altogether. A cliché is an overused expression that has lost its impact due to repeated use. Once powerful plot devices, like a stalled car engine building suspense, they now elicit eye rolls rather than engagement from savvy audiences. The elements of surprise and imagination have been drained away through tired repetition. It’s time for Hollywood to retire these stale tricks and rejuvenate its creative spirit. There are several movie tropes that deserve retirement, ranging from the comically predictable to the lazily absurd. The goal is not to criticize creativity, but to encourage a challenge. This is a call for writers and directors to move beyond formulaic fallbacks and tap into the original sources that sparked intrigue, fear, joy, whatever authentic emotional response defines the story’s essence. The byproduct of creativity without constraints is originality; and from originality, great films are born.

10 The Ticking Clock Used to manufacture cheap tension

The clichéd movie trope of the ticking clock, usually employed in action films, has become an overused crutch that diminishes the art of storytelling. The literal ticking time bomb to emphasize stakes is tired and unimaginative, betraying a lack of confidence in the narrative’s intrinsic drama. Hollywood should retire this trope, as it often results in predictable suspense, relying on mundane audio cues rather than crafting authentic and nuanced stakes. It’s time to let go of this tired device and seek more creative and compelling ways to build tension in cinematic storytelling.

9 “They’re Behind Me, Aren’t They?” An unrealistic plot device

The cliché of a character insulting someone only to find that person unexpectedly standing behind them is an absurd, unrealistic plot device that primarily serves overly contrived humor. As seen to formulaic effect in comedy films and sitcoms, this trite reveal strains credibility by relying on an improbable coincidence played for awkwardness. Beyond lacking originality, it lazily manufactures canned embarrassment via transparent situations rarely occurring in reality. Unless actively parodying overused premises, filmmakers should allow this bit to fade away rather than insult viewer intelligence with a scenario exceeding the bounds of believability.

8 Guns With Infinite Ammo An action movie mainstay that needs to end

The unrealistic action movie trope where characters continuously fire rounds without reloading needs to be discarded. An example of this occurs in The Walking Dead character Hershel defends his farm from zombies, ignoring the realistic constraints of ammunition. This not only compromises narrative integrity to sidestep logistical inconveniences, but also fosters laziness by overlooking the necessity for resourceful solutions in dangerous conflicts. Such oversight breaks immersion and should be abandoned indefinitely. Audiences deserve stories that acknowledge their basic understanding of firearms mechanics. Writers must abandon this unrealistic trope, which replaces authentic stakes with a crude and contrived infinite ammo cartridge.

7 It’s Just A Cat A lazy attempt at a jump scare

The jump scare reveal of “it’s just a cat” is an overused and ineffective cliché commonly seen in horror films. This trope attempts to build tension through implied danger, only to comically deflate it by presenting an innocuous feline. After the umpteenth time, the moment loses its impact and comes across as lazy and ultimately ineffective writing. Audiences deserve more creativity and authentic scares. Retiring this played-out cat trope would motivate writers to craft original moments that genuinely unsettle viewers. Horror cinema should explore new territory rather than recycle cheap tricks at the expense of authenticity.

6 Cutting Hair After A Traumatic Moment An overused and sexist trope

The trope of a female character cutting her hair to signify recovering from trauma is tired and sexist. This raging cliché implies women must alter their appearance to demonstrate inner growth. It also promotes an unrealistic standard that serious personal struggle can be resolved with a simple haircut. Movies should showcase women overcoming hardship through tenacity of spirit, not salon skills. Retiring this superficial shortcut would demand thoughtful approaches. Seeing complex heroines heal through hard-fought courage and conviction, not magical makeovers, is something that should be displayed on film. Strong storytelling stems from actions and choices, not quick-fix beauty remedies.

5 The Perfect Enhancement Images Technology has its limitations

The cinematic cliché of impossibly zooming in on photos to reveal crucial details not only defies logic, but also underestimates the audience’s intelligence. Unlike the seamless image enhancement seen in some of the worst CSI tropes, real-world technology has inherent limitations. It serves as a narrative shortcut to navigate complex plot obstacles. Eliminating it requires writers to construct clever clues through nuanced plotting, relying on realistic investigative techniques instead of relying on impossible technological feats. It’s time for smart storytelling that respects the constraints of reality, challenging characters to uncover evidence through tenacity and skill.

10 Most Overused Sci-Fi Movie Tropes We’re All Tired Of Sci-fi movies use tropes to help ground their fantastical premises, but some of these genre conventions have been overused to the point of exhaustion.

4 The Medicine Cabinet Reflection An expected jump scare

Seeing a ghostly figure in a medicine cabinet reflection is an overused cliché in horror films that has come to be expected in these predictable scenes. A trope that has been done to death, as soon as the cabinet door opens, audiences know to expect a spooky figure peering back when it closes. Relying on this formula demonstrates a lack of creativity and unearned scares. Getting rid of the medicine cabinet as a go-to scare tactic would push the genre to explore fresh ideas that genuinely catch viewers off guard. Horror cinema deserves better than this formulaic fright.

3 Hacking Exaggerated hacking feeds misconceptions

Effortless and rapid hacking often serves as a convenient plot device, sidestepping narrative challenges and prioritizing theatrics over authentic skill portrayal. Depicting hackers creating intricate virtual schematics at implausible speeds is a clichéd fiction, divorced from reality. A more accurate representation of the coding craft necessitates diverse portrayals of hacking, showcasing real-world training and expertise developed over time. It is crucial to move away from the depiction of tech trickery and instead emphasize the meticulous mental tenacity that defines a proficient hacker. This shift would contribute to a more factual and insightful portrayal of hacking in movies.

2 Geniuses Who Know Everything Unrealistic portrayals of genuine intelligence

True brilliance is found in niche mastery, not boundless intellect. Genuine geniuses devote decades to a single discipline, be it pioneering medical discoveries or innovating mathematical theorems. However, movies often present protagonists as polymaths, excelling in obscure disease diagnosis, linguistics, and concert piano. This well-rounded portrayal surpasses even well-known geniuses like Einstein. Instead of depleting the genius label through implausible overuse, retiring this trope allows exploration of the genuine struggles faced by uniquely creative specialists. Compelling drama arises from managed weaknesses, not effortless excellence across every domain.

1 Deus Ex Machina Misses out on meaningful storytelling

The overused “deus ex machina” plot device, featuring an unexpected savior rescuing characters from calamity, has become a tired cliché. Relying on these contrived rescues instead of showcasing protagonists’ resourcefulness undermines meaningful storytelling. In Saving Private Ryan, an unseen battalion of tanks arriving in the emotional climax robs the squad of their hard-fought triumph, undercutting their resilience. Cop-out endings, where problems self-resolve, are unfulfilling. Retiring this lazy trope prompts writers to develop more ingenious plots for authentic resolutions. Earnest storytelling arises from characters leveraging strengths to overcome adversity, not sudden contrivances parachuting in for salvation.
