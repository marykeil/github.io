10 Most Impactful Final Lines In TV Shows Ever

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1600 words 8 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

Brilliant final lines in TV show finales create unforgettable endings that resonate with long-term fans. The final line carries emotional weight and can be a powerful message that reflects the show’s overall story. Context matters in crafting a memorable final line, as it should make sense within the show’s narrative.

Literature Review

Series finales give a TV show one last chance to create an impact, and a memorable last line can sum up an entire show in a single moment.

For a great TV show ending, the very last line is often the most important, since this is the message that the show leaves the audience with. A series finale gives a show a chance to wrap up all the story lines and create one final impression. Outstanding series finales can be among a show’s most memorable episodes, summing up multiple seasons at once, and the final line is a big part of this. Not all shows take this opportunity to deliver an iconic final sentence, but some of the most popular shows of all time save the best until last. Brilliant final lines still have to make sense in context. While they can often sound just as powerful on their own, they will be even more emotional for long-term fans of the show who have watched the characters for years. Whether it’s one last joke or a parting piece of wisdom, these lines often become famous. Final lines are loaded with meaning, and their position within the story of the entire show lends them a unique weight. Some shows prefer a low-key ending, but shows which put a lot of thought into the very last sentence create unforgettable finales.

10 TV Shows That Peaked With Their Final Season
Most TV shows run out of steam toward the end, but a select few manage to save the best for last, delivering masterpieces in their final seasons.

10 “Should We Get Some Coffee?” “Sure, Where?” Rachel and Chandler in Friends (1994-2004)

The Friends finale brought ten seasons of story lines to a close, with Ross and Rachel finally getting back together, and Chandler and Monica moving into a new home with their newborn twins. Although Ross and Rachel’s fiery on-again-off-again relationship drives most of the show’s plot, Friends’ strongest couple is Chandler and Monica. As the six friends take one last look at the apartment that has been the center of their social universe for so long, Rachel suggests they all go to get coffee. Chandler’s final quip shows that some things will never change, and they head to Central Perk.

9 “I Just Didn’t Like Seeing You All Adrift In There On Your Own.” Rob in Catastrophe (2015-2019)

Rob and Sharon’s relationship is never straightforward. They initially get together as nothing more than a one-night stand while Rob is visiting London for work, but Sharon’s surprise pregnancy brings them together, and they decide to try living together. Although they fall in love, their personalities frequently clash and create huge rifts. In the series finale, Sharon swims into the ocean, unaware of the dangerous riptides. Rather than warning her from the beach, Rob goes in after her. This act is symbolic of their love, in that they choose to live dangerously rather than to sit and watch.

8 “Driver, We’re Ready.” Johnny in Schitt’s Creek

The bittersweet irony of Schitt’s Creek’s finale is that the Roses have grown closer together, but their lives are leading them in different directions.

The series finale, “Happy Ending,” is one of Schitt’s Creek’s best episodes, giving the Rose family a sweet send-off after their journey from riches to rags and back again. After the wedding, and David’s sordid pre-wedding massage, Johnny and Moira bid farewell to the family. The bittersweet irony of Schitt’s Creek’s finale is that the Roses have grown closer together, but their lives are leading them in different directions. Johnny stops the driver on the way out of town to look back at the “Welcome to Schitt’s Creek” sign, pausing to consider how much the town has changed him.

7 “This Is Nice.” BoJack in BoJack Horseman

BoJack Horseman’s final season hinted that its protagonist could die, finally paying off the tease from the opening credits, but BoJack survives almost drowning in the pool. Instead, he lives to face the consequences of his actions. At Princess Carolyn’s wedding, BoJack has another chance to spend time with the people closest to him. Bojack Horseman’s finale ends with him and Diane sitting together on the rooftop, both knowing that this is probably the last time they will see each other. After a long history of emotional turmoil, they decide to just enjoy the moment.

6 “You’d Hate The Flowers. But I Brought The Dubonnet.” Gary in Veep

Selina Meyer trades everything for one last taste of power, not just her relationship with her daughter and her last shred of credibility, but her dutiful assistant, Gary. Selina’s betrayal of Gary underlines her ruthless hunger for power. She essentially sells her soul for four more divisive years in the White House. The real tragedy is that Gary retains a sense of love and loyalty for her after spending 20 years in prison. He places a hand on her coffin and brings her favorite lipstick. Gary dedicates his life to Selina, and he gets nothing but pain in return.

5 “We Do Have This Drone. It’d Be Cool To Get A Drone Shot, Maybe.” Nathan in Nathan For You

Maci offers Nathan a chance to form a meaningful bond which has nothing to do with his TV show, but Nathan ultimately reverts to the comfort of artifice.

Nathan For You mixes reality with entertainment, and the final episode heightens the divide more than ever. The boundary between the real Nathan Fielder and the awkward character seems to shift throughout “Finding Frances.” Nathan sees a grim portent of his future in William, a Bill Gates impersonator who yearns for a love he lost decades ago. Maci offers Nathan a chance to form a meaningful bond which has nothing to do with his TV show, but Nathan ultimately reverts to the comfort of artifice. He chooses a visually appealing ending over one that brings him joy.

4 “If You Can Find Someone To Love The You That You Love, Well, That’s Just Fabulous.” Carrie in Sex and the City

Sex and the City’s ending was controversial, placing each of the main four characters in long-term relationships with men. This somewhat contradicts the show’s emphasis on female empowerment and self-fulfillment, but Carrie’s final words highlight that committed relationships and self-love aren’t mutually exclusive. The best relationships are those in which people appreciate each other and build each other up. Carrie also notes “The most exciting, challenging, significant relationship of all is the one you have with yourself.” Carrie and her friends may have their fairy tale endings, but their partners are just one part of that.

3 “Who Would Have Noticed Another Madman Around Here? Good Luck, Everyone.” Blackadder in Blackadder

Blackadder moves the same few characters to a different historical setting each season. The fourth and final season is set in the trenches of the First World War. Gradually, the sitcom builds toward a tragic ending, with the British troops preparing to climb into No Man’s Land, knowing they are unlikely to survive. Baldrick comes up with one final cunning plan, but there’s no time to hear it. Blackadder jokes that it can’t have been worse than his plan to have himself declared mentally unfit for duty, but his final words are an uncharacteristically earnest message to his friends.

2 “There’s A Lot Of Beauty In Ordinary Things. Isn’t That Kind Of The Point?” Pam in The Office

Pam’s final interview in The Office perfectly sums up the show’s philosophy on everyday life. The mockumentary follows an uninteresting paper company, but the stories of the people who work there touch on all aspects of love and life. Pam is perceptive enough to see the beauty of everyone around her trying to forge a community in the workplace. Her final words accompany the image of her watercolor painting of the office, reflecting her own idealized vision of her time there. Even though she and Jim didn’t love every day, they are thankful for the experience.

1 “I’ll Say This To You, My Friend, With All The Love In My Heart & All The Wisdom Of The Universe: Take It Sleazy.” Michael in The Good Place

The Good Place is full of emotional moments, especially toward the end of the final season when the four friends in the afterlife begin to consider moving on for good. After years of being obsessed with the minutia of everyday human life, Michael is granted his wish to live on Earth. This makes him uniquely positioned to appreciate the beauty of unremarkable human moments people take for granted. When someone tells him to take it easy, it represents a minor dream come true. He takes inspiration from Eleanor and his near-eternity of experience with the universe’s inner workings to respond.
