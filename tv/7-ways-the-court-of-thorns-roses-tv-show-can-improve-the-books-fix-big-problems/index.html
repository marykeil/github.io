7 Ways The Court Of Thorns & Roses TV Show Can Improve The Books (& Fix Big Problems)

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 1400 words 7 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

ACOTAR ’s supporting characters need more screen time and development in the Hulu adaptation to ensure viewers get invested in them. The TV show should show critical story developments on-screen instead of relying on off-page journeys, allowing for a more immersive viewing experience. The series has the opportunity to explore and expand on the serious subjects and trauma presented in the books, resonating more deeply with viewers.

Literature Review

Hulu’s A Court of Thorns & Roses TV show can improve upon Sarah J. Maas’ book series by expanding on certain storylines, themes, and characters.

A Court of Thorns & Roses is among of the most popular books regularly discussed in the online book community, but Sarah J. Maas’ story has its flaws — and Hulu’s upcoming TV adaptation has the opportunity to improve on the source material by fixing them. In general, book adaptations fare better when they stick faithfully to the stories they’re based on, something Disney’s Percy Jackson show is proving 10 times over. However, it’s necessary to take some liberties when bringing a story from the page to the screen. In ACOTAR’s case, it might even benefit the narrative. ACOTAR is popular for a reason, and its Hulu adaptation will fill a gap in TV’s fantasy genre. Unfortunately, that may not be enough to get the series renewed multiple times, especially in a world where streaming shows are canceled constantly. Finding and fixing the weak points in Maas’ source material might help improve the story overall, increasing its chances of long-term success. There are several places Hulu’s A Court of Thorns & Roses could start when it comes to that endeavor.

7 Hulu’s ACOTAR Series Must Keep Its Characters Around More Than The Books The Audience Must Get To Know The Supporting Cast Art by Charlie Bowater

One of the most jarring elements of the ACOTAR books is that they send the characters off on random quests throughout the story, so the supporting players disappear for long periods of time. This is a terrible habit that Hulu’s A Court of Thorns & Roses must fix, as viewers won’t get invested in the side characters if they’re never on-screen. Lucien is the best example of this, as his character plays a major role in the first ACOTAR book, then appears sporadically in the sequels. However, Feyre’s father, her sisters, and the Night Court could use more permanent presences in the TV show’s narrative.

6 Hulu’s A Court Of Thorns & Roses Show Must Show Important Developments On-Screen Sarah J. Maas’ Books Often Feature Off-Page Journeys Image credit: Charlie Bowater

Sending characters away for entire chapters or even books doesn’t just make it impossible to get attached; it also leads to important developments happening off the page, with Maas telling readers about them rather than showing them. Hulu’s A Court of Thorns & Roses show must ensure that all critical story beats take place on-screen, even if it means diverging from Feyre and Rhysand’s perspectives. Given the success of fantasy ensembles like Game of Thrones, showing multiple POVs would likely work in the ACOTAR show’s favor.

5 Lucien Deserves More Development In The ACOTAR Show The ACOTAR Books Don’t Do Him Justice Image credit: Dominique Wesson 

Some characters don’t just need more screentime; they also need further development overall. This is especially true of Lucien, who becomes Feyre’s friend during her time living at the Spring Court. Lucien often serves as the voice of reason between Feyre and Tamlin, the latter of whom is both possessive and temperamental. However, Lucien sides with his friend over Feyre when she leaves — and the books never dig too deeply into his true feelings about her or Tamlin, opting instead to write him out of the narrative. When Lucien does return, he only gets brief moments of exploration, with the later books redeeming him slightly. Still, the Hulu show has the opportunity to expand on Lucien’s character. This would be a welcome change, as he was a quick fan-favorite when A Court of Thorns & Roses first hit shelves.

4 ACOTAR’s Characters Need More Nuanced Portrayals In The Show The Hulu Series Doesn’t Need To Be From Feyre’s Perspective Image credit: morgana0anagrom

While Tamlin is undoubtedly an abusive character, it feels like he becomes a completely different person between the first two ACOTAR books. From A Court of Mist & Fury onward, he’s portrayed as a full-blown villain. And while he qualifies as one, it would be nice to see Tamlin transition into this antagonist without stripping him of his complexity as a character. Tamlin’s not the only person in ACOTAR who doesn’t get a nuanced portrayal, either. Nesta is also consistently portrayed in a negative light despite clearly having a ton of trauma to contend with. It’s possible Maas’ cast isn’t nuanced because the books are told from Feyre’s perspective. However, the Hulu series doesn’t need to show its characters through Feyre’s eyes. As such, A Court of Thorns & Roses can give the people around her more depth, resulting in stronger heroes and villains.

3 The Hulu Series Needs To Dig Deeper Into The Books’ Serious Subjects ACOTAR Can Resonate With Viewers If It Handles Them Well Image credit: ACOTAR coloring book and Tamirobson

A Court of Thorns & Roses explores many difficult subjects, from domestic abuse to depression. Nearly every character in the series has trauma, but it isn’t always given the weight it requires. While Maas’ does a good job with certain serious topics — like Feyre healing from Tamlin’s abuse — she sometimes drops the ball on others. An ACOTAR TV show will have the time to flesh out these storylines further, approaching them with the care they require. Nesta’s depression and the Night Court’s trauma can resonate with viewers if they’re handled well.

2 A TV Show Could Expand Upon Sarah J. Maas’ World-Building Viewers Could See More Of Feyre’s World Image credit: Charlie Bowater

While Maas’ ACOTAR books have enough world-building to immerse readers in Feyre’s story, they don’t go into as much detail as seasoned fantasy fans are accustomed to. The series is very character-centric, which leaves many questions about the backdrop, an admittedly fascinating element of the books. A TV runtime will allow Hulu’s A Court of Thorns & Roses to elaborate on ACOTAR’s magic system and the world of the Fae. It will need to have top-tier world-building if it wants to compete with the other popular fantasy TV shows, so viewers may even get to see parts of Feyre’s world that aren’t in the books.

1 Hulu’s A Court Of Thorns & Roses Should Strengthen The Third Book’s Ending It Doesn’t Need To Peak With A Court Of Mist & Fury

While A Court of Thorns & Roses is an ongoing series, the original arc that centered Feyre and Rhysand reaches its climax in A Court of Wings & Ruin — and the book’s ending is underwhelming compared to A Court of Mist & Fury. While many trilogies suffer from middle-book syndrome, ACOTAR has the opposite problem. A Court of Mist & Fury is often regarded as the strongest novel in the series, with the other two remaining popular but falling short of its narrative. For this reason, Hulu’s A Court of Thorns & Roses will need at least two seasons to succeed, with Feyre and Rhysand’s story elevating the plot. It should also make an effort to make ACOWAR’s ending more gripping on-screen. Perhaps the adaptation can keep getting better rather than peaking with ACOMAF. That would help maintain the excitement surrounding the show and set the stage for the later books to be adapted as well.
