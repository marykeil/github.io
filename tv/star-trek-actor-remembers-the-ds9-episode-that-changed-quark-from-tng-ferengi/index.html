Star Trek Actor Remembers The DS9 Episode That Changed Quark from TNG Ferengi

 Ella
2020-10-16 About 700 words 4 minutes  - views   - comments  
Contents
    • SUMMARY
    • Literature Review

SUMMARY

Quark’s character development in Star Trek: Deep Space Nine showcased the complexity and depth of Ferengi characters, breaking away from their comedic portrayal in Star Trek: The Next Generation. The episode “Move Along Home” marked a turning point for Quark, as he displayed care and concern for others rather than solely focusing on profit and self-interest. Quark’s actions reflected his secret compassion, proving that he had a heart despite his adherence to Ferengi values and the pursuit of profit.

Literature Review

A playful Star Trek: DS9 episode gave Quark a chance to be more 3-dimensional than the one-note Ferengi on TNG, says Armin Shimerman.

Quark actor Armin Shimerman remembers the Star Trek: Deep Space Nine episode that made Quark different from the way Ferengi had been portrayed on Star Trek: The Next Generation. First seen in TNG season 1, episode 5, “The Last Outpost,” The Ferengi were initially intended to be a major enemy of the Federation that rivaled the Romulans in power. Lacking a truly threatening demeanor, the narrative function of the Ferengi shifted from legitimate adversaries to TNG’s comedic antagonists. They remained that way until DS9, when Shimerman’s own Quark showed that it was possible for Ferengi characters to be as complex as the other characters on Star Trek: Deep Space Nine.

On The Delta Flyers podcast episode reviewing DS9 season 1, episode 10, “Move Along Home”, Armin Shimerman acknowledges the deliberate acting choices that kickstarted Quark’s development into a more sympathetic character. In “Move Along Home”, members of Deep Space Nine’s senior staff become Quark’s player tokens in a Gamma Quadrant game called Chula. As the game progresses, Quark is forced to choose which token to sacrifice, so two others may continue. Delta Flyers co-host Robert Duncan McNeill praises Shimerman’s emotional approach to Quark’s dilemma, prompting Shimerman’s reflection on the scene. Read Shimerman’s quote below and listen to The Delta Flyers, starting at the 56:30 timestamp.

Armin Shimerman: “There are some episodes where I distinctly remember what choices I made. This was a very important moment to me. To me – not Quark – but for me. And why: because up until this point, for the most part, I’m still thinking a little like the Ferengi from TNG. I’m still thinking, oh, these are comic characters. … What I remember thinking is, I need to show them that this character has heart. And that he’s equally three-dimensional as any of the other characters on the show.”

Quark Does Have a Heart Quark will never admit it out loud, but he really does care.

While “Move Along Home” is one of DS9’s sillier episodes, it also features the first moments in DS9 when Quark displays care and concern for people other than himself. Even before the breakdown that Shimerman recalls in his quote above, Quark’s true character begins to emerge. When Quark first realizes that the game pieces brought by the alien Wadi really are DS9’s missing senior officers, Quark begins playing Chula more cautiously, and takes the path safer for Commander Benjamin Sisko (Avery Brooks), Major Kira Nerys (Nana Visitor), Lt. Jadzia Dax (Terry Farrell) and Dr. Julian Bashir (Alexander Siddig) instead of the route that will earn Quark more latinum.

Quark’s character continues to develop from there, as the Ferengi culture that shapes Quark’s worldview comes into conflict with other perspectives. Quark is a product of his environment: a Ferengi traditionalist who believes in adhering to the Rules of Acquisition, the objectification of women, and the pursuit of profit above all. Quark plays up traditional Ferengi values when it suits his bottom line, but at the end of the day, Quark really does care for other people, whether he likes it or not. Quark reveals his secret, inner compassion through actions more than words, reflecting the differences between TNG Ferengi and Quark in Star Trek: Deep Space Nine.
